# Culinary Arts Management 2 Curriculum System

West Ashley Culinary Program

This folder is the working curriculum system for Culinary Arts Management 2.

The course is not an advanced cooking class built around constant novelty. It is a deeper study of fundamentals through repetition, refinement, Bistro service, and composure under pressure.

North star:

Can this student be dropped into a basic kitchen and be useful without supervision?

Primary outcome:

Students move calmly, cleanly, and precisely under pressure.

## Current Build-Out

Culinary 2 is strong as a framework and pacing system. It is not yet as fully packaged as Culinary 1.

Use `Culinary-2-Curriculum/` as the working location for unit packets, lab sheets, teacher notes, student-facing materials, slides, line checks, and delivery-ready exports as they are created.

## Working Rule

Menu can flex. Process should stay consistent.

Use the pacing guide as a map, not a calendar. School events, testing, weather, guest speakers, field trips, early release, and Bistro needs will move things around. That is fine. The routines, expectations, and skill habits should not drift.

## Foodways and Place

This curriculum is technique-first and place-embedded.

For West Ashley Culinary, the West Ashley 25 gives local dish vehicles that can be dispersed across the year. Shrimp and grits, Hoppin' John, red rice, she-crab soup, fried fish, collards, benne wafers, biscuits, shrimp Alfredo, and other dishes are used where they fit the skill.

Do not turn this into a separate Lowcountry unit.

Do not turn this into a separate international foods unit.

The technique comes first. The dish gives the technique a place to live.

The list should honor both heritage foods and living foods. Student-requested foods such as shrimp Alfredo, macaroni and cheese, banana pudding, biscuits, and deviled eggs can be legitimate technique vehicles when they teach timing, sauce control, seasoning, holding quality, or service.

### Building a Local 25 Somewhere Else

Another instructor can build a Local 25 by looking at:

- geographic location
- local agriculture and seafood
- restaurant and hospitality culture
- family and community food traditions
- student-requested foods
- immigrant and regional foodways represented in the room
- affordable ingredients the school can actually source
- dishes that teach useful techniques

The list should not be a museum. It should be a practical set of recipe vehicles for knife work, rice/grains, sauces, proteins, frying, baking, service, and composure under pressure.

## Yearly Workflow

1. Review `09_Annual_Adjustment_Process.md` before the school year starts.
2. Mark the known interruptions: testing, field trips, early release, major school events, district dates, and Hospitality Scholars Tuesdays.
3. Choose anchor recipes for each module.
4. Keep Thursday as Bistro prep and Friday as Bistro service once Bistro begins.
5. Use the weekly guide to build projected agendas.
6. After each quarter, revise the pacing notes based on what students actually needed.

## Audit of the Provided Framework

The original framework is clear and useful. These are the places that needed tightening:

| Area | Issue | Curriculum Decision |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Module overlap | Module 1 is listed as Weeks 1-4 and Module 2 as Weeks 4-5. Module 3 starts Week 6, while Module 4 starts Week 10. | Treat Weeks 4-5 as Bistro systems weeks layered over baseline skill review. Treat Weeks 10-12 as overlap where proteins continue while sauces and moist heat begin. |
| Bistro timing | Bistro begins around Week 4, but students need training before live service expectations rise. | Week 4 starts controlled Bistro systems. Weeks 4-5 are training and mock-service heavy. Friday service can be limited or soft-launch during those weeks. |
| CKC timing | CKC should happen early Semester 1 around Week 9, but the framework does not place it. | CKC is embedded Weeks 1-9 and scheduled as a practical credential push around Week 9 if the class is ready. |
| Hospitality Scholars | Last Tuesday of each month can interrupt the weekly skill sequence. | Treat that Tuesday as a professional readiness day. Shift the Tuesday kitchen task to Monday or Wednesday when needed. |
| Assessment | The framework rejects worksheet-heavy assessment but needs a usable system. | Use short practical rubrics, station checks, line checks, teacher observation, and service evidence. Written checks stay brief and kitchen-connected. |
| Recipes | The framework says recipes are vehicles, but recipe selection needs guardrails. | Recipes must serve a skill target, fit the time block, allow repetition, and support group work. They are not the curriculum. |

## Maintenance Notes

Do not rebuild the whole curriculum each year. Update:

- known calendar interruptions
- Bistro menu anchors
- anchor recipes that worked or failed
- CKC timing
- line check cards that students struggled with
- assessment language that needs to be clearer

Keep the core habits protected:

- recipe reading and following
- knife control
- mise en place
- clean-as-you-go
- purposeful movement
- awareness and scanning
- tasting and seasoning
- communication
- follow-through
- composure under pressure
