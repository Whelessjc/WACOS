# Mise en Place Lab Teacher Guide

## Setup Before Class
- Choose the day application: peanut butter bars, rice cookery, fried rice, or simple stir fry.
- Confirm allergy plan, especially for peanut butter bar work.
- Prepare recipe copies or display the recipe clearly.
- Stage ingredients so students can practice shared ingredient stewardship without crowding.
- Stage measuring tools, bowls, pans, pots, rice cookers, saute pans or woks, spatulas, spoons, towels, sanitizer, and dish supplies as needed.
- Set the dish-flow, towel, trash, floor, and reset expectations before students begin.
- Decide whether heat work is student-managed, teacher-approved, or teacher-led.

## Demo Points
- Show how to read the recipe before starting.
- Model marking ingredients, equipment, yield, and important steps.
- Show how to turn recipe steps into a short prep list.
- Demonstrate careful measuring with one dry and one liquid example if needed.
- Show what a ready mise en place station looks like.
- Show what full reset looks like before students begin.

## Safety Watch-Outs
- Peanut allergies and cross-contact.
- Students measuring over shared ingredient containers.
- Students crowding ingredient pickup or hot stations.
- Hot pans, steam, rice cookers, and splatter during stir fry.
- Pan handles turned outward.
- Students thinking the lab is finished when the food is finished.

## Recipe Reading Checkpoints
- Students have read the full recipe.
- Ingredients, equipment, yield, and steps are identified.
- Group can explain what happens first.
- Students know where safety or timing issues may happen.
- Questions are asked before ingredients are handled.

## Measuring Checkpoints
- Students use the correct measuring tool.
- Dry ingredients are leveled when needed.
- Liquids are checked at eye level when appropriate.
- Ingredients are measured over a separate bowl when possible.
- Shared ingredients stay clean and are closed or returned.

## Mise en Place Checkpoints
- Recipe is visible.
- Team roles are assigned.
- Tools and ingredients match the recipe.
- Prep list is simple and useful.
- Towel, dish, trash, and cleaning plan are clear.
- Station is organized enough to begin.

## Lab Application Notes
- Peanut butter bars: emphasize allergen awareness, measuring, sequence, shared tools, and dish control.
- Rice cookery: emphasize ratio, timing, steam safety, and waiting without opening equipment repeatedly.
- Fried rice or stir fry: emphasize prep before heat, hot pan awareness, batch work, and order of operations.

## Common Student Mistakes
- Starting before reading the whole recipe.
- Gathering food but not tools.
- Ignoring yield, timing, or cooling directions.
- Measuring too quickly or using the wrong tool.
- Adding ingredients out of order.
- Letting one student do all the work.
- Leaving dishes, counters, towels, trash, or floor until the end.

## Cleanup Timing
- Pause halfway through the lab if stations are getting messy.
- Start dishes before final minutes.
- Have students store or return shared ingredients as soon as they are finished.
- Give a clear final reset deadline.
- Inspect stations before dismissal.
- Require groups to fix incomplete reset immediately.

## What to Look For While Circulating
- Are students reading or guessing?
- Is the recipe visible?
- Is mise en place visible before cooking?
- Are ingredients and tools being handled with care?
- Is measuring slow enough to be accurate?
- Are students communicating clearly?
- Is cleanup shared by the whole group?
- Is the final station ready for the next group?

## Bistro Connection
Name the link out loud: prep lists, timing, station setup, shared tools, and reset are the same habits students need before Bistro service. Service gets smoother when students organize before pressure starts.

## Quick Debrief Prompts
- What did your group know before you began?
- What did your prep list help with?
- What did your group need to measure more carefully?
- How did your group keep the station usable while cooking?
- What should we practice again before the next lab?
