# Teacher Notes: Orientation and Systems

## Main Focus
Learn the room before cooking.

Week 1 should be practical and calm. Students are entering a working kitchen environment, so the first job is to teach systems: how to enter, dress, move, wash hands, communicate, measure, set up, clean, and reset.

## Course Message
Culinary Arts Management 1 is about real cooking and real skills. Students are not expected to act like chefs on day one. They are expected to learn routines, stay safe, support the team, and take responsibility for shared spaces.

## Keep Repeating
- Systems before speed.
- Cooking includes cleaning.
- Read before acting.
- Organize before cooking.
- Safety is everyone's job.
- Reset means ready for the next person.
- Ask early instead of guessing.

## Uniform Expectations
Explain uniforms as a safety, sanitation, and professionalism system. Keep the first-week explanation clear and practical.

Students should understand:
- Uniforms help keep food and students safer.
- Hair restraint expectations matter.
- Closed-toe or approved shoes matter.
- Loose items, jewelry, and dangling accessories can become safety issues.
- Being dressed correctly is part of being ready to work.

Use the school's actual uniform policy when teaching this section.

## Daily Entry Routine
Teach this as a repeated system, not a one-time direction.

Suggested routine:
1. Enter calmly.
2. Put belongings in the assigned place.
3. Check uniform/readiness.
4. Go to the assigned starting area.
5. Listen for the day's kitchen status.
6. Wash hands when directed.
7. Begin only when released to work.

## Kitchen Communication
Students need simple, repeatable language.

Practice:
- "Behind"
- "Hot"
- "Sharp"
- "Corner"
- "Excuse me"
- "I need help"
- "Ready?"
- "Heard"

Do not assume students know how to communicate in a crowded workspace. Practice it out loud before labs.

## Handwashing Expectations
Model handwashing early and revisit it before Friday's lab.

Students should wash hands:
- Before handling food
- After touching face, hair, phone, trash, floor, or dirty dishes
- After using the restroom
- After coughing, sneezing, or blowing nose
- After handling raw or contaminated items
- Whenever the teacher directs them to reset

## Basic Safety Overview
Keep this overview short and usable. Deeper safety work continues in later units.

First-week safety priorities:
- Walk in the kitchen.
- Keep pathways clear.
- Report spills immediately.
- Do not use equipment without permission.
- Treat hot equipment as hot.
- Keep towels away from heat.
- Use tools for their intended purpose.
- Listen when the teacher stops the room.

## Measuring Stations
Use measuring stations to prepare for pancakes and build confidence.

Include practice with:
- Dry measuring cups
- Liquid measuring cups
- Measuring spoons
- Leveling dry ingredients
- Reading liquid measurements at eye level
- Matching tool to ingredient

Keep the practice low-pressure. Accuracy matters, but the bigger goal is careful habits.

## Station Setup
Students should see what "ready" looks like before they cook.

A ready station has:
- Clean hands
- Clear workspace
- Correct tools
- Ingredients gathered or staged
- Towel where expected
- Trash plan
- Sanitizer access
- Recipe or directions visible
- Team roles understood

## Cleanup Expectations
Teach cleanup before the first lab, not after students are already tired.

Students need to know:
- Where dirty dishes go
- How dish flow works
- How to wipe and sanitize counters
- Where towels go
- How to return tools
- How to check the floor
- What a reset station looks like

## Friday Pancake Lab Notes
The pancake lab is a systems lab. Pancake quality matters less than student behavior, setup, safety, and cleanup.

Primary teaching points:
- Recipe reading
- Basic measuring
- Mise en place
- Station setup
- Teamwork
- Heat awareness
- Cleanup routines

Use a simple teacher-selected pancake recipe or mix. Avoid adding extra complexity.

## What to Watch For During Pancake Lab
- Students skipping the recipe and guessing
- Students using the wrong measuring tool
- Crowding at ingredients or heat sources
- Students turning on equipment before approval
- Towels too close to heat
- One student doing all the cleanup
- Groups thinking they are done when the food is done

## Debrief Questions
- What helped your group stay organized?
- What did your group do safely?
- What routine did your group forget or rush?
- What should we practice again next week?

## Bridge to Week 2
Week 2 moves into Food Handler and Stewardship. Use Week 1 to identify which systems need reteaching: handwashing, towels, dishes, counters, sanitizer, storage, or reset.
