# Culinary 1 Foodways and Recipe Vehicles

## Purpose
This companion document identifies dishes, foods, ingredients, and kitchen experiences that naturally support the existing Culinary Arts Management 1 curriculum.

It does not redesign the curriculum. It does not create new units. It simply offers possible food vehicles for the skills already present in the pacing guide.

The goal is not authenticity. The goal is not exposure to every cuisine. The goal is to connect foundational culinary skills to foods that matter to students, their community, and the working kitchen.

Use these ideas as flexible teaching vehicles. Choose foods based on budget, equipment, allergies, student needs, school rules, available ingredients, and the skill being taught.

## How To Use This Document
- Start with the skill focus, not the dish.
- Choose one vehicle that helps students practice the target skill.
- Keep recipes simple enough for Culinary 1.
- Use placeholders until exact recipes, yields, and quantities are approved.
- Respect cultural origins without claiming authenticity.
- Keep safety, sanitation, stewardship, and reset visible in every lab.

## Week 1: Orientation and Systems
**Skill Focus:** Learn the room before cooking. Practice entry routine, uniforms, huddle, handwashing, measuring stations, station setup, heat awareness, and cleanup/reset.

### Possible Lowcountry Vehicles
**Hoe cakes or cornmeal pancakes**

Why it fits: This keeps the existing pancake lab structure while connecting to a familiar regional cornmeal tradition. Students can practice measuring, mixing, heat awareness, and griddle/pan cleanup without adding too much complexity.

### Possible Southern Vehicles
**Basic pancakes or biscuit-style drop batter**

Why it fits: Pancakes are already the primary lab. A simple batter reinforces measuring, reading directions, team roles, and station reset.

### Possible Student-Favorite Vehicles
**Pancake lab with simple topping station**

Why it fits: A topping station can make the lab feel student-friendly while still teaching setup, portion control, sanitation, and reset. Keep toppings simple and teacher-controlled.

### Possible International Vehicles
**Crepe-style batter demonstration**

Why it fits: A thin batter comparison can show how measurement and pan heat change results. This should be a simple demonstration or comparison, not a full new unit.

## Week 2: Food Handler and Stewardship
**Skill Focus:** Safety becomes behavior. Practice personal hygiene, cleaning and sanitation, cross contamination, allergens, dish systems, storage, floors, thermometer use, and stewardship jobs.

### Possible Lowcountry Vehicles
**Chicken bog temperature check**

Why it fits: Chicken bog connects to Lowcountry foodways while supporting thermometer use, safe poultry handling, and time/temperature control. Use as a temperature-check vehicle, not a full recipe unless approved.

### Possible Southern Vehicles
**Baked chicken temperature lab**

Why it fits: Baked chicken gives students a clear reason to use a thermometer and clean probes. It connects doneness to safety instead of guessing by color.

### Possible Student-Favorite Vehicles
**Brownie batter and baked brownies**

Why it fits: Brownies are already in the pacing. They are useful for teaching handwashing, shared tools, sticky counter cleanup, oven safety, storage, and dish flow.

### Possible International Vehicles
**Chicken rice bowl temperature check**

Why it fits: A simple rice bowl setup can connect cooked poultry safety, holding, storage, and allergen awareness. Keep the focus on food safety, not cuisine coverage.

## Week 3: Recipe Reading and Mise en Place

### Possible Lowcountry Vehicles
**Red rice**

### Possible Southern Vehicles
**Pimento cheese**

Why it fits: Pimento cheese is useful for reading ingredient lists, measuring, mixing, portioning, allergen awareness, and cold-station organization. It can be scaled for tasting or service prep.

### Possible Student-Favorite Vehicles
**Fried rice**

Why it fits: Fried rice is already in the pacing as an option. It teaches mise en place because the cooking moves quickly once the pan is hot.

### Possible International Vehicles
**Vegetable stir fry**

Why it fits: Stir fry reinforces prep lists, sequencing, cut size, heat readiness, teamwork, and clean-as-you-go habits. Use respectful language and keep the goal on skill practice.

## Teacher Notes For Selection
- Choose the vehicle that makes the skill easier to teach, not the one that sounds most impressive.
- A simple vehicle taught well is better than a complicated dish that causes unsafe habits.
- Use local foods as bridges to skill, memory, community, and pride.
- Use student favorites as bridges to engagement and repetition.
- Use international vehicles as respectful comparisons, not as claims of authenticity.
- Keep cleanup and reset equal to cooking in importance.
- Keep Bistro/service connections practical: prep lists, timing, communication, consistency, guest safety, dish flow, and reset.
