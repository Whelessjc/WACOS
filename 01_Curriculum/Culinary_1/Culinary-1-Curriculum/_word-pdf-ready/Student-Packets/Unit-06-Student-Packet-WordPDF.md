## What We Are Learning
Over the next four weeks, we are building knife control through repeated practice. You will practice safety, board
setup, grip, claw, rough chop, dice, slice, mince, julienne, batonnet, prep lists, cooking applications, and deep clean
reset.

## Why Knife Control Matters
Knife work affects safety, cooking, timing, and teamwork. Food cooks more evenly when pieces are cut with care. Cut
size can change cooking time, texture, and browning. Bistro prep works better when containers are organized, labeled,
and ready.

Knife cuts are tools for cooking, not trophies.

## Minimum Competence
For Culinary 1, minimum competence means:
- I can set up a safe cutting station.
- I can use grip and claw.
- I can keep the blade path controlled.
- I can cut at a controlled pace.
- I can make beginner cuts that are useful for cooking.
- I can sort usable prep, scraps, and waste.
- I can clean and reset the station safely.

## What Success Looks Like
- I can keep my board stable.
- I can keep my guiding hand in claw position.
- I can keep my knife controlled.
- I can improve speed without losing safety.
- I can use a prep list.
- I can explain how cut size affects cooking.
- I can reset a knife station for the next group.

## Knife Safety Expectations
- Carry knives safely as directed.
- Keep knives down when the teacher is talking.
- Keep your board stable.
- Keep fingers tucked.
- Keep eyes on the cut.
- Stop if the blade path feels unsafe.
- Do not rush.
- Do not scrape with the knife edge.
- Clean and store knives as directed.

## Board Setup
- Damp towel under board
- Board stable
- Knife placed safely
- Towel in the correct place
- Scrap/waste plan clear
- Usable prep container ready
- Counter clean enough to work

## Grip and Claw
Grip:
- Hold the knife with control.
- Keep the knife steady.
- Do not hold the handle loosely.
- Ask for help if the knife feels unstable.

Claw:
- Tuck fingertips back.
- Use knuckles as a guide.
- Keep thumb behind fingers.
- Move slowly before trying to move faster.

## Cuts We Will Practice
Rough chop:
- Larger, practical pieces.
- Useful for some prep and cooking tasks.

Dice:
- Smaller pieces.
- Useful when food needs to cook evenly.

Slice:
- Even pieces or strips.
- Useful for vegetables, sandwiches, stir fry, and saute.

Mince:
- Very small pieces.
- Useful for aromatics like garlic, herbs, or onions.

Julienne:
- Thin matchstick-style cuts.
- Useful for quick cooking and garnish-style prep.

Batonnet:
- Larger stick-style cuts.
- Useful for potato prep, fries, and consistent roasting.

## Speed With Control
Speed is not the first goal. Control comes first.

You may increase speed only when:
- Board is stable
- Grip and claw are correct
- Blade path is controlled
- Product is organized
- Station is clean enough to work

## How Knife Work Connects to Cooking
Cut size affects:
- How fast food cooks
- Whether food cooks evenly
- Texture
- Browning
- Service timing
- How easy a station is to work

In Bistro, better knife work helps prep lists, batch containers, labels, storage, station timing, and service readiness.
