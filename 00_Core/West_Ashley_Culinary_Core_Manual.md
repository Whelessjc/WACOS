## Preface

The West Ashley Culinary Core Manual defines how the West Ashley High School Culinary Arts Program operates at the level of identity, principles, governance, and instructional direction.

This manual is not a student handbook, a recipe collection, a safety binder, a pacing guide, or a set of station SOPs. Those documents are subordinate to this manual. They may change as calendars, menus, equipment, staffing, and student needs change. This manual defines the program logic those documents must serve.

The program is built around authentic culinary and hospitality work. Students learn by entering a real kitchen culture, practicing repeatedly, accepting responsibility, serving guests, recovering from mistakes, and developing habits that transfer beyond school.

The Core Manual should be used when creating or revising lesson plans, Bistro systems, grading tools, AI assistants, curriculum maps, SOPs, public-facing materials, certification plans, and administrative documents. When a future document conflicts with this manual, the document should be revised or the Core Manual should be formally updated.

## Program Identity

West Ashley Culinary is a working kitchen inside a high school.

The program is not built around culinary spectacle, isolated recipe completion, or the appearance of being "advanced." It is built around practice, service, teamwork, responsibility, repetition, and professional habits.

The program identity is expressed through two governing phrases:

**Classroom to Table** means that learning becomes visible through preparation, service, hospitality, and shared work. Students do not only study culinary arts; they practice culinary work in a real instructional kitchen.

**High School, High Standards** means that the program honors the reality that students are teenagers while still maintaining professional expectations. The standard is not lowered because the setting is a school. The standard is taught, practiced, reinforced, and assessed because the setting is a school.

### Public Identity

The public identity of the program should remain clear and stable:

- West Ashley Culinary.
- Classroom to Table.
- High School, High Standards.
- Kitchen, classroom, and Bistro as one instructional ecosystem.
- Hands-on culinary arts and hospitality education.
- Real service, real responsibility, and real professional habits.

Public materials should avoid overpromising. The strongest claim the program can make is also the most practical: students learn to become useful, safe, reliable, reflective contributors in a working culinary environment.

## Mission and Vision

### Mission

West Ashley Culinary prepares students to work safely, think practically, cook with growing skill, serve with hospitality, communicate clearly, and contribute responsibly to a real kitchen and service environment.

The program gives students durable culinary, employability, and life skills through repeated practice, professional standards, and authentic Bistro operations.

### Vision

West Ashley Culinary will be a serious high school culinary and hospitality pathway where students develop confidence, discipline, craft, and responsibility through the daily work of running a student-centered kitchen.

The program should be known for students who are prepared, clean, organized, useful, coachable, and capable of serving others well.

### Program Outcome

The central question for the program is:

Can this student safely and professionally contribute to a kitchen, service team, or hospitality workplace with appropriate supervision?

The program does not promise that every student will become a chef. It does intend that every student leave with skills, standards, and habits that matter.

## Educational Philosophy

Culinary education is technical and behavioral.

The [Professional Thinking Framework](Professional_Thinking_Framework.md) states the program's governing instructional philosophy: **We are not training chefs. We are training students to think like chefs.** The kitchen is the classroom, culinary is the vehicle, and professional thinking is the destination. Students may or may not enter culinary careers, but every student should leave more prepared, organized, responsible, reflective, and capable.

Students must learn knife safety, sanitation, equipment use, recipes, cooking methods, kitchen math, tasting, cleaning, and service. They must also learn punctuality, attention, follow-through, communication, emotional control, teamwork, and responsibility.

Recipes are not the curriculum. Recipes are vehicles for skill practice. A pancake lab, a fried rice lab, a soup, a salad, a Bistro special, or a service station is valuable because it reveals whether students can prepare, execute, communicate, clean, reflect, and improve.

### Depth Before Breadth

The program favors depth over breadth. Students do not need a scattered tour of every possible culinary topic. They need repeated contact with the fundamentals until those fundamentals become dependable.

Culinary 2 is not simply an advanced class. It is a deeper dive into fundamentals under pressure.

This distinction protects the program from drift. "Advanced" must not mean harder recipes, more obscure ingredients, more guest speakers, or more disconnected projects. Higher-level work means stronger fundamentals, better pace, cleaner stations, better judgment, more responsibility, and greater consistency during real production.

### Authentic Work

Authentic work creates authentic learning. The Bistro gives students a real audience, real timing, real interdependence, real guest recovery, and real consequences.

The point of authenticity is not sales volume. Learning and safety remain more important than revenue. The value of the Bistro is that it makes the standards visible.

### Teenagers First

Students are teenagers before they are culinary students. They bring uneven maturity, sleep, confidence, energy, motivation, background knowledge, and emotional readiness.

The program should not pretend students are college culinary students or paid employees. It should also not excuse poor habits as inevitable. The work of the program is to teach the standard explicitly, create routines that make success more likely, and hold students accountable in ways that are clear, fair, and practical.

## Enduring Principles

Enduring principles are expected to remain true across calendars, menus, staffing changes, and future curriculum revisions.

### Authentic Work Creates Authentic Learning

Students learn differently when their work matters to guests, classmates, instructors, and the room itself. The Bistro is therefore not a side project. It is the central applied-learning environment of the program.

### Responsibility Is Taught Through Systems

Students become responsible when expectations are visible, repeated, practiced, and assessed. Responsibility cannot depend on memory, personality, or adult rescue. The program must use checklists, station roles, prep lists, service routines, closing standards, and reflection to make responsibility concrete.

### Fundamentals Are the Highest Standard

Knife safety, sanitation, mise en place, recipe reading, measuring, cooking methods, tasting, seasoning, communication, and cleaning are not beginner topics to rush through. They are the craft.

### Professional Habits Matter as Much as Technical Skill

Talent does not cancel out poor safety, poor communication, unreliable work habits, or disrespect for the room. A student who can cook but cannot work cleanly, communicate clearly, or reset the station is not meeting the program standard.

Meaningful learning should connect three levels when authentic opportunities exist: the knowledge students are learning, the skill they are practicing, and the professional-thinking habit they are strengthening. New and substantively revised units, Daily Teaching Guides, assessments, instructor guidance, and AI prompts should make these connections explicit.

### Hospitality Is a Mindset

Hospitality is not limited to front-of-house roles. It includes how students speak to each other, how they handle mistakes, how they protect guests, how they reset the room, and how they leave the next group ready to succeed.

### Repetition Builds Confidence

Confidence comes from repeated practice under clear standards. Novelty has a place, but repetition builds cooks.

### Clean Is a Skill

Clean-as-you-go is not a slogan. Students must learn to notice full trash, empty sanitizer, wet floors, missing labels, unstocked tools, dirty handles, uncovered food, loose towels, and unreset stations.

### Leadership Emerges Through Competence

Leadership is not a title first. It is visible through preparation, awareness, calm communication, service to the team, and the ability to make others better without taking over.

### Instructor Judgment Remains Central

Tools, AI assistants, rubrics, checklists, and student leaders may support the program. They do not replace instructor judgment, especially in safety, grading, discipline, guest recovery, and program direction.

## Curriculum Framework

The curriculum is organized as a pathway of foundation, depth, and application.

### Culinary 1

Culinary 1 is a one-semester foundation and exploration course.

Its purpose is to give every student useful culinary, safety, service, and employability skills whether or not the student continues in the pathway.

Culinary 1 should prepare students to:

- Understand basic food safety and sanitation.
- Work toward the ServSafe Food Handler credential.
- Use foundational kitchen vocabulary and communication.
- Set up, work, and reset a station.
- Read recipes and measure accurately.
- Practice kitchen math.
- Use knives safely.
- Cook simple food with increasing confidence.
- Apply flavor balance through vinaigrette and a teacher-approved, same-day refrigerated quick pickle; this does not include canning or fermentation.
- Practice both animal-protein and tofu applications with appropriate food-safety and allergen controls.
- Understand dish, laundry, storage, labeling, and cleaning systems.
- Participate appropriately in Bistro roles after readiness has been established.
- Decide whether Culinary 2 is the right next step.

Culinary 1 should not try to become restaurant school. It should produce durable beginner competence and enough authentic exposure for students to understand the work.

### Culinary 2

Culinary 2 is a full-year depth and operations course.

Its governing phrase is fundamentals under pressure.

Culinary 2 should not be framed as "advanced" if that word implies novelty, loosened standards, or impressive-looking food without better execution. Culinary 2 students should demonstrate higher standards in uniform, punctuality, sanitation, station readiness, communication, cooking, service, and closing.

Culinary 2 should prepare students to:

- Reconfirm Culinary 1 fundamentals.
- Strengthen knife work, cooking methods, seasoning, tasting, and product awareness.
- Use gentle poaching explicitly and apply broiler/grill methods only after equipment-specific safety instruction, SOP review, and individual authorization.
- Execute Bistro prep and service with greater independence.
- Rotate through front-of-house, back-of-house, support, and leadership roles.
- Practice menu math, costing, inventory awareness, and production planning where appropriate.
- Work toward Certified Kitchen Cook alignment.
- Mentor newer or less confident students without doing the work for them.
- Build a portfolio of practical skill, reflection, and service evidence.

### Hospitality Work-Based Learning

Hospitality Work-Based Learning is the application stage after or alongside advanced pathway work.

It may include on-campus or off-campus application of culinary and hospitality skills. It should remain connected to the same standards: safety, professionalism, communication, hospitality, responsibility, and reflection.

### The Bistro Across the Pathway

The Bistro connects the pathway. It gives Culinary 1 students exposure and role practice after readiness is established. It gives Culinary 2 students depth, pace, leadership, and production responsibility. It gives work-based learning students a place to apply and extend their skills.

The Bistro should shape curriculum decisions, but it should not consume the entire curriculum. Monday through Wednesday learning time should be protected whenever possible so students continue building the skills that make Thursday prep and Friday service successful.

## Professional Standards

Students are expected to be ready, clean, organized, safe, useful, and coachable.

These standards apply in classroom work, labs, Bistro prep, Bistro service, cleanup, written work, leadership roles, AI-assisted work, and public-facing program activity.

### Readiness

Students arrive on time, in uniform, with required materials, and ready to work. Readiness includes mental readiness: attention, posture, willingness to listen, and willingness to begin.

### Safety

Safety governs all work. Students follow knife, heat, equipment, chemical, movement, allergen, and emergency procedures. No production goal justifies unsafe work.

### Sanitation

Students wash hands, prevent contamination, use sanitizer correctly, label and store food safely, protect TCS foods, and understand the difference between cleaning and sanitizing.

### Mise en Place

Students set the station before work begins. Tools, product, recipes, timelines, labels, and quality targets should be visible enough that students can work without constant adult rescue.

### Communication

Students use professional kitchen language and clear service communication. They practice calls such as behind, corner, hot, sharp, heard, and walking in. They speak with purpose, listen for confirmation, and report problems early.

### Awareness

Students are expected to see work. Empty sanitizer, missing labels, full trash, low supplies, unstocked spoons, dirty stations, unsafe movement, and unfinished closing tasks are not invisible.

### Teamwork

Students complete assigned work and support the team. Helping is not the same as taking over. Accountability is shared, but responsibility is still specific.

### Hospitality

Students treat guests, classmates, staff, equipment, ingredients, and the room with respect.

### Quality

Students follow recipes, taste thoughtfully, check doneness, season with intention, plate appropriately, and correct errors when possible.

### Reflection

Students identify what worked, what failed, and what should change next time. Reflection should support improvement, not become paperwork for its own sake.

### Non-Negotiables

The following are program-level non-negotiables:

- No unauthorized equipment use.
- No unsafe knife handling.
- No food service without required sanitation controls.
- No unlabeled stored food.
- No wet towels as pot holders.
- No leaving stations for others to reset without approval.
- No AI output used as operational fact without instructor review.

## Kitchen Culture

Kitchen culture is the daily expression of the program's standards.

The room should teach students how to enter calmly, observe what needs doing, communicate clearly, prepare carefully, work cleanly, recover from mistakes, and leave the kitchen better than they found it.

### Calm Urgency

The program values pace without panic. Students should learn to move with purpose, not chaos. Speed without safety, cleanliness, or communication is not professional.

### Respect for the Room

Respect for the room includes equipment, ingredients, tools, floors, dish systems, storage areas, laundry, trash, and the next class. A clean reset is a professional obligation.

### Mistakes and Recovery

Mistakes are expected in learning kitchens. Hiding mistakes, ignoring safety, blaming others, or refusing correction is not acceptable. Students should learn to report problems, receive coaching, repair what can be repaired, and improve the next attempt.

### Speaking and Articulation

Students need practice speaking about their work. The program should provide regular opportunities for students to explain what they are doing, name a problem, brief a station, report a service issue, describe a quality standard, or reflect on a result.

Speaking is part of leadership because leadership requires articulation.

### Guest Recovery

Guest issues should be handled calmly using the HEARD method: Hear the concern without interrupting, Empathize with the guest's experience, Apologize genuinely even if the issue wasn't the student's fault, Resolve the issue quickly, and Diagnose the root cause so it doesn't happen again. The deeper standard is consistent: students should respond with respect, communicate the issue, and involve the instructor when needed.

## Bistro Operating Philosophy

The Bistro is the central instructional model of West Ashley Culinary.

It is a student-led restaurant and service experience inside the school. It is also a classroom, a lab, a leadership environment, an assessment setting, and a public expression of the program.

### Purpose

The Bistro exists to help students apply culinary and hospitality skills through preparation, service, teamwork, communication, guest interaction, and closing systems.

The Bistro also supports program needs such as uniforms, food, equipment, tools, class library, field trips, competitions, and related opportunities. Revenue supports the program, but the Bistro is not primarily a revenue machine. Learning, safety, and standards come first.

### Weekly Rhythm

The current operating rhythm is:

- Monday through Wednesday: new learning, technique, theory, skill labs, and prep foundations.
- Thursday: Bistro prep, production planning, station setup, labels, storage, and readiness.
- Friday: Bistro service, guest interaction, closing, reflection, and assessment.

This rhythm is an operational decision, not an enduring principle. The enduring principle is that authentic service must be prepared for, executed, closed, and reflected on with professional standards.

### Readiness Before Launch

The Bistro should open only after students have practiced the systems needed to operate safely and usefully.

Readiness includes:

- Safety and sanitation.
- Dish systems.
- Triple sink and dish machine basics.
- Storage, labeling, dating, and FIFO.
- Menu knowledge.
- Stocking and smallwares awareness.
- Station roles.
- Front-of-house basics.
- Back-of-house basics.
- Guest flow.
- Life of an order.
- Mock service.
- Closing and reset.

The program uses a readiness-based launch model. For 2026-2027, Week 4 (August 31-September 4) begins Bistro systems, mock service, and optional tightly controlled soft-opening work. Because Friday, September 4 is an early-release day, the first normal public Bistro service is Friday, September 11 in Week 5. The instructor may reduce, postpone, or pause service when safety or readiness requires it.

### Life of an Order

Students should understand service as a connected system:

1. Guests check in or enter the service flow.
2. Guests are seated or assigned a service point.
3. Orders are taken or called.
4. The order is fired in the kitchen.
5. Kitchen, expo, and front-of-house coordinate timing and handoff.
6. Guest issues are handled calmly and escalated when appropriate.
7. Tables, dishes, stations, storage, and equipment are reset.

### Interdependence

One missed role can break the restaurant. A student who fails to stock spoons, label food, run dish, check sanitizer, communicate a shortage, or close a station affects everyone else.

The Bistro should teach this directly. Students are not only completing individual tasks; they are protecting flow.

### Role Rotation

Students should rotate through back-of-house, front-of-house, support, and leadership roles. Rotation helps students understand the whole operation and prevents them from defining culinary skill too narrowly.

Common roles include prep, hot line, cold station, baking, dish, stewarding, expeditor, host, order support, runner, busser, beverage, customer service, stocking, labels, laundry, trash, sanitizer, Bistro manager, station lead, sanitation lead, and inventory assistant.

### Bistro Documentation

Bistro operations require subordinate documents:

- Menus.
- Prep lists.
- Station assignments.
- Production timelines.
- Recipes and production packets.
- Front-of-house flow.
- Closing checklists.
- Live grading tools.
- Reflection tools.
- Inventory and ordering notes.
- Guest recovery procedures.

These documents should be simple, visible, current, and aligned with this manual.

## Assessment Philosophy

Assessment should reflect how culinary learning actually happens: repeated practice, visible habits, practical performance, service pressure, cleanup, and reflection.

Written tests have a place, especially for safety, sanitation, vocabulary, kitchen math, and credentials. The core of the program, however, is performance.

### Current Quarter Grade Structure

The current quarter grade structure is:

- 45% Summative Assessments: Projects, Tests, and Bistro.
- 35% Formative Assessments and Labs.
- 20% Employability Skills.

This structure replaces older or conflicting grade-weight references in the archive.

### Employability Skills

Employability Skills grades measure professional behavior and readiness. Evidence may include punctuality, uniform, materials, participation, communication, teamwork, initiative, coachability, station responsibility, and closing habits.

Employability Skills are not a soft extra. In culinary and hospitality work, employability is part of competence.

### Formative Assessments and Labs

Formative Assessments and Labs measure developing knowledge and performance through checks for understanding, practice, process, safety, technique, product, station work, teamwork, cleanup, and reflection. They should reward how students learn and work, not only how the final plate looks.

### Summative Assessments: Projects, Tests, and Bistro

Summative evidence measures demonstrated competence at a checkpoint. It may include projects, tests, practical exams, credential checks, portfolios, Bistro performance assessments, and major skill demonstrations.

Summative work should test readiness, not trivia. Students should be able to show what they can do, explain what they know, and apply standards under realistic conditions.

### Practical Evidence

Assessment should include:

- Daily employability evidence.
- Practical skill checks.
- Lab rubrics.
- Bistro live grading.
- Self-reflection and self-assessment.
- Credential progress.
- Kitchen math checks.
- Safety and sanitation checks.
- Portfolio or checklist evidence where useful.

### Live Bistro Grading

Bistro grading should be lightweight enough to use during service. The instructor or co-teacher may use a grid, clipboard, tablet, app, or station notes.

Station leaders and student auditors may provide evidence, but the instructor or authorized adult makes final grading decisions.

Live evidence should focus on:

- Pre-service readiness.
- Mid-service performance.
- Communication and role execution.
- Safety and sanitation.
- Closing and reset.
- Reflection submitted.

### Four-Level Practical Scale

The program may use a four-level practical scale:

4 means independent, safe, clean, accurate, efficient, and helpful to the team.

3 means competent with minor reminders.

2 means partially competent and needing frequent support or missing key steps.

1 means unsafe, unprepared, incomplete, or unable to demonstrate.

The scale should be used to clarify expectations, not to reduce professional judgment to a number.

## Leadership Philosophy

Leadership in West Ashley Culinary emerges through competence, reliability, communication, and service to the team.

The program should not turn leadership into abstract vocabulary or status. A leader in the kitchen is someone who arrives prepared, sees work, communicates calmly, protects standards, helps the station recover, and makes others better.

### Student Leadership Roles

Student leadership may include station lead, Bistro manager, expeditor, inventory assistant, sanitation lead, front-of-house lead, steward or dish lead, and peer mentor.

These roles should be taught and bounded. Student leaders may coordinate, remind, report, model, and support. They may not shame peers, approve unsafe equipment use, change grades, override instructor directions, handle major conflict alone, or act outside school policy.

### Leadership Through Speaking

Students should receive structured opportunities to speak in low-stakes and practical settings:

- Station briefings.
- Pre-service reports.
- Post-service reflections.
- Menu explanations.
- Safety callouts.
- Peer coaching.
- Quality checks.
- Guest service language.

Speaking builds leadership because students must learn to name what matters.

### Advanced Responsibility

Culinary 2 should feel like a higher-responsibility space, not an elitist club. Students earn trust through readiness, consistency, and professionalism.

Advanced students should not receive looser standards. They should receive clearer responsibility and a higher expectation of independence.

## Instructor Operating Principles

The instructor is the chief steward of program identity, safety, standards, assessment, and curriculum direction.

### Protect the Core

The instructor should protect the program from drift toward too many disconnected events, guest speakers, special projects, or novelty units. Events and partners are valuable when they strengthen the core. They become harmful when they displace too much skill practice.

### Teach the System Before Expecting the System

Students cannot be held to invisible standards. The instructor should make expectations visible through demonstrations, models, checklists, posted timelines, station language, repeated routines, and feedback.

### Coach During Work

Brief instruction, demonstration, circulation, live correction, and coaching should drive daily teaching. Students should spend significant time doing culinary work, not only hearing about it.

### Build Interruption-Ready Plans

School calendars include testing, field trips, guest speakers, weather, early release, pep rallies, school events, and partner programs. Plans should be modular enough to survive interruption.

### Use Canvas and Digital Tools as Support

Canvas or similar tools may serve as reference hubs, submission points, and documentation supports. They should not become the main experience of a hands-on culinary program.

### Maintain the Documentation System

The instructor should maintain WACOS as the current operating documentation system. New decisions should be recorded. Retired systems should be marked as historical. Major updates should preserve traceability.

### Adult Support Roles

Teacher assistants, co-teachers, substitutes, volunteers, and guest speakers support the program. They do not replace instructor authority in safety, grading, discipline, program direction, or final approval of student-facing material.

## AI Operating Principles

AI may support West Ashley Culinary. It must not govern it.

AI should help the instructor draft, organize, compare, convert, generate options, analyze reflections, create checklists, develop lesson supports, build prep plans, and maintain documentation. AI should reduce administrative friction so the instructor can focus more fully on students, safety, teaching, and service.

### Approved AI Uses

AI may assist with:

- Lesson planning from WACOS, calendar constraints, and skill targets.
- Bistro prep lists, station plans, and production timelines for instructor review.
- Recipe scaling and kitchen math tutoring.
- Skill checklist and line-check question drafting.
- Reflection pattern analysis.
- Rubric and practical-check drafting.
- Documentation maintenance.
- Comparison of new notes against current WACOS.
- Public-facing drafts when reviewed before use.

### AI Must Never Replace

AI must never replace:

- Instructor judgment.
- Live safety decisions.
- Food safety authority.
- Allergen verification.
- Equipment authorization.
- Student grading decisions.
- District policy.
- Guest recovery judgment.
- Final approval of student-facing assessments, recipes, safety instructions, or family/admin communications.

### Student-Facing AI

Student-facing AI should coach thinking rather than simply provide answers. It may ask guiding questions, help students reason through scaling, review vocabulary, quiz safety knowledge, or support reflection.

Student-facing AI must not provide unreviewed food safety instructions, invent program policy, create grades, or present archived/private material as current authority.

### Source-of-Truth Hierarchy for AI

AI tools serving the program must follow the same source hierarchy as this manual:

1. Current instructor-confirmed decisions.
2. Current WACOS documentation and this Core Manual.
3. Current subordinate documents.
4. Archived material only when explicitly identified as historical or supporting context.

AI should flag conflicts instead of silently resolving them when the current policy is unclear.

### Human Review

Instructor review is required before using AI-generated assessments, recipes, safety instructions, public communications, family communications, administrative communications, or student-facing policy statements.

## Governance

### Purpose of Governance

Governance keeps the program coherent. It prevents old documents, brainstorms, retired systems, or one-off decisions from becoming accidental policy.

### Document Hierarchy

The Core Manual governs identity, principles, and program architecture.

WACOS operating documents govern curriculum, Bistro, assessment, AI workflows, administration, onboarding, templates, and future development.

SOPs govern specific procedures.

Student-facing documents translate the program for students and families.

Archived material preserves history and evidence.

### Revision Rules

Any major revision should:

- Identify the reason for change.
- Name the source documents or current decision prompting the change.
- Preserve or update traceability.
- Retire conflicting language instead of leaving contradictions in place.
- Mark unresolved conflicts in a decision register or appendix.

### Semester Review

The Core Manual and WACOS should be reviewed after each semester. Review should focus on:

- Whether current operations still match the manual.
- Whether grading practices match stated categories.
- Whether Bistro systems are supporting learning.
- Whether certification plans remain current.
- Whether AI tools are using current source material.
- Whether archived material has been accidentally revived.

### Decision Records

New program decisions should be recorded in a decision note or decision register with:

- Decision.
- Status.
- Source.
- Reason.

## Appendix A: Source Tensions and Resolved Conflicts

### ProStart

Older documents and source packets reference ProStart curriculum and competition pathways. Current program direction states that the National ProStart Certificate of Achievement is no longer part of the program.

Resolution: ProStart references are historical unless a current instructor-approved document reinstates a specific ProStart resource for a limited purpose.

### Certification

Sources include ServSafe Food Handler, ServSafe Manager, CKC, NOCTI, AAFCS, and possible custom credential discussions.

Resolution: ServSafe Food Handler is the Culinary 1 credential and Certified Kitchen Cook is the Culinary 2 credential. ServSafe Manager is retired from the current Culinary 2 pathway. Other credentials may be researched or used as references, but they do not govern the pathway unless formally adopted.

### Grading Weights

The archive includes several grading structures, including 10/20/30/40, 25/35/40, Bistro as a major percentage, and evidence-type models without percentages.

Resolution: Current quarter grading is 45% Summative Assessments: Projects, Tests, and Bistro; 35% Formative Assessments and Labs; and 20% Employability Skills. The weights were confirmed for 2026-2027 on July 19, 2026, and the official category names were confirmed on July 23, 2026.

### Culinary 2 Identity

Older language sometimes frames Culinary 2 as advanced culinary work. Current WACOS language moves away from "advanced" as the governing idea.

Resolution: Culinary 2 is a full-year depth and operations course centered on fundamentals under pressure.

### Bistro Role

Some historical materials treat the Bistro as a major component or project. Current program direction treats it as central.

Resolution: The Bistro is the central instructional model and should shape curriculum, assessment, leadership, and operations.

### Archive Use

The archive contains brainstorming, generated drafts, source conversations, historical syllabi, and older frameworks.

Resolution: The archive supports traceability and historical context. It does not override current WACOS documentation or current instructor-confirmed decisions.
