# Program Philosophy

## Purpose
Define the instructional identity of West Ashley Culinary so decisions about curriculum, Bistro operations, assessment, leadership, and AI use stay aligned.

## Core Identity
West Ashley Culinary is a working kitchen inside a school. The program is not built around culinary spectacle, trendy "advanced" cooking, or isolated recipe completion. It is built around practice, service, teamwork, responsibility, repetition, and the development of habits that students can carry into jobs, further training, or daily life.

The most stable public language from the source history is:
- **West Ashley Culinary**
- **Classroom to Table**
- Hands-on culinary arts and hospitality
- Practice, service, teamwork, responsibility
- Real kitchen experience

## Pathway Model
| Stage | Program Meaning | Student Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Culinary 1 | One-semester foundation and exploration course | Durable beginner skill set, Food Handler credential target, Bistro exposure, and enough experience to decide whether to continue |
| Culinary 2 | Full-year depth and operations course | Stronger fundamentals under pressure, Bistro station competence, leadership opportunities, CKC alignment, and deeper craft practice |
| Hospitality Work-Based Learning | Work-based application stage, sometimes described informally as the next course after Culinary 2 | On-campus or off-campus application of culinary and hospitality skills |

## Learning Beliefs
- Culinary education is both technical and behavioral.
- Students are teenagers first; the system must account for mixed energy, sleep, motivation, confidence, and maturity.
- Culinary 1 should not try to be restaurant school. It should give students durable life and employability skills.
- Culinary 2 should not simply mean "fancier food." It should mean deeper fundamentals, real production, and higher responsibility.
- Bistro service gives the program a real audience and real consequences, but learning and safety remain more important than sales.
- Repetition matters more than novelty.
- Recipes are vehicles for skill practice, not the curriculum itself.
- Leadership should emerge through competence, reliability, and service to the team.

## Professional Philosophy
The program should teach students how to enter a kitchen calmly, observe what needs doing, communicate clearly, prepare carefully, work cleanly, recover from mistakes, and leave the room better than they found it.
