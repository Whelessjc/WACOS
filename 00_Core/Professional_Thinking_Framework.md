# WACOS Professional Thinking Framework

**Status:** Approved Core Framework
**Approved:** July 23, 2026

## Purpose

The purpose of West Ashley Culinary is not simply to teach students how to cook.

The kitchen is our classroom. Culinary is our vehicle. Professional thinking is our destination.

The program uses authentic culinary experiences to help students develop the habits of mind that lead to success in school, careers, and life.

This philosophy guides every lesson, lab, assessment, Bistro experience, and student interaction.

---

## Guiding Statement

> **We are not training chefs. We are training students to think like chefs.**

This statement is not about preparing every student for a culinary career.

It is about helping students develop the professional habits that skilled chefs demonstrate every day and that every profession values.

---

## What Does It Mean to Think Like a Chef?

Thinking like a chef means approaching work with intention, preparation, awareness, and professionalism.

Students learn to:

- Plan before acting.
- Read before doing.
- Prepare before beginning.
- Organize their workspace.
- Observe carefully.
- Communicate clearly.
- Solve problems calmly.
- Work safely.
- Manage time effectively.
- Produce quality work.
- Clean and reset for the next person.
- Reflect and improve continuously.

These are not simply culinary skills.

They are professional habits.

---

## Culinary Is the Vehicle

The West Ashley Culinary program does not measure success by how many students become chefs.

Instead, culinary provides an authentic environment where students repeatedly practice professional thinking through meaningful work.

Every recipe, lab, Bistro service, and cleanup routine becomes an opportunity to strengthen habits that transfer beyond the kitchen.

---

## Professional Thinking Transfers Everywhere

The habits developed in West Ashley Culinary apply across careers.

A nurse prepares before treating a patient.

An electrician organizes tools before beginning work.

A teacher plans before instruction.

A mechanic diagnoses before repairing.

An engineer tests before building.

A business owner anticipates problems before they occur.

Professional thinking is not unique to chefs.

Chefs simply provide one highly visible model of professional excellence.

---

## Three Levels of Learning

Every meaningful learning experience in West Ashley Culinary should intentionally develop all three levels when authentic opportunities exist.

### 1. Knowledge

What information are students learning?

Examples include vocabulary, food safety, recipe components, equipment identification, and cooking principles.

### 2. Skill

What are students practicing?

Examples include measuring, knife skills, communication, teamwork, heat control, cleaning, and service.

### 3. Professional Thinking

What habit of mind is being strengthened?

Examples include preparation, organization, observation, precision, stewardship, accountability, adaptability, communication, and continuous improvement.

The strongest lessons intentionally connect all three levels.

---

## Instructional Philosophy

Students learn best by doing meaningful work.

Instruction should move students beyond memorization toward understanding, application, reflection, and professional judgment.

Activities should encourage students to ask:

- What is my plan?
- What do I need?
- What could go wrong?
- How will I know if I was successful?
- What should I improve next time?

These questions help students think like professionals regardless of the career they eventually choose.

---

## Classroom Commitment

West Ashley Culinary maintains **High School, High Standards**.

Students are expected to grow through authentic practice, thoughtful reflection, and consistent professional habits.

Every student may not become a chef.

Every student can become more prepared, organized, responsible, and capable.

That is the lasting goal of the program.

---

## Implementation Rule

This framework is a governing lens for future WACOS development, not a requirement to rewrite every existing resource immediately.

When a curriculum unit, Daily Teaching Guide, assessment, rubric, instructor guide, or AI prompt is newly created or substantively revised, it should:

1. Identify the knowledge students are learning.
2. Identify the skill students are practicing.
3. Identify the professional-thinking habit being strengthened.

Existing materials remain usable. They should be brought into alignment through normal revision cycles.
