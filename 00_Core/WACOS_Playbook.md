# WACOS Playbook

Daily and Weekly Operating Guide

Current as of July 4, 2026

## Purpose

The WACOS Playbook turns the Core Manual into daily practice. It answers: How do we actually run the program tomorrow?

This is an instructor-facing operating guide. It is not a student handbook, full SOP manual, pacing guide, or gradebook policy document.

The Core Manual remains the authority. If this Playbook conflicts with the Core Manual, revise the Playbook or flag the issue for instructor confirmation.

## Daily Class Flow

A normal class should move through six stages:

1. Entry.
2. Briefing.
3. Kitchen Mode.
4. Work period.
5. Close and reset.
6. Reflection or evidence capture.

The timing may change by class length, calendar interruptions, Bistro needs, and safety requirements.

## Kitchen Mode Routine

Kitchen Mode is the daily transition from school-class behavior into focused culinary work.

Use Kitchen Mode to check:

- Uniform and personal readiness.
- Notebook, pen, and required materials.
- Handwashing and hygiene.
- Station assignment.
- Safety focus.
- Recipe, prep list, or task target.
- Tools and mise en place.
- Closing expectation before work begins.

Kitchen Mode should be brief, consistent, and visible. It is not a new policy category; it is the routine label for entering the kitchen standard.

## Monday-Wednesday Instructional Rhythm

Monday through Wednesday should be protected for new learning, technique, theory, skill labs, and practice whenever possible.

Use these days for:

- Demonstration.
- Skill building.
- Knife work.
- Cooking methods.
- Recipe reading.
- Kitchen math.
- Tasting and seasoning.
- Safety and sanitation practice.
- Line checks.
- Practical skill evidence.
- Prep foundations that support Bistro service.

Planning question:

What must students practice Monday through Wednesday so Thursday prep and Friday service are safer, cleaner, faster, and more independent?

## Thursday Bistro Prep Rhythm

Thursday is current Bistro Prep day.

Use Thursday to:

- Review menu and production goals.
- Assign stations and roles.
- Post prep lists and timelines.
- Check recipes, labels, containers, and storage plans.
- Complete production work within safe holding and storage limits.
- Confirm front-of-house needs.
- Confirm back-of-house needs.
- Identify shortages or substitutions.
- Set up Friday service for success.
- Capture employability and lab evidence during prep.

Instructor checkpoint:

Do we have enough readiness, product, staffing, and safety control to serve tomorrow?

## Friday Bistro Service Rhythm

Friday is current Bistro Service day.

Use Friday to:

- Run pre-service briefing.
- Confirm roles, menu, guest flow, and escalation path.
- Check sanitizer, labels, tools, serviceware, and stations.
- Observe live role performance.
- Coach communication and pace.
- Protect safety and guest experience.
- Capture lightweight grading evidence.
- Lead closing and reset.
- Gather reflection before memory fades.

Instructor checkpoint:

What did service reveal that needs to be retaught next week?

## Employability Grading in Practice

Employability is 20% of the current grade structure.

Employability evidence should be observable. It may include:

- On-time readiness.
- Uniform and materials.
- Communication.
- Teamwork.
- Initiative.
- Coachability.
- Safe behavior.
- Station responsibility.
- Awareness.
- Closing and reset habits.

Avoid grading personality. Grade visible professional habits.

Fast evidence method:

Use a simple daily mark, short note, or four-level scale. Record exceptions and patterns rather than writing a paragraph for every student.

## Lab Grading in Practice

Labs are 35% of the current grade structure.

Lab evidence may include:

- Safety.
- Sanitation.
- Mise en place.
- Technique.
- Recipe use.
- Timing.
- Product quality.
- Teamwork.
- Cleanup.
- Reflection.

Labs should assess how students work, not only what the final product looks like.

Fast evidence method:

Choose one or two focus criteria per lab. Rotate focus criteria across the week so grading stays possible.

## Summative Assessment in Practice

Summative Assessments: Projects, Tests, and Bistro are 45% of the current quarter grade structure.

Summative evidence may include:

- Practical exams.
- Major skill checks.
- Written safety or vocabulary checks.
- Open-note applied assessments.
- Credential checks.
- Bistro performance assessments.
- Portfolios.
- Capstone or production tasks.

Summative work should answer: Can the student apply the standard with appropriate independence?

## Student Leadership in Practice

Student leaders may coordinate, remind, model, and report. They may not grade peers, shame classmates, approve unsafe equipment use, override instructor direction, or handle major conflict alone.

Use leadership roles when they improve flow:

- Station lead.
- Bistro manager.
- Expeditor.
- Sanitation lead.
- Inventory assistant.
- Front-of-house lead.
- Steward or dish lead.
- Peer mentor.

Leadership evidence should show preparation, awareness, calm communication, service to the team, and follow-through.

## Cleanup and Reset Expectations

No class or service is complete until the room is ready for the next group.

Reset includes:

- Food stored safely.
- Labels complete.
- Dishes moving or complete.
- Laundry handled.
- Trash addressed.
- Floors safe.
- Tools returned.
- Equipment off or secured.
- Sanitizer and towels handled correctly.
- Stations checked.
- Instructor or assigned lead notified of issues.

Teach reset as part of the work, not as what happens after the work.

## Reflection Routines

Reflection should be short, useful, and connected to improvement.

Use prompts such as:

- What went well today?
- What slowed us down?
- What safety or sanitation issue showed up?
- What did your station need from you?
- What should we change next time?
- What do you need to practice before the next service?

Reflection may be oral, written, exit-ticket based, or collected through a form.

## Weekly Review Cycle

At the end of each week, review:

- Bistro wins.
- Bistro problems.
- Safety concerns.
- Missing fundamentals.
- Student leadership notes.
- Employability patterns.
- Lab evidence gaps.
- Summative readiness.
- Inventory or ordering issues.
- Reteach priorities.

Use the review to plan the next Monday-Wednesday instructional focus.

## Common Planning Prompts

Use these prompts when planning a week:

- What fundamental skill needs repetition?
- What did last service expose?
- What can students practice before production pressure?
- Which standards need to be visible on the board or station sheet?
- What can be assessed live without creating a grading backlog?
- What should be delayed because students are not ready?
- What can student leaders handle, and what must remain instructor-led?
- What could interrupt the week, and what is the fallback plan?

## Instructor Decision Checkpoints

Pause for instructor confirmation when deciding:

- Whether students are ready for Bistro launch.
- Whether service should be modified or paused for safety/readiness.
- Whether a recipe is approved for student use.
- Whether an AI-generated item can be used.
- Whether a student may use specific equipment.
- Whether a grade, discipline issue, allergen issue, or guest complaint needs formal handling.
- Whether an archived system should become current again.

## Weekly Minimum Documentation

Keep the weekly documentation light enough to maintain:

- Weekly menu or skill focus.
- Station assignments.
- Prep list.
- Service notes.
- Grading evidence.
- Reflection summary.
- Reteach priority for next week.

If the documentation is too complex to use during a school week, simplify it.
