# Life of an Order

Current as of 2026-07. Source: real Bistro training script, used to teach students the whole operation before they lock into a single station.

## Purpose

Before any student is assigned a station, they need to see how a single order moves through the entire Bistro - from the second a guest walks in to the moment they walk back out. Understanding a job in isolation misses the point: every role connects to the next one.

This script is written as a talk-track. Deliver it as a conversation - ask the question, wait for students to answer, then add the detail.

## The Eleven Steps

### 1. Guest Arrival

A guest walks in. Front of house (host/server) owns this moment.

The very first action: scan the guest in and assign a queue number. The number controls order, keeps things fair, and protects the flow of the room. (Teachers get front-of-line privileges - they're on a planning period with limited time; hospitality means reading the room and honoring people's time.)

This moment sets the tone - not the food, not the price. The welcome.

Ask: Are we looking up? Are we explaining the number system clearly? Are we calm when the line grows?

### 2. Seating

The guest is seated. This depends on a clean table, no crumbs, chairs straight - which depends on floors, counters, laundry, and dishes all having done their job already. Every station connects to the next.

### 3. Server Approach

The server walks up. What matters: eye contact, voice, confidence, menu knowledge. Rushing this part or not knowing the menu breaks the guest's confidence immediately.

### 4. Taking the Order

The order is taken. What has to be right: repeat it back, ask about allergies, note modifications, confirm clarity. Getting this wrong ripples forward - wrong food, wasted time, an annoyed guest, a stressed kitchen.

### 5. Ticket Enters the System

The ticket hits the kitchen - POS, ticket printer, or handwritten slip. Expo/the line owns it now. This is the brain of the kitchen; where the ticket goes and what order it's worked in matters.

### 6. Cooking

The line owns this - grill, saute, fry, prep. What matters: timing, temperature, communication, not panicking. If one station is slow, food sits, plates die, and the guest waits. One slow station affects everyone.

### 7. Plating and Checking

Before food leaves: check temp, check garnish, wipe the plate, do a quality check. Expo catches mistakes here - this is the last chance before a guest sees it.

### 8. Running the Food

Food hits the table. Right person, right dish, no auctioning (asking the table who ordered what), confidence. Know how to recover calmly if a plate is dropped.

### 9. Check Back

Two minutes later: how is everything, anything missing, water refills. This is what separates good service from just delivering food.

### 10. Clearing and Payment

The meal is done: clearing, payment, a genuine thank you.

### 11. Dishes and Reset

Everything goes to the dish pit - scraping, washing, air dry - and the next guest's setup begins. Full circle.

## The Point

Every step matters. If one part is weak, the whole system feels it. Students don't just work a station - they protect the flow.

This is hospitality. Not cooking. Not cleaning. Taking care of people through systems.
