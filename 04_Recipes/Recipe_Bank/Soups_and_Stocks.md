# Soups and Stocks

## Stock-Making Principles and Pork Stock

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - foundational technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/STOCK'S.docx`, standardized 2026-07. Kept the source's own teaching voice for the general principles, since they're genuinely useful guidance (not just a recipe).
- **Yield and portion size**: Not a portioned dish. Recipe as written: 6 gallon batch (768 oz).
- **Equipment**: Heavy-bottomed stockpot, sachet d'épices bag, fine sieve, cheesecloth, ice bath.
- **Ingredients**: 160 oz pork trotters, 160 oz ham hocks, 64 oz pork skin, 20 oz onion, 6 oz celery, 6 oz leek, 4 oz thyme, 4 oz oregano, 4 oz parsley stems, 4 oz black pepper, 8 oz crushed garlic.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Transfer bones to a heavy-bottomed stockpot. Add enough cold water to cover by about a quart per pound of bones.
  2. Bring to a simmer; skim the scum that rises to the surface.
  3. Add mirepoix (carrots, celery, onion) and a sachet d'épices (herbs, spices, garlic); tie the sachet string to the pot handle for easy retrieval.
  4. Continue to simmer, skimming impurities as needed. Top up water if the level drops below the bones.
  5. After 4-6 hours, remove from heat.
  6. Strain through a cheesecloth-lined sieve. Cool quickly (ice bath if needed).
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling; cool quickly and refrigerate/freeze promptly after straining.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Clear, not cloudy or greasy (a sign the stock never boiled hard); good body/gelatin from cartilage-rich bones (trotters, hocks).
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate a few days or freeze for months.
- **Scaling notes**: The source's general ratio applies across any protein: about 20% mirepoix by weight relative to bones. Cooking time scales by protein, not batch size: vegetable stock 45 min-1 hr; chicken/pork/lamb 4-6 hours; veal/beef 16-24 hours. Any bone type can be substituted for the pork in this specific recipe using the same ratio.
- **Skill focus**: Bone selection (cartilage-rich cuts for body), cold-water start for collagen extraction, never boiling or roasting bones (per the source's own emphatic guidance), skimming technique, sachet d'épices use.

## Chicken Stock

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - foundational technique.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `8 - Stocks & Sauces/Chicken Stock.docx` (a simple classroom ratio recipe) and `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Chicken stock.pdf` ("Basic White Chicken Stock," source: Rouxbe, a more detailed version of the same technique). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not a portioned dish. Classroom recipe as written: 4 lb bones, ~3-4 qt water. Rouxbe version scales to 6 lb bones, serves 6 (as a soup base).
- **Equipment**: Stockpot, fine sieve, cheesecloth.
- **Ingredients**: 4-6 lb chicken bones, 8 oz-1 lb mirepoix (roughly 2:1:1 onion:carrot:celery by weight; the Rouxbe version adds leeks), aromatics (parsley stems, thyme, bay leaf, whole peppercorns, celery leaves), cold water to cover by about 2".
- **Procedure**:
  1. Rinse bones and mirepoix to remove any dirt or particles.
  2. Place bones in the pot; cover with cold water. Bring slowly to a simmer over medium heat - do not let it boil.
  3. Skim the surface as impurities rise.
  4. Add the mirepoix after the first 30 minutes-1 hour of simmering (per the Rouxbe version); add aromatics tucked under the surface.
  5. Continue simmering, skimming as needed, 3-4 hours total.
  6. Skim fat from the surface, remove solids, and strain through a cheesecloth-lined sieve.
  7. Use immediately, or cool over an ice bath and refrigerate/freeze.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling; cool quickly and store promptly.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Clear, light gold color; clean chicken flavor without a boiled/dull taste (a sign of a gentle simmer, not a rolling boil).
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate a few days, freeze for several months.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight - keep the mirepoix at roughly 20% of the bone weight.
- **Skill focus**: Gentle-simmer technique (never boiling), skimming for clarity, building a bouquet garni.

## Vegetable Stock (Browned/Roasted)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - foundational technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `11 - Vegetable Forward Cooking/Vegetable stock.pdf` ("How to Make Vegetable Stock," source: Simply Recipes). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Sachet-Simmered Vegetable Stock below - this version actively browns the vegetables and uses dried mushrooms and tomato paste for a deeper, more savory result, a genuinely different technique and flavor target, not a minor variant.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 5 quarts (source yield). Not portion-counted; scales by batch.
- **Equipment**: Large stockpot, fine sieve, coffee filter or paper towel (for a very clear strain).
- **Ingredients**: 1 oz dried mushrooms (or 5-6 oz fresh, dry-sautéed first), 4 Tbsp extra-virgin olive oil, 4 cups chopped onion, 2 cups chopped celery, 3 cups chopped carrot, 1 cup chopped fennel bulb (optional), salt, 2 large garlic cloves (smashed, skin-on is fine), 1 Tbsp tomato paste, 1 Tbsp fresh rosemary, 2 tsp dried thyme, 1 tsp black peppercorns, 4 bay leaves, 1/2 cup chopped parsley, 4 additional quarts water (plus the mushroom soaking water).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Rehydrate dried mushrooms in 1 quart boiling water; set aside.
  2. Heat oil in a large stockpot; add onion, celery, carrot, and fennel. Salt lightly. Cook over high heat, stirring only occasionally, until the vegetables begin to brown (takes longer than expected given their moisture content).
  3. Add garlic and tomato paste; cook, stirring often, 2-3 minutes, until the paste turns a rusty color.
  4. Add the mushrooms and their soaking liquid, rosemary, thyme, peppercorns, bay leaves, parsley, and 4 additional quarts of water.
  5. Bring to a simmer, then drop to a bare simmer (surface barely bubbling). Cook 1.5 hours.
  6. Remove large solids with a spider or slotted spoon.
  7. Strain through a paper-towel- or coffee-filter-lined sieve for full clarity, changing the filter partway through as it clogs.
  8. Chill or freeze in jars, leaving headroom for expansion if freezing.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling; cool and refrigerate/freeze promptly.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Deep amber color, rich umami flavor (from the browning and dried mushrooms), fully clear if double-filtered.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate up to a week, or freeze.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by the 5-quart base.
- **Skill focus**: Dry-browning high-moisture vegetables for depth, building umami with dried mushrooms and tomato paste, fine filtration for clarity.

## Vegetable Stock (Sachet-Simmered)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - foundational technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/VEGETABLE STOCK.docx`, standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Browned/Roasted version above.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not a portioned dish; batch as written uses about 17 lb total vegetables.
- **Equipment**: Small stockpot, sachet/bouquet garni bag, fine sieve.
- **Ingredients**: 3 lb mushrooms, 9 lb onions, 2 leeks, 3 lb tomatoes, 2 lb celery, 3 lb carrots, 1 sachet/bouquet garni.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cut all vegetables into 2" pieces.
  2. Place vegetables and sachet in a stockpot; cover with water by 2".
  3. Bring to a simmer; simmer 2 hours.
  4. Strain, cool, and label.
  5. *Alternative*: roast or sweat the vegetables before adding water, for more color/flavor. Tomato consommé (see below) can replace some or all of the water for more body - note this makes the stock non-vegan, since the consommé uses egg whites.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling; cool and label promptly.
- **Allergens**: None inherent (unless made with the tomato consommé variation, which contains egg).
- **Quality indicators**: Clean, balanced vegetable flavor; clear if strained well.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate or freeze after cooling.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Plain-simmer vegetable stock technique (a simpler contrast to the browned version above), using a sachet for easy removal, extending a stock with a consommé for added body.

## Roux-to-Stock Ratio (Sauce Medium Thickness)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - foundational ratio/technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `20 - Nutritional Cooking/From TTC/Roux to Stock Ratio.doc`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not a portioned dish - a ratio reference. Recipe as written yields 11 gallons at medium (nappe) thickness.
- **Equipment**: Large stockpot, whip.
- **Ingredients**: 3.5 lb clarified butter or oil, 4.4 lb all-purpose flour, 10 gallons stock.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Melt the butter (or heat the oil); add the flour and cook over medium heat for 1/2 hour, stirring every minute or two.
  2. Cool the roux to within 50°F of the stock's temperature.
  3. Add the roux to the stock. Bring to a simmer, stirring with a whip every few minutes.
  4. Simmer 10-15 minutes. Season as desired.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy (if using butter).
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, lump-free sauce at a consistent medium (nappe) thickness.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold hot for service; cool and refrigerate promptly if not used same-day.
- **Scaling notes**: The ratio (roughly 35% butter/oil to flour by weight, added to stock) scales linearly to any batch size - this is the reference ratio underlying the Béchamel and Velouté entries below.
- **Skill focus**: Roux-to-liquid ratio math, temperature-matching roux and stock before combining (prevents lumps), whisking technique for a smooth sauce.

## Consommé (Raft Method)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - advanced clarification technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/CONSOMMÉ.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Batch size 128 oz, yielding 128 portions per batch (already at production scale).
- **Equipment**: Stockpot, cheesecloth, ladle (for breaking/ladling through the raft).
- **Ingredients**: 5 oz egg whites, 16 oz lean ground meat, 4 oz onion, 2 oz leek, 2 oz carrot, 6 oz tomatoes, 1/2 oz thyme, 1/2 oz parsley stems, 128 oz stock (any base - use the type matching your intended dish).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Whip egg whites until frothy.
  2. Combine egg whites, meat, mirepoix, thyme, parsley stems, and tomatoes in a stockpot.
  3. Add cold stock; mix well.
  4. Bring to a simmer, stirring occasionally, until the meat/vegetable mixture hardens and rises to the top as a "raft."
  5. Break a small hole in the raft to let the broth bubble through without disturbing it further.
  6. Simmer about 90 minutes.
  7. Strain through cheesecloth; degrease.
  8. Adjust seasoning.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock/egg/meat handling; confirm the raft mixture reaches a safe temperature during the simmer.
- **Allergens**: Egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Crystal-clear, deeply flavored broth - the defining test of a proper consommé.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold hot for service, or cool and refrigerate/freeze.
- **Scaling notes**: Already at production scale (128 portions per batch); scale down proportionally for smaller runs.
- **Skill focus**: Building and managing a clarification "raft," patience and gentle heat control (agitating the raft ruins clarity), degreasing and final seasoning of a delicate broth.

## Tomato Broth/Consommé

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - clarification technique; base for Vegetable Stock or clear tomato soup.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/TOMATO BROTH_CONSOMMÉ.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not a portion-counted dish; batch as written uses 4 qt tomato juice/purée as its base.
- **Equipment**: Food processor, tall pot, china cap or cone strainer, coffee filters.
- **Ingredients**: 4 qt tomato juice or purée, 3 cups egg whites (about 6 eggs), 1 cup onion (rough chop), 3 cloves garlic, 3 bay leaves, 1 cup parsley stems, 2 ribs celery (rough chop), 2 (4") pieces carrot (peeled, rough chop), 1 cup fennel tops (rough chop, optional).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Pulse vegetables, garlic, bay leaf, and parsley in a food processor until very finely chopped.
  2. Place tomato juice in a tall pot; whisk in the vegetables and egg whites.
  3. Bring to a simmer (do not boil vigorously). Cook 20 minutes.
  4. Strain a small amount at a time through a coffee-filter-lined china cap, changing the filter as needed, until the liquid runs clear. Gently press the solids to extract liquid.
  5. Over-ripe tomatoes puréed in a food processor can substitute for some or all of the tomato juice.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Fully clear, bright tomato flavor.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate; use as a stock extender or serve as a clear soup.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by the 4-quart base.
- **Skill focus**: Egg-white clarification technique applied to a vegetable liquid rather than a meat stock, patient fine-filtration.

## Béchamel Sauce

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - mother sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Mother Sauces.docx` (the full "Standardized Recipe Form" version, garlic- and nutmeg-infused) and `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Bechamel and Veloute Sauce.docx` (a simpler quick-ratio version without the garlic infusion). Standardized 2026-07. Both are the same sauce technique; the simpler version is noted as a faster classroom variant.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 cups (source yield - a technique-practice batch). Scales linearly for service volume.
- **Equipment**: 2 medium saucepans, whisk.
- **Ingredients**: 3 Tbsp butter (for the roux), 3 Tbsp AP flour, 1 Tbsp butter (for infusing), 3 cloves garlic (minced), 2 cups milk, 1/2 tsp ground nutmeg, salt to taste. *(Quick variant: 2 Tbsp butter, 2 Tbsp flour, 1.25 cups milk, salt and pepper - no garlic/nutmeg infusion.)*
- **Procedure**:
  1. Melt 3 Tbsp butter in a saucepan over medium-low heat. Add flour; whisk until smooth. Cook, whisking, until the roux turns a light golden ("white roux") color, about 3 minutes.
  2. In a separate saucepan, melt 1 Tbsp butter over medium-low heat. Add garlic; cook to soften without browning.
  3. Add milk to the butter/garlic; bring to a simmer.
  4. Add the hot milk mixture to the roux, 1 cup at a time, whisking continuously until smooth.
  5. Add nutmeg; season with salt.
  6. Return to a simmer; cook 10 minutes, stirring constantly, until thickened to nappe consistency.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, lump-free, nappe consistency (coats the back of a spoon); no raw-flour taste (a sign the roux was cooked enough).
- **Holding/storage**: Hold hot in a bain-marie with a film of butter or plastic wrap directly on the surface to prevent a skin from forming; refrigerate leftovers.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly from the 2-cup base; keep the roux-to-milk ratio constant.
- **Skill focus**: White roux technique, tempering hot liquid into a roux without lumps, building a mother sauce as the base for derivatives (add cheese for a cheese sauce, or Parmesan for an Alfredo-style derivative, per the source's own noted variation).

## Velouté Sauce

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - mother sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Mother Sauces.docx` and `Bechamel and Veloute Sauce.docx` (a simpler quick-ratio version). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 cups. Scales linearly.
- **Equipment**: Small and medium saucepans, whisk.
- **Ingredients**: 3 Tbsp butter, 3 Tbsp AP flour, 2 cups chicken stock (or a stock matching the intended dish - fish or vegetable stock make a fish or vegetable velouté), salt and white pepper to taste.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Melt butter in a small saucepan; add flour and whisk to a smooth paste.
  2. Cook, whisking, until the roux turns a light golden ("white roux") color, about 3 minutes.
  3. Heat the stock separately.
  4. Gradually whisk the hot stock into the roux until smooth.
  5. Simmer 10 minutes, whisking occasionally, until nappe consistency.
  6. Season with salt and white pepper.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, nappe consistency, no raw-flour taste.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold hot in a bain-marie; refrigerate leftovers.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly; keep the roux-to-stock ratio constant (see Roux-to-Stock Ratio above for a large-batch reference).
- **Skill focus**: White roux technique, matching the stock to the intended dish (the same base technique used in the Fish Velouté component of Poisson Dugléré in `Seafood.md`, and in Crawfish Velouté), building toward derivative sauces (e.g., suprême sauce with cream).

## Sauce Espagnole (Brown Sauce)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - mother sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Mother Sauces.docx` (uses olive oil and veal stock, deglazed with red wine) and `8 - Stocks & Sauces/Sauce Espagnole.docx` (uses clarified butter and brown stock, no wine, longer simmer). Both are the same classical technique with minor variance in fat/stock choice and simmer time - condensed into one entry using the Mother Sauces version as primary, with the longer-simmer variant noted.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 cups (Mother Sauces version). Scales linearly. *(The Sauce Espagnole.docx variant scales to about 5 qt brown stock and simmers 1.5 hours for a deeper reduction.)*
- **Equipment**: 3-qt heavy saucepan, china cap or fine mesh strainer.
- **Ingredients**: 2 Tbsp olive oil (or clarified butter), 2 onions (chopped), 1 carrot (chopped), 2 celery ribs (chopped), 4 cloves garlic, 1/4 cup tomato paste (or tomato purée), 1 cup red wine (omit if using the no-wine variant), 4 cups veal or brown stock, 10 black peppercorns, 5 bay leaves, 1/4 bunch fresh thyme (or 1/2 tsp dried), 8 parsley stems.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Heat oil (or clarified butter) in a heavy saucepan over medium heat.
  2. Add onions, carrots, and celery; cook until tender, about 6 minutes (or until well caramelized, per the longer-simmer variant).
  3. Add garlic and tomato paste; cook 3 minutes.
  4. Deglaze with red wine, if using; reduce until half the liquid remains, about 4 minutes.
  5. Add stock, peppercorns, bay leaves, and thyme (as a sachet, in the longer variant).
  6. Simmer 30-45 minutes (or up to 1.5 hours for a deeper reduction), until thickened to desired consistency, skimming periodically.
  7. Strain through a china cap or cheesecloth-lined strainer before serving.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling.
- **Allergens**: None inherent (confirm alcohol content from the wine is acceptable per program policy).
- **Quality indicators**: Deep brown color, glossy nappe consistency, no raw tomato-paste taste.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold hot in a bain-marie; refrigerate or freeze leftovers - this sauce is also the base for a Demi-Glace (see below) when combined with additional brown stock and reduced further.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly; longer simmer times develop more depth in a larger batch.
- **Skill focus**: Brown roux/mirepoix caramelization, tomato paste cooking to remove rawness, wine deglazing, long reduction and skimming, straining for a refined final sauce.

## Tomato Sauce (Mother Sauce)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - mother sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Mother Sauces.docx`, standardized 2026-07. This is the classical mother-sauce version (a base technique), kept distinct from the Italian pasta sauces (Pasta Pomodoro, Bolognese) that will be covered in the future International pass.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 cups. Scales linearly.
- **Equipment**: Medium saucepan, food processor or immersion blender.
- **Ingredients**: 1/4 cup olive oil, 1 cup onion (finely diced), 4 cloves garlic (minced), 2 cups whole canned tomatoes with juice, 1/2 cup water, 2 sprigs fresh oregano (minced), 8 leaves fresh basil (chiffonade), salt and black pepper to taste.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Heat oil in a saucepan over medium heat.
  2. Add onion and garlic; cook until tender but not browned.
  3. Add tomatoes with juice and water.
  4. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer 1 hour.
  5. Purée with a food processor or immersion blender.
  6. Add oregano and basil; season with salt and pepper.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard produce handling.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth (or textured, per preference) purée, balanced acidity, bright herb finish added at the end (not cooked down).
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate or freeze.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Long, low simmer to mellow raw tomato acidity, pureeing technique, adding fresh herbs at the end to preserve their flavor.

## Hollandaise Sauce

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - mother sauce (emulsion).
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `8 - Stocks & Sauces/Copy of Hollandaise Sauce.docx` (a classical clarified-butter/cooked-yolk-base technique, with an extensive list of derivative sauces) and `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Mother Sauces.docx` (a simpler double-boiler method). Standardized 2026-07, using the more detailed version as primary.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not explicitly stated in either source (the Mother Sauces version lists "Yield-2 cups" as a template default, likely not accurate for the ingredient quantities given - flagged rather than repeated as fact). Scales linearly; a 1 lb butter/4 yolk batch (the Copy of Hollandaise Sauce version) yields roughly 2-2.5 cups.
- **Equipment**: Small saucepan, medium stainless steel bowl, double boiler (or a bowl over barely-simmering water), whisk.
- **Ingredients**: 1 lb unsalted butter, 4 egg yolks, juice of 1 lemon, 1 tsp Tabasco sauce, salt to taste. *(Simpler double-boiler variant: 8 oz clarified butter, 2 egg yolks, 5 Tbsp water, 2 tsp vinegar, salt/pepper/lemon juice/cayenne to taste.)*
- **Procedure**:
  1. Melt butter in a small saucepan over medium heat; simmer to cook off the milk solids, skimming off impurities every 5 minutes until fully clarified.
  2. Whisk egg yolks and lemon juice together until well incorporated.
  3. Set the bowl over a saucepan of barely simmering water (or use a double boiler). Whisk rapidly, taking care the eggs don't get hot enough to scramble - they're ready when pale yellow and doubled in volume.
  4. Slowly drizzle in the clarified butter, whisking constantly, until thickened (nappe consistency).
  5. Remove from heat; add Tabasco and season with salt.
  6. *(Simpler variant: combine egg yolks, water, and vinegar in a metal bowl - not plastic or glass. Cook over a double boiler until lighter in color and ribbon-consistency forms. Heat clarified butter to 125°F; slowly whisk into the yolks. Season with salt, cayenne, and lemon juice.)*
- **Food safety controls**: Cook egg yolks to a safe temperature via the double-boiler method without scrambling them; hold hollandaise in the narrow safe zone (warm enough to prevent bacterial growth, not so hot it breaks) and don't hold longer than a couple of hours.
- **Allergens**: Egg, dairy.
- **Quality indicators**: Thick, glossy, pale yellow emulsion that holds together (doesn't break/separate); balanced acid-richness.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold warm (not hot) for a short service window; does not reheat or store well once made, due to the emulsion.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight/ratio (roughly 4:1 butter to yolks by weight); emulsions get harder to manage in very large batches, so consider multiple smaller batches rather than one large one.
- **Skill focus**: Butter clarification, cooking egg yolks to ribbon stage without scrambling, building and holding an emulsion. *Derivative sauces noted in the source (built from hollandaise by changing the acid or adding ingredients): Béarnaise (tarragon-shallot vinegar reduction in place of lemon), Choron (Béarnaise plus tomato purée), Foyot/Valois (Béarnaise plus meat glaze), Colbert (Foyot plus reduced white wine), Café de Paris (Béarnaise plus curry powder), Paloise (Béarnaise with mint instead of tarragon), au Vin Blanc (hollandaise plus white wine and fish stock reduction, for fish), Bavaroise (hollandaise plus cream, horseradish, and thyme), Crème Fleurette (hollandaise plus crème fraîche), Dijon/Moutarde/Girondine (hollandaise plus Dijon mustard), Maltaise (hollandaise plus blood orange zest and juice), Mousseline/Chantilly (hollandaise with whipped cream folded in - add reduced sherry for Sauce Divine), Noisette (made with browned butter).

## Demi-Glace

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - reduction sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/DEMI.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Batch size 128 oz, yielding 128 portions per batch (already at production scale).
- **Equipment**: Heavy saucepan.
- **Ingredients**: 256 oz veal or beef stock (source notes this works best with veal or beef; pork stock is usable but less effective for this purpose).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Reduce the stock over low heat (just below a simmer) until it coats the back of a spoon. (Tip from source: dip a spoon in, then run a finger across the back - if the demi holds a clean line, it's ready.)
  2. Do not season with salt until the very end, since reducing concentrates the stock's existing saltiness.
  3. Reduce slowly for the best result - rushing with high heat produces an inferior demi.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard stock handling; cool and refrigerate/freeze promptly.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Coats the back of a spoon (nappe/glace consistency), deep, concentrated flavor without a burnt or over-reduced taste.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate or freeze; concentrates further if frozen and can be thinned with stock or water as needed.
- **Scaling notes**: Already at production scale (128 portions per batch, 2:1 reduction from 256 oz stock). Scale down proportionally for smaller runs. This is also, effectively, what Sauce Espagnole becomes when reduced further with additional stock - see that entry above.
- **Skill focus**: Long, patient reduction, judging doneness by the spoon-coating test, timing seasoning correctly in a concentrating liquid.

- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly from the 32 oz batch; requires demi-glace made in advance.

## Vegetable Demi-Glace

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Stocks and Sauces unit - vegetarian reduction sauce.
- **Source and revision date**: `8 - Stocks & Sauces/VEGETABLE DEMI GLACE.docx`, standardized 2026-07. A vegetarian analog to the meat-based Demi-Glace above, using mushrooms, soy, and a cornstarch slurry to build body without meat stock.

## Homemade Chicken Noodle Soup

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Stocks and Sauces unit - from-scratch technique.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from `6 - Stocks, Sauces, and Soups/Chicken Noodle Soup.pdf` (source: Rouxbe, using chicken legs) and `8 - Stocks & Sauces/Homemade Chicken Noodle Soup (from scratch!) - RecipeTin Eats.pdf` (source: RecipeTin Eats, using a whole chicken and a stock-powder finish). Both are the same fundamental technique (make broth from raw chicken, then build the soup) with minor variance in the cut of chicken and seasoning approach - condensed into one entry using the Rouxbe version as the primary detailed technique, with the whole-chicken variant noted.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 4-6 (Rouxbe version). Scaled 4x below to ~16-24 portions.
- **Equipment**: Tall stockpot, fine mesh sieve, cheesecloth, large pot (for noodles).
- **Ingredients (4x batch)**: 20 chicken legs (or one 3.5-4 lb whole chicken, per the RecipeTin Eats variant, plus 1 head garlic halved), 8 stalks celery, 4 large carrots, 8 medium onions, 8 cloves garlic, 1 large bunch parsley, 40 sprigs fresh thyme, 8 bay leaves, 4 tsp black peppercorns, 48 cups chicken broth (built from the above, plus water to just cover), garniture: 8 carrots and 8 stalks celery (fresh, cut for the finished soup), 4 cups dried egg noodles, salt to taste, 8-12 tsp stock powder or bouillon (optional, per the RecipeTin Eats variant - a Vegeta-style all-purpose seasoning), black pepper, fresh parsley for garnish.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Place chicken in a tall pot; cover with cold water. Bring to a boil, skimming impurities, then drain and discard the murky water (this "blanching" step keeps the final broth clear - the RecipeTin Eats method skips this and instead just skims during the main simmer).
  2. Cover chicken with fresh cold water; add the mirepoix (onion, celery, carrot), garlic, and bouquet garni (parsley, thyme, bay leaf, peppercorns). Bring to a gentle simmer.
  3. Simmer gently until the chicken is cooked through and tender, 1.5-2.5 hours depending on the cut (legs cook faster than a whole bird), skimming as needed.
  4. Remove chicken; cool slightly, then pull the meat from the bones and set aside. Return bones/carcass to the pot; continue simmering the broth 1 hour more to extract further flavor.
  5. Strain the broth through a cheesecloth-lined sieve; discard the solids. Skim fat from the surface (chilling the broth first makes this easier).
  6. To build the soup: bring the strained broth to a simmer. Add fresh-cut carrots and celery (the garniture); simmer until nearly tender.
  7. Meanwhile, cook the noodles separately in salted boiling water until done, then drain and add to the soup (cooking noodles separately, rather than directly in the broth, keeps the broth from turning starchy/cloudy - stir the pot as little as possible if you do cook them directly in the broth).
  8. Add the reserved chicken meat to heat through. Season to taste with salt and pepper; garnish with parsley and serve.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard poultry handling; confirm chicken is fully cooked through before removing from the broth.
- **Allergens**: Gluten (noodles).
- **Quality indicators**: Clear, flavorful broth (a sign of gentle simmering and good skimming), tender chicken, noodles not overcooked/bloated.
- **Holding/storage**: Store noodles and vegetables separately from the broth if holding more than a day - noodles absorb liquid and bloat; broth freezes well for months, finished soup with noodles keeps 3-4 days refrigerated.
- **Scaling notes**: Scaled 4x from the Rouxbe source's 4-6 serving yield.
- **Skill focus**: From-scratch broth building (blanch-and-restart vs. single-simmer methods), skimming for clarity, separating broth-building and soup-finishing stages, timing starchy additions (noodles) to avoid clouding the broth.
