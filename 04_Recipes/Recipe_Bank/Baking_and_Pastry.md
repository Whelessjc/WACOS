## Cookies

### Macaron (French Macaroon)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - bagged/piped cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `BAGGED COOKIES/BAGGED COOKIES--MACAROON.docx` (source: Allrecipes, recipe by "Elle"). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated as a serving count; ~24-30 small sandwich cookies is typical for this batch. Ingredients as given: 3 egg whites, 1/4 cup white sugar, 1 2/3 cups confectioners' sugar, 1 cup finely ground almonds (by weight: 100 g egg whites, 50 g white sugar, 200 g confectioners' sugar, 110 g ground almonds).
- **Equipment**: Stand mixer with whisk attachment, silicone baking mat, pastry bag with plain round tip.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Line a baking sheet with a silicone mat.
  2. Beat egg whites until foamy; beat in white sugar until glossy and holding soft peaks.
  3. Sift confectioners' sugar and ground almonds together; fold into the egg whites in about 30 strokes.
  4. Test-pipe a small disk - it should flatten on its own. If it holds a peak, fold a few more strokes and retest.
  5. Pipe rounds (about 1.5" diameter) onto the mat, spaced apart. Let stand at room temperature about 1 hour, until a skin forms.
  6. Preheat oven to 285°F. Bake until set but not browned, about 10 minutes. Cool completely before filling.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Egg, tree nut (almond).
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, crackle-free tops, a "foot" (ruffled base) after baking, chewy interior.
- **Holding/storage**: Store filled cookies airtight; best within a few days.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by weight (weights given above are more reliable than volume for macarons).
- **Skill focus**: Folding technique and batter-consistency testing (the defining macaron skill), resting for a skin before baking, low-temperature baking to avoid browning.

### Ladyfingers

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - bagged/piped cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `BAGGED COOKIES/BAGGED--LADY FINGERS.docx` (source: Allrecipes, recipe by "Roxanne"). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated; a standard 2-sheet-pan batch. Ingredients: 4 eggs (separated), 2/3 cup white sugar, 7/8 cup all-purpose flour, 1/2 tsp baking powder.
- **Equipment**: Stand mixer, 2 large baking sheets, pastry bag with plain 1/2" tip.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Preheat oven to 400°F. Line two 17x12" sheets with parchment.
  2. Beat egg whites to soft peaks; slowly add 2 Tbsp of the sugar and beat to stiff, glossy peaks.
  3. In a separate bowl, beat egg yolks with remaining sugar until thick and pale.
  4. Sift flour and baking powder together.
  5. Fold half the egg whites into the yolk mixture; fold in the flour, then the remaining whites.
  6. Pipe onto prepared sheets. Bake 8 minutes.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Egg, gluten.
- **Quality indicators**: Light, spongy texture; pale golden color.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight; freezes well.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Separated-egg-foam technique (whites and yolks whipped separately), gentle folding to preserve air. *(Variation noted in source: substitute 3/4 cup flour plus 2 Tbsp cocoa powder for the flour to make Chocolate Ladyfingers.)*

### Double Chocolate Biscotti

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - bar/biscotti technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `BAR COOKIES--BISCOTTI/BAR COOKIE--CHOCOALTE BISCOTTI.docx` (source: Allrecipes, recipe by Janet Allen). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the plain Biscotti below - a chocolate dough and different mix-ins, a distinct result.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated; two 9" logs, sliced.
- **Equipment**: Mixer, cookie sheets, wire rack.
- **Ingredients**: 1/2 cup butter (softened), 2/3 cup white sugar, 1/4 cup unsweetened cocoa powder, 2 tsp baking powder, 2 eggs, 1.75 cups all-purpose flour, 4 (1 oz) squares white chocolate (chopped), 3/4 cup semisweet chocolate chips.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cream butter and sugar until light and fluffy. Beat in cocoa and baking powder; beat 2 minutes. Beat in eggs one at a time.
  2. Stir in flour by hand. Mix in white and semisweet chocolate. Chill dough about 10 minutes.
  3. Preheat oven to 375°F. Divide dough in two; roll each into a 9" log on a greased sheet, spaced 4" apart. Flatten slightly.
  4. Bake 20-25 minutes, until a toothpick comes out clean. Cool on the sheet 5 minutes, then transfer to a rack to cool 1 hour.
  5. Cut into 1/2"-wide diagonal slices. Bake at 325°F for 9 minutes, flip, and bake 7-9 minutes more.
  6. Cool completely; store airtight.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Crisp, twice-baked texture; even chocolate distribution.
- **Holding/storage**: Stores very well airtight, per the source's own note.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Double-bake (biscotti) technique, log-shaping and clean diagonal slicing after the first bake.

### Biscotti (Anise)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - bar/biscotti technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `BAR COOKIES--BISCOTTI/BAR--BISCOTTI.docx` (source: Allrecipes, recipe by JANDEE). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated; two logs, sliced.
- **Equipment**: Cookie sheets, wire rack.
- **Ingredients**: 1/2 cup vegetable oil, 1 cup white sugar, 3.25 cups all-purpose flour, 3 eggs, 1 Tbsp baking powder, 1 Tbsp anise extract (or 3 drops anise oil).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Preheat oven to 375°F. Grease or line cookie sheets.
  2. Beat oil, eggs, sugar, and anise flavoring until well blended.
  3. Combine flour and baking powder; stir into the egg mixture to form a heavy dough.
  4. Divide in two; shape each into a roll as long as the sheet. Place on the sheet, press to 1/2" thickness.
  5. Bake 25-30 minutes, until golden. Cool on a rack.
  6. Slice crosswise into 1/2" slices; place cut-side up on the sheet. Bake an additional 6-10 minutes per side, until lightly toasted.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Crisp, dry texture typical of a proper double-bake; balanced anise flavor.
- **Holding/storage**: Stores well airtight.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Oil-based (rather than butter-based) biscotti dough, double-bake technique.

### Rolled Sugar Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - rolled cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: Condensed from four sources that are the same base recipe at different scales/fat ratios: `Baking & Pastry/sugar cookies.docx` and `sugar cookies(1).docx` (identical, small batch, butter+shortening+granulated+confectioners' sugar), `Baking & Pastry/sugar cookies big version less bp.docx` (roughly 2x the small batch, same fat blend, reduced baking powder), and `ROLLED--COOKIE/ROLLED--SUGAR.docx` ("The Best Rolled Sugar Cookies," all-butter, no confectioners' sugar) - all four share word-for-word identical procedure text. Standardized 2026-07 using the larger batch as primary.
- **Yield and portion size**: Makes 5 dozen (source yield, both the small and large versions are labeled this despite different quantities - likely an un-updated template label; use the ingredient scale actually mixed to judge real yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer, rolling pin, cookie cutters, cookie sheets.
- **Ingredients (large-batch version)**: 12 Tbsp (3/4 cup) butter, 3/4 cup shortening, 2 cups white sugar, 1 cup confectioners' sugar, 4 eggs, 1 tsp vanilla extract, 5 cups all-purpose flour, 1/2 tsp baking powder, 1 tsp salt. *(All-butter variant, no confectioners' sugar: 1.5 cups butter, 2 cups sugar, 4 eggs, 5 cups flour, 2 tsp baking powder, 1 tsp salt - same procedure.)* *(Small-batch/original: half these quantities, plus 2 tsp baking powder instead of 1/2 tsp.)*
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cream together butter (and shortening, if using) with both sugars until smooth, about 5 minutes.
  2. Beat in eggs and vanilla.
  3. Stir together flour, baking powder, and salt; add to the wet mixture until just blended.
  4. Cover and chill at least 1 hour, or overnight.
  5. Preheat oven to 400°F. Roll dough on a floured surface to 1/4-1/2" thick. Cut into shapes; place 1" apart on ungreased sheets.
  6. Bake 6-8 minutes. Cool completely.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Clean-cut edges (dough was properly chilled), even light golden color, tender not tough.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight; unbaked dough can be refrigerated or frozen.
- **Scaling notes**: See yield note above - confirm actual output against the ingredient scale used, since the "5 dozen" label doesn't reliably track the batch size across source copies.
- **Skill focus**: Creaming method, chilling for clean cut-out edges, comparing all-butter vs. butter-shortening blends for texture.

### Shortening-Based Rolled and Drop Sugar Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - rolled/drop cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/crisco sugar cookies.docx` (branded Crisco/Pillsbury recipe). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Rolled Sugar Cookies above - this is all-shortening (no butter), which behaves differently (less spread, less browning) and the source builds in an explicit drop-cookie adaptation.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated in source.
- **Equipment**: Mixer, rolling pin, cookie cutters, cookie sheets.
- **Ingredients**: 2 cups butter-flavor all-vegetable shortening, 2 cups sugar, 2 large eggs, 4 Tbsp milk, 2 Tbsp vanilla extract, 6 cups all-purpose flour, 3 tsp baking powder, 1 tsp salt.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Beat shortening and sugar until smooth and creamy. Beat in egg, milk, and vanilla until blended.
  2. Combine flour, baking powder, and salt; gradually add to the shortening mixture until well blended. Wrap and refrigerate 1 hour.
  3. Heat oven to 375°F. Roll 1/3 of the dough at a time to 1/8" thickness; cut with 2-3" floured cutters. Place 1" apart on ungreased sheets; sprinkle with sugar if desired.
  4. Bake 5-9 minutes, until edges begin to brown. Cool 2 minutes on the sheet, then transfer to a rack. Frost/decorate as desired.
  5. *(Drop cookie variation: skip refrigeration. Drop rounded tablespoons of dough 2" apart on an ungreased sheet. Bake at 375°F for 7-8 minutes.)*
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Pale, tender texture typical of an all-shortening dough (less browning than a butter dough).
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: All-shortening dough handling and its effect on spread/browning, adapting one dough between rolled and drop formats.

### Ideal Sugar Cookies (with Royal Icing)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - decorated cut-out cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Martha Stewart Sugar Cookies.docx` (adapted from "The Martha Stewart Living Christmas Cookbook," 2003). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Rolled Sugar Cookies above - a richer, brandy-spiked dough baked to a paler finish specifically for icing work, not a general-purpose cut-out cookie.
- **Yield and portion size**: Makes 2 dozen (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer, rolling pin, cookie cutters, baking mats/parchment.
- **Ingredients**: 2 cups all-purpose flour, 1/4 tsp salt, 1/2 tsp baking powder, 1/2 cup (1 stick) unsalted butter, 1 cup sugar, 1 large egg (lightly beaten), 2 Tbsp brandy (or milk), 1/2 tsp vanilla extract.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Whisk flour, salt, and baking powder together; set aside.
  2. Cream butter and sugar until light and fluffy. With mixer running, add egg, brandy, and vanilla; mix well.
  3. On low speed, add the flour mixture until just combined.
  4. Shape into 2 discs; wrap and refrigerate at least 1 hour.
  5. Preheat oven to 350°F. Roll to 1/8" thickness on a floured surface; cut into shapes, leaving 1" between on lined sheets. (Scraps can be re-rolled once.)
  6. Bake until lightly golden, about 10 minutes - do not let brown. Cool on racks.
  7. Decorate with Royal Icing (below), if desired.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; contains alcohol (brandy, mostly baked off - note for service to sensitive populations).
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Crisp, pale (not browned) finish, holds a clean cut shape - the dough should feel firm, not soft, when it goes into the oven.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight; undecorated cookies freeze well.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Baking a cut-out cookie pale on purpose (for a cleaner iced surface), dough-firmness judgment before baking.

**Bundled Royal Icing recipe** (from the same source): 1 lb confectioners' sugar, 5 Tbsp meringue powder, scant 1/2 cup water. Beat on low speed until smooth, about 7 minutes; adjust water/beating time for consistency. *Use meringue powder rather than raw egg whites for icing served to children, pregnant women, or immunocompromised people, per the source's own safety note.* Test consistency on parchment before piping a full batch - the source recommends adding water gradually rather than all at once. Makes 2.5 cups.

### Taste of Home Sugar Cookies (Drop Style)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1, Baking & Pastry unit - drop cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Taste of Home Sugar Cookies.docx` (a family recipe, per the source's own headnote, dating to a Swedish grandmother born in 1877). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Rolled Sugar Cookies above - this is a drop-and-flatten cookie, not rolled and cut, a different technique and result.
- **Yield and portion size**: 30 servings (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer, drinking glass (for flattening), baking sheets.
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup butter (softened), 1 cup shortening, 2 cups sugar, 2 eggs, 2 tsp vanilla extract, 4.5 cups all-purpose flour, 1 tsp baking powder, 1 tsp baking soda, additional sugar for rolling.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Preheat oven to 350°F. Cream butter, shortening, and sugar until light and fluffy. Add egg and vanilla; mix well.
  2. Combine flour, baking powder, and baking soda; gradually add to the creamed mixture.
  3. Shape into 1" balls; roll in sugar. Place on a greased sheet; flatten with a glass.
  4. Bake 10-12 minutes, until set. Cool completely on racks.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Slightly crackled sugared top, tender crumb.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Drop-and-flatten technique (contrast this against the roll-and-cut technique in the Rolled Sugar Cookies entries above), working from a recipe originally passed down "by feel" and later measured out.

### Lemon Frosted Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1, Baking & Pastry unit - drop cookie with frosting.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/COOKIES.docx` (a Crisco/Pillsbury branded recipe). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 dozen (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer, cookie sheets, cooling rack.
- **Ingredients**: Cookies: 2 cups all-purpose flour, 1/3 cup sugar, 1 Tbsp baking powder, 1/4 cup all-vegetable shortening, 1/2 cup milk, 1 large egg (beaten), 2 tsp lemon extract. Frosting: 1/3 cup all-vegetable shortening, 2 cups powdered sugar, 1/4 cup warm milk, 1 tsp lemon extract.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Heat oven to 375°F. Lightly spray a cookie sheet with no-stick spray.
  2. Combine flour, sugar, and baking powder; cut in shortening. Stir in milk, egg, and lemon extract until well blended.
  3. Roll into 1" balls with floured hands; place 1" apart on the sheet.
  4. Bake 10-12 minutes, until golden. Cool completely on a rack.
  5. For the frosting, combine shortening and powdered sugar with a pastry blender; stir in warm milk gradually until spreadable, then stir in lemon extract.
  6. Spread a thin layer of frosting on the cooled cookies.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Soft, cake-like cookie texture, smooth spreadable frosting.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight; best within a few days once frosted.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Cutting shortening into a lean drop-cookie dough, building a simple shortening-based spreadable frosting.

### Cookie Mold Sugar Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - molded cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `MOLDED COOKIES/MOLDED--SUGAR COOKIE.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated in source.
- **Equipment**: Cookie mold(s), mixer.
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup white sugar, 3 cups all-purpose flour, 1 cup shortening (source recommends shortening over butter for this technique), 1 Tbsp vanilla extract, 2 eggs, 1 tsp salt.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Beat the fat 1 minute; add sugar and beat 3 minutes more.
  2. Beat in vanilla and eggs; mix 1 minute.
  3. Add flour and salt; beat 1 minute, scraping the bowl.
  4. Refrigerate dough 1-2 hours.
  5. Preheat oven to 375°F. Oil the mold, dust with flour, and tap out excess. Press dough into the mold, scraping level with a knife.
  6. Loosen edges and let the shaped dough fall onto an ungreased sheet.
  7. Bake 12-15 minutes, until lightly browned. Cool briefly before removing from the sheet.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Crisp, clean mold-detail definition (shortening holds detail better than butter, per the source's own substitution note).
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Cookie-mold pressing and release technique, choosing shortening over butter for detail retention.

### Classic Peanut Butter Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1, Baking & Pastry unit - molded cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `MOLDED COOKIES/PEANUT BUTTER.docx` (source: Allrecipes). Standardized 2026-07.

- **Equipment**: Mixer, fork (for the crosshatch pattern).
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup unsalted butter, 1 cup crunchy peanut butter, 1 cup white sugar, 1 cup packed brown sugar, 2 eggs, 2.5 cups all-purpose flour, 1 tsp baking powder, 1/2 tsp salt, 1.5 tsp baking soda.

### Basic Icebox Cookies (Lemon)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - icebox/refrigerator cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `ICEBOX COOKIES/EASY ICEBOX COOKIE.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: About 3 logs x 28 slices (source yield, roughly 84 cookies).
- **Equipment**: Mixer, wax paper (for shaping logs).
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup butter or shortening (softened), 1 cup granulated sugar, 1 cup packed light brown sugar, 2 large eggs, 1 tsp grated lemon rind, 2 Tbsp fresh lemon juice, 3.5 cups all-purpose flour, 1 tsp baking soda, 1/2 tsp salt.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Beat butter/shortening and sugars until fluffy. Add eggs one at a time, beating well after each. Add lemon rind and juice; beat until blended.
  2. Combine flour, baking soda, and salt; gradually add, beating just until blended.
  3. Divide into 3 portions; roll each on wax paper into a 12" log. Cover and chill 8 hours.
  4. Cut each log into 1/2" slices (about 28 per log); place on lightly greased sheets.
  5. Bake at 350°F for 12-14 minutes, until edges lightly brown. Cool on racks.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg, dairy (if using butter).
- **Quality indicators**: Clean round slices (even chilling and slicing), lightly browned edges.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight, or freeze the unbaked logs.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by log count.
- **Skill focus**: Icebox-log shaping and chilling for clean slicing. *Variations noted in source: add 1 cup toasted coconut, 1 cup toasted sliced almonds, 2 tsp poppy seeds, or 1 cup toasted chopped pecans in place of some of the lemon flavor.*

### Checkerboard Icebox Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - icebox/refrigerator cookie technique (advanced/decorative).
- **Source and revision date**: `ICEBOX COOKIES/ICEBOX CHECKERBOARD COOKIES.docx`, standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Basic Icebox Cookies above - a two-tone assembled log requiring precision measuring, a distinctly more advanced technique.
- **Yield and portion size**: Makes about 4 dozen (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer with paddle, ruler, sharp knife, rolling pin, plastic wrap.
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup (8 oz) shortening (room temperature), 1/2 cup sugar, 1 tsp pure vanilla extract, 1/4 tsp pure lemon extract, 1/4 tsp salt, 2.5 cups sifted all-purpose flour, 3 Tbsp Dutch-process cocoa powder, 1 large egg.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cream fat and sugar until well blended, 1-2 minutes. Add vanilla, lemon extract, and salt. On low speed, gradually add flour.
  2. Turn dough out (it will be loose/crumbly); knead by hand 1-2 minutes. Divide in half. Knead cocoa powder into one half until fully incorporated.
  3. Between plastic sheets, roll each half into a 7" square, about 3/8" thick. Using a ruler and sharp knife, slice each square into nine 3/4"-wide strips.
  4. Whisk egg with 1 Tbsp water for an egg wash. On plastic wrap, alternate white and chocolate strips, brushing between layers with egg wash and pressing gently together, to build a checkerboard log. Repeat for a second log, reversing the color pattern.
  5. Wrap; refrigerate 30 minutes or freeze 15 minutes.
  6. Preheat oven to 350°F. Slice each log into 1/4"-thick slices; place on a lined sheet. Bake 10-12 minutes. Cool 2 minutes on the sheet, then transfer to a rack.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Clean, even checkerboard pattern (precise strip measurement is the key skill here), consistent slice thickness.
- **Holding/storage**: Dough can be made 1-2 days ahead and refrigerated, or frozen several weeks; baked cookies store airtight up to 2 days.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by log count; precision in strip width matters more than batch size.
- **Skill focus**: Precision measuring and layering for a two-tone pattern, working two doughs (plain and cocoa) to matching consistency.

### Raspberry-Walnut Rugelach

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - international/rolled cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `INTERNATIONAL COOKIES/RUGHLACH.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 4 dozen cookies (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixer with paddle, rolling board, pizza wheel, offset spatula, parchment-lined sheet pans.
- **Ingredients**: Dough: 8 oz cream cheese (room temperature), 1/2 lb unsalted butter (room temperature), 1/4 cup plus 9 Tbsp granulated sugar (divided), 1/4 tsp kosher salt, 1 tsp pure vanilla extract, 2 cups all-purpose flour. Filling: 1/4 cup light brown sugar (packed), 1.5 tsp ground cinnamon, 3/4 cup raisins, 1 cup finely chopped walnuts, 1/2 cup seedless raspberry jam. Egg wash: 1 egg beaten with 1 Tbsp milk. Topping: 3 Tbsp granulated sugar mixed with 1 tsp cinnamon.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cream cheese and butter until light. Add 1/4 cup sugar, salt, and vanilla. On low speed, add flour and mix until just combined.
  2. Roll dough into a ball; cut into quarters, wrap each, and refrigerate 1 hour.
  3. Combine 6 Tbsp sugar, brown sugar, 1/2 tsp cinnamon, raisins, and walnuts for the filling.
  4. Roll each dough quarter into a 9" circle. Spread with 2 Tbsp jam; sprinkle with 1/2 cup filling, pressing lightly into the dough.
  5. Cut into 12 wedges (quarters, then thirds). Roll up each wedge from the wide edge; place points tucked under on a parchment-lined sheet. Chill 30 minutes.
  6. Preheat oven to 350°F. Brush with egg wash; sprinkle with cinnamon-sugar topping.
  7. Bake 15-20 minutes, until lightly browned. Cool on a rack.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg, tree nut (walnut).
- **Quality indicators**: Flaky, tender cream-cheese dough, filling that stays inside the roll (not leaking out during baking).
- **Holding/storage**: Dough freezes well (make a double batch and freeze half, per the source's own tip). Baked cookies store airtight.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly by dough quarter (each makes 12 cookies).
- **Skill focus**: Cream-cheese pastry dough handling, wedge-cutting and rolling technique, working in stages across multiple days (a real production-planning skill the source explicitly breaks into a 3-day workflow). *Filling variations noted in source: apricot-pecan, chocolate-walnut, cinnamon-raisin, walnut-fig, chocolate-coconut, cranberry-orange, chocolate-hazelnut, peanut butter-chocolate, apricot-coconut, cherry-chocolate, apple-walnut - swap the jam/filling combination while keeping the same dough and rolling technique.

### No-Bake Chocolate Oatmeal Cookies

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1, Baking & Pastry unit - no-bake ("faux") cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `NO BAKE COOKIES/NO BAKE CHOCOLATE.docx` (source: Allrecipes, "No Bake Cookies I"). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not stated in source.
- **Equipment**: Medium saucepan, wax paper.
- **Ingredients**: 1.75 cups white sugar, 1/2 cup milk, 1/2 cup butter, 4 Tbsp unsweetened cocoa powder, 1/2 cup crunchy peanut butter, 3 cups quick-cooking oats, 1 tsp vanilla extract.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Combine sugar, milk, butter, and cocoa in a saucepan; bring to a boil and cook 1.5 minutes.
  2. Remove from heat; stir in peanut butter, oats, and vanilla.
  3. Drop by teaspoonfuls onto wax paper. Let cool until hardened.

### Classic Yellow Layer Cake (with Fudge Frosting)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - classic technique with a distinctive ingredient trick.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Classic Yellow Layer.pdf` (source: BraveTart / Stella Parks, "Classic American Desserts"). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the other two yellow cakes here - this version's signature technique (a small amount of potato flour, in place of some cake flour, for lightness) is a deliberate point of difference from the source's own comparison to a standard Betty Crocker-style cake.
- **Yield and portion size**: One 8x4.5" layer cake, 12 servings.
- **Equipment**: Stand mixer with paddle, two 8x3" cake pans, parchment, serrated knife.
- **Ingredients**: 3.33 cups (13.5 oz) bleached cake flour, 2 sticks (8 oz) unsalted butter (cool, pliable, ~65°F), 2 cups (14 oz) sugar, 1 Tbsp baking powder, 1 Tbsp potato flour (not potato starch), 1 tsp kosher salt (half as much if iodized), 5 oz egg yolks (about 8 large eggs, ~70°F), 4.5 tsp vanilla extract, 1.625 cups (13 oz) milk (~70°F), 1 recipe Fudge Frosting (below).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Adjust oven rack to lower-middle; preheat to 350°F. Line two 8x3" pans with parchment and pan spray.
  2. Sift the flour; set aside.
  3. Combine butter, sugar, baking powder, potato flour, and salt in a stand mixer; mix on low to moisten, then cream on medium until fluffy, about 5 minutes, scraping halfway through.
  4. With the mixer running, add egg yolks one at a time, then vanilla.
  5. Reduce speed to low; alternate adding the flour (in thirds) and milk (in thirds), starting and ending with flour, mixing just to incorporate between additions. Fold with a spatula to ensure it's mixed from the bottom.
  6. Divide between pans, about 26 oz each. Bake until golden and firm, about 40 minutes (210°F internal) - a toothpick will have a few moist crumbs and the surface will spring back with a slight indentation.
  7. Cool 2 hours (no trace of warmth should remain).
  8. Loosen with a knife, invert onto a rack, peel off parchment, and reinvert. Trim the domed top crust with a serrated knife (helps the cake absorb frosting moisture).
  9. Place one layer cut-side up on a plate; cover with 1 cup frosting. Top with the second layer, cut-side down. Frost top and sides with remaining frosting, sculpting swoops with the back of a spoon.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Golden color, fine and tender (not coarse/cornbread-like - the source warns all-purpose flour instead of cake flour causes this) crumb, level layers.
- **Holding/storage**: Under a cake dome, keeps 24 hours at room temperature; wrap cut slices individually for up to 2 more days.
- **Scaling notes**: Can be divided among 3 pans for a 3-layer cake, made into 24-30 cupcakes (bake ~20 minutes), or baked as a 12x18" sheet cake.
- **Skill focus**: Using potato flour as a tenderizing trick (an unusual but deliberate technique - resist substituting all-purpose flour, per the source's explicit warning), trimming a domed crust for better frosting adhesion. *Variations noted in source: Pumpkin Spice (add ginger/cinnamon/cloves, swap some milk for pumpkin purée), Almond Torte (reduce flour, add processed toasted almonds - or hazelnuts/pecans), Classic Yellow Cupcakes (28 cupcakes, finish with Cream Cheese Frosting), Eggnog (add nutmeg/cinnamon, swap vanilla for bourbon/brandy, frost with a boozy Marshmallow Buttercream), Gluten-Free (swap the cake flour for a tapioca/rice/corn/oat flour blend, omit the potato flour).

**Bundled Fudge Frosting recipe** (from the same source; yields about 3 cups, enough for this cake or 24 cupcakes): 1 cup (8 oz) milk, 1 cup (8 oz) packed light brown sugar, 1 cup (7 oz) white sugar, 1/2 tsp kosher salt, 2 cups (8 oz) roughly chopped 72% dark chocolate (not chips), 1 Tbsp vanilla extract, 1 stick (4 oz) unsalted butter (soft, ~68°F). Combine milk, sugars, salt, and chocolate in a 3-quart pot; stir over medium heat until melted and bubbling, about 6 minutes, then stop stirring. Wash down the sides of the pot with a wet pastry brush to prevent sugar crystals. Cook undisturbed to 236°F, about 6 minutes; cool to 115°F, about 1 hour. Whip in a stand mixer with vanilla, then beat until thick and creamy (about 10 minutes); add butter a tablespoon at a time, beating until incorporated between additions, until it cools to 78°F. Let stand 30 minutes to firm before using. *Temperature is the key variable for this fudge-style frosting - undercooking the syrup (below 236°F) prevents proper set, and under-cooling before whipping (above 115°F) causes crystallization; a curdled/oily texture from cold storage can be fixed by microwaving briefly and rewhipping.*

### White Mountain Layer Cake with Marshmallow Buttercream

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - advanced technique (cooked-sugar buttercream).
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/White Mountain Cake.pdf` (source: Saveur). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Three 8" layers, typically 12-16 slices.
- **Equipment**: Digital thermometer, 3-quart stainless pot, stand mixer with whisk and paddle, three 8x3" cake pans.
- **Ingredients**: Marshmallow buttercream: 1 packet (2.25 tsp) unflavored gelatin, 1/4 cup cool water, 1 Tbsp vanilla extract and/or 1 vanilla bean, 3/4 cup water (for syrup), 3/4 cup plus 2 Tbsp light corn syrup, 2 cups sugar, 1/2 tsp kosher salt, 5 sticks (20 oz) unsalted butter (soft but cool, ~65°F). Cake: 4 cups (16 oz) bleached cake flour, 2 sticks (8 oz) unsalted butter (~65°F), 2/3 cup (4 oz) virgin coconut oil (solid but creamy, ~70°F), 2.25 cups (16 oz) sugar, 2.5 tsp baking powder, 1 tsp baking soda, 3/4 tsp kosher salt, 1 cup (8.5 oz) egg whites (about 8 large eggs, ~70°F), 2 Tbsp vanilla extract, 1 tsp almond extract, 2 cups (16 oz) buttermilk (~70°F).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Bloom gelatin in cool water with vanilla extract (if using); if using a vanilla bean, split and scrape seeds into the gelatin without stirring.
  2. Combine remaining water, corn syrup, sugar, and salt in a pot; cook, stirring until bubbling, then without stirring to 250°F.
  3. Pour the hot syrup into a stand mixer bowl; cool to exactly 212°F, then add the gelatin. Whip on low until melted, then medium-high until tripled in volume, snowy white, and balling on the whisk, about 10 minutes.
  4. Scrape into a container; let stand at cool room temperature at least 2 hours (up to a week) until thick and firm.
  5. For the cake: preheat oven to 325°F; line three 8x3" pans. Sift the flour.
  6. Combine butter, coconut oil, sugar, baking powder, baking soda, and salt; cream until fluffy, about 5 minutes.
  7. Add egg whites one at a time, then vanilla and almond extracts.
  8. Alternate adding flour and buttermilk in thirds, starting and ending with flour. Fold to finish.
  9. Divide among pans, about 22 oz each. Bake until firm and pale (browned only at the edges), about 40 minutes (210°F). Cool about 90 minutes.
  10. Finish the buttercream: whip the marshmallow base, adding butter a tablespoon at a time until light and creamy but thick enough to hang from a spoon (troubleshoot too-stiff by briefly microwaving and rewhipping; too-loose by chilling 15 minutes then rewhipping).
  11. Crumb-coat and frost the cooled cake.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; the marshmallow syrup reaches candy-making temperatures - handle with care.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Pale, fine crumb, marshmallow buttercream light and creamy (not greasy or loose).
- **Holding/storage**: Under a cake dome, keeps 24 hours at room temperature; wrap cut slices for up to 2 more days.
- **Scaling notes**: Batter can hold at room temperature up to 3 hours if working with fewer than 3 pans.
- **Skill focus**: Cooked-sugar (Italian marshmallow-style) buttercream technique, precise candy-thermometer work, troubleshooting buttercream texture by temperature.

### Jeweled Chocolate Cake (with Praline)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - melt-and-mix chocolate cake with candy garnish.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/jeweled chocolate cake.pdf` (adapted from Café Mezzo, via Ruth Reichl). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: One 9" round cake (source yield).
- **Equipment**: 9" deep round cake pan, saucepan, baking sheet.
- **Ingredients**: Cake: 1/3 cup cocoa powder (not Dutch process, plus more for dusting the pan), 3 oz good-quality bittersweet chocolate, 6 Tbsp butter, 1/3 cup neutral vegetable oil, 2/3 cup water, 1 cup sugar, 2 eggs, 1.25 cups all-purpose flour, 2 tsp baking powder, 1/2 tsp salt, 1/3 cup buttermilk. Praline: 1/2 cup slivered blanched almonds, 1/2 cup blanched hazelnuts, 1/4 cup water, 3/4 cup sugar. Frosting: 2 Tbsp sugar mixed into 1 cup mascarpone.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Preheat oven to 300°F. Butter a 9" pan, line with parchment, butter the paper, and dust with cocoa.
  2. Melt chocolate with cocoa, butter, oil, and water over low heat, stirring until smooth. Remove from heat; whisk in sugar.
  3. Cool completely; whisk in eggs one at a time.
  4. Combine flour, baking powder, and salt; whisk into the chocolate mixture. Stir in the well-shaken buttermilk.
  5. Bake about 45 minutes, until a toothpick comes out clean. Cool on a rack 10 minutes, then turn out, peel the parchment, and cool completely.

  8. Mix sugar into mascarpone for the frosting. Spread over the cooled cake; heap crushed praline on top.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; handle molten sugar with extreme care (oven mitt, no bare skin contact) when making praline.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg, tree nut (almond, hazelnut).
- **Quality indicators**: Moist, dense chocolate crumb, deep golden (not burnt) praline, crunchy praline texture against the soft mascarpone frosting.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate once frosted; praline can be made ahead and stored airtight (add moisture-sensitive garnishes close to service).
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly; praline batch size is flexible to taste.
- **Skill focus**: Melt-and-mix chocolate cake technique at a lower bake temperature (300°F), dry caramel technique for praline (color judgment, burn safety), a simple two-ingredient mascarpone frosting.

### Matcha Mousse Cake

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - advanced entremet/mousse technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Matcha Mousse Cake.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; scale depends on tart mold size used.
- **Equipment**: Parchment-lined pan, tart mold/ring, kitchen torch, several small bowls.
- **Ingredients**: Cake: 3 eggs (room temp, separated), 60 g sugar, 25 g oil, 60 g milk, 1 Tbsp matcha powder, pinch of salt, 60 g cake flour. Mousse: 2 gelatin sheets (12 g), 125 g milk (warmed), 100 g mascarpone (room temp), 70 g sugar, 175 g cream, 1 Tbsp matcha powder.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Cake: whisk a small amount of sugar into the egg yolks. Drizzle in the oil.
  2. Make a matcha paste with a little milk, then slowly whisk in the rest of the milk. Add to the yolks with the salt.
  3. Sift in the cake flour; whisk in.
  4. Whip egg whites with the remaining sugar to soft peaks; fold into the matcha base.
  5. Pour into a parchment-lined pan; bake at 350°F for 15 minutes. Cool, then cut to fit the mold.
  6. Mousse: bloom gelatin sheets in cold water until soft; squeeze out excess water. Add to warm milk; whisk until dissolved.
  7. Pour the milk over the mascarpone; whisk smooth. Add sugar; whisk until dissolved.
  8. Whip the cream until thick (not necessarily to peaks).
  9. Fold the gelatin mixture into the cream in two additions.
  10. Divide the mousse into 5 bowls at graduated matcha intensities (per the source: one bowl at 120 g plain, then increasing matcha additions of 1/4, 1/2, 3/4, and 1 tsp with correspondingly smaller mousse portions), thinning each with about a teaspoon of water before mixing in the mousse.
  11. Place the cut cake in the tart mold on a serving plate. Pour the plain mousse in first, then pour each successively darker shade slowly into the center without moving the mold, letting it push outward to create a gradient.
  12. Refrigerate at least 4 hours.
  13. Use a torch to loosen the tart ring; slice.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; use food-safe gelatin sheets and bloom properly for a stable set.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Distinct visible gradient of matcha shades, clean release from the tart ring, sponge cake moist (not dry) beneath the mousse.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate; best within a couple of days.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by mold size; the graduated-mousse technique works at any scale as long as the ratio between bowls is kept consistent.
- **Skill focus**: Genoise-style sponge (oil-based, whipped whites folded in), gelatin blooming and incorporation, building a color/flavor gradient by portioning and layering mousse pours.

### Steamed Cake

- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Steamed cake.docx`, standardized 2026-07. **Note**: the source's procedure mentions "stir in chocolate chips" but no chocolate chips appear in the ingredient list - flagged rather than silently added at a guessed quantity.

### Chocolate Pie

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - custard pie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Chocolate pie.docx` (source: Allrecipes). Standardized 2026-07. The source page included an extensive reader comment about tempering the eggs to ensure the custard sets properly - folded into the procedure below as the recommended method, since it addresses a real, commonly reported failure point (a runny filling) rather than being just commentary.
- **Yield and portion size**: One 9" pie (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Mixing bowl, saucepan, 9" pie crust.
- **Ingredients**: 1.5 cups white sugar, 3 Tbsp unsweetened cocoa powder, 2 eggs, 1 (5 oz) can evaporated milk, 1/4 cup melted butter, 1 tsp vanilla extract, 1 (9") unbaked pie crust. Optional: 3/4 cup chopped pecans.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Preheat oven to 350°F.
  2. Mix sugar, cocoa, evaporated milk, and butter in a saucepan; stir over medium heat until blended.
  3. In a separate bowl, beat the eggs. Slowly add about 1/2 cup of the hot chocolate mixture to the eggs, whisking constantly, to temper them (bring them up to temperature gradually so they don't scramble).
  4. Pour the tempered egg mixture back into the remaining chocolate mixture, stirring constantly - it will thicken noticeably within moments.
  5. Stir in pecans, if using. Pour into the pie shell (a deep-dish shell handles this well).
  6. Bake at 350°F for 45 minutes to 1 hour, until set. Shield the crust edges with foil strips if browning too fast.
  7. Cool at least 1 hour before slicing.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; the tempering step is also a food-safety technique (gradual, even heating of the eggs rather than a sudden temperature shock).
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg, tree nut (if using pecans).
- **Quality indicators**: Fully set (not runny) filling - properly tempering the eggs is the key to avoiding the "won't set" problem some cooks report with this style of pie.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate; slices cleanly once fully cooled and set.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by pie count.
- **Skill focus**: Egg tempering for a stovetop custard filling, recognizing and correcting a common recipe failure point.

### Contemporary Chocolate Mousse

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - advanced bombe-method mousse.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/chocolate mousse.pdf` (source: "On Baking" textbook). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 2 lb 2 oz (972 g); divide among 8-10 serving glasses (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Bain-marie, stand mixer with whisk attachment, chilled mixing bowl, digital thermometer, rubber spatula.
- **Ingredients**: 9 oz bittersweet chocolate couverture (64% cacao, chopped), 2 oz unsalted butter, 12 fl oz heavy cream, 3 eggs (4.8 oz), 1 egg yolk (0.6 oz), 1 fl oz water, 4 oz granulated sugar. Garnish: chocolate curved triangles (or other chocolate garnish), fresh berries.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Melt chocolate and butter together to 125°F; hold at that temperature over a bowl of warm water.
  2. Whip the cream to a thick but soft consistency in a chilled bowl; set aside.
  3. Whip eggs, egg yolk, water, and sugar together in a bain-marie until the mixture reaches 165°F (this is the "bombe" mixture). Transfer to a stand mixer with the whip attachment; whip on medium-high until it forms thick ribbons and feels cool to the touch.
  4. Rewarm the melted chocolate to 125°F if needed. Fold one-fifth of the whipped cream into the chocolate; whisk until smooth.
  5. Add the bombe mixture to the chocolate, folding gently with a rubber spatula.
  6. Fold in the remaining whipped cream gently until no streaks remain.
  7. Divide among 8-10 serving glasses. Garnish with chocolate decoration and fresh berries.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; the bombe mixture is cooked to 165°F, a safe temperature for the eggs.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, velvety texture (not dense or grainy - the source notes this results from the chocolate cooling too much before the whipped ingredients are folded in). If too dense, the source suggests using a higher-cocoa-butter chocolate, adding extra butter, or increasing the whipped cream.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate; best within a day or two.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly. **Chocolate substitution note from source**: for every 4% less cocoa mass than the 64% specified, add 1 oz (30 g) more chocolate to compensate.
- **Skill focus**: The "bombe" method (a cooked egg foam base) for a stable, food-safe mousse structure, temperature control at each folding stage (keeping the chocolate warm enough not to seize prematurely), gentle folding technique to preserve volume. *Variation noted in source: Flavored Chocolate Mousse - add 1-2 fl oz liqueur, coffee extract, or another extract to the finished mousse. For mousse used as a torte filling, the source also describes stabilizing with a small amount of gelatin and substituting Italian meringue for some of the whipped cream when working with a delicate fruit purée base (see the source's own "Procedure for Preparing a Mousse with Italian Meringue" for that technique).*

### Cream Puffs and Éclairs (Pâte à Choux)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - classical piped pastry technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `CREAM PUFFS/CREAM PUFFS_ECLAIRS_RINGS.docx` (choux recipe by Shellie Wendel; pastry cream recipe unattributed in source, likely also Allrecipes given matching format). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Saucepan, pastry bag with 1/2" round tip (or two spoons), skewer, wire rack.
- **Ingredients**: Choux dough: 8 Tbsp unsalted butter (plus more for greasing), salt, 1 cup flour, 4 eggs. Pastry cream filling: 2 cups milk, 1/4 cup white sugar (divided, see note below - source lists sugar twice, once at 1/4 cup and again as 1/3 cup for a total of about 7/12 cup), 2 egg yolks, 1 egg, 1/4 cup cornstarch, 2 Tbsp butter, 1 tsp vanilla extract.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Heat oven to 400°F; grease a baking sheet with butter.
  2. Bring butter, a pinch of salt, and 1 cup water to a boil in a saucepan.
  3. Reduce heat to low; add all the flour at once. Stir constantly until the mixture pulls away from the pan and forms a ball, about 30 seconds.
  4. Remove from heat; beat in the eggs one at a time (an electric mixer helps) until smooth. (Dough can be covered and refrigerated up to 2 days at this point.)
  5. Pipe (or spoon) onto the baking sheet: cream puffs about 1" circles, éclairs as 3-4" fingers about 1" wide.
  6. Bake until golden, puffed, and hollow-sounding when tapped - about 30 minutes for cream puffs, 40 for éclairs. Prick with a skewer to release steam; cool on a rack.
  7. For the pastry cream: bring milk and 1/4 cup sugar to a boil in a saucepan. Whisk egg yolks and egg together in a bowl; stir in the remaining sugar and cornstarch until smooth.
  8. Drizzle the boiling milk into the egg mixture in a thin stream while whisking, to avoid cooking the eggs. Return everything to the saucepan; bring slowly back to a boil, stirring constantly so it doesn't curdle or scorch.
  9. Once thickened and boiling, remove from heat. Stir in butter and vanilla until fully blended.
  10. Pour into a heatproof container; press plastic wrap directly onto the surface to prevent a skin. Refrigerate until chilled.
  11. To fill: poke a hole and pipe filling in, or split and spoon filling in like a sandwich. Serve as is or drizzle with chocolate sauce.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; cook the pastry cream to a full boil to fully set the starch and cook the eggs through.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Choux puffed and hollow (not doughy inside), pastry cream smooth and thick (not lumpy or thin - a sign of proper tempering and full boiling).
- **Holding/storage**: Fill shells close to serving to keep them crisp; pastry cream keeps refrigerated (plastic pressed to the surface) a few days.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Pâte à choux technique (cooking the panade, then beating in eggs to the right consistency), pastry cream tempering and full-boil starch activation, timing fill vs. bake to preserve shell crispness.

### Vanilla Bean Ice Cream (Professional Batch)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - professional-scale frozen dessert.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/VANNILLA ICE CREAM.docx`, standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Already at production scale: batch size 128 oz, yielding 128 portions per batch.
- **Equipment**: Large saucepan, candy/frying thermometer, ice cream freezer.
- **Ingredients**: 64 oz half and half, 32 oz heavy whipping cream, 32 oz sugar, 4 oz vanilla bean (with pulp).
- **Procedure**:
  1. Combine all ingredients, including the split vanilla bean and its pulp, in a large saucepan over medium heat.
  2. Attach a thermometer; bring to 170°F, stirring occasionally. (If no thermometer is available, bring just barely to a simmer and remove from heat at the first sign of a bubble - do not let it boil.)

  4. Pour into a lidded container; refrigerate overnight to mellow the flavor and texture.
  5. Freeze in an ice cream freezer per the unit's instructions - it won't freeze fully hard in the machine; once increased 1/2 to 3/4 in volume and at soft-serve consistency, transfer to a lidded container and harden in the freezer at least 1 hour before serving.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling; the 170°F hold pasteurizes the base.
- **Allergens**: Dairy.
- **Quality indicators**: Visible vanilla bean flecks, rich mouthfeel, properly hardened (not icy) after freezing.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate the base overnight before churning; harden churned ice cream at least 1 hour before serving.
- **Scaling notes**: Already at production scale (128 portions per batch). Scale down proportionally for smaller runs.
- **Skill focus**: Heat-treating a dairy base to a target temperature without a thermometer if needed (visual bubble cue), overnight aging for flavor/texture development, real vanilla bean use.

### Sugary Hazelnut Ice Cream (Pacojet Method)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - advanced/equipment-specific frozen dessert.
- **Source and revision date**: `Sugary Hazelnut Ice Cream (Pacojet Method).docx` (root of archive; base adapted from Jeni's Splendid Ice Creams method). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Saucepan, fine sieve, Pacojet container and machine (or a standard ice cream maker as a substitute, noting the texture will differ from the Pacojet's characteristic ultra-smooth result).
- **Ingredients**: 2 cups whole milk, 4 tsp cornstarch, 1.25 cups heavy cream, 2/3 cup sugar, 2 Tbsp light corn syrup, 1/4 tsp kosher salt, 3 Tbsp cream cheese (softened), 1 cup chopped sugared/caramelized hazelnuts (for infusion), 1/2 cup chopped sugared hazelnuts (for mix-in). Optional: 4 oz melted dark chocolate, 2 Tbsp espresso or coffee reduction, for garnish.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Whisk 1/4 cup of the milk with the cornstarch to make a slurry; set aside.
  2. Combine remaining milk, cream, sugar, corn syrup, salt, and 1 cup chopped hazelnuts in a saucepan. Heat to a gentle simmer (do not boil); steep 15-30 minutes for deeper hazelnut flavor.
  3. Strain through a fine sieve, pressing on the hazelnuts to extract liquid; discard solids.
  4. Return the strained mixture to the pot; bring back to a gentle simmer. Whisk in the cornstarch slurry; cook 1-2 minutes, stirring, until slightly thickened.
  5. Whisk the softened cream cheese smooth in a bowl. Temper in a ladle of the warm base, then whisk in the rest.
  6. Pour into a Pacojet container (or a zip-top bag, ice-bathed 30 minutes for rapid cooling). Freeze overnight.
  7. Process in the Pacojet per manufacturer instructions. Fold in the remaining 1/2 cup chopped hazelnuts for crunch, if desired.
  8. Garnish with melted dark chocolate or a coffee reduction swirl, whole hazelnuts, or flaky salt. Pairs well with biscotti or as part of an affogato.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, tree nut (hazelnut).
- **Quality indicators**: Ultra-smooth, dense texture characteristic of the Pacojet method, pronounced caramelized-hazelnut flavor.
- **Holding/storage**: Freeze the base overnight before processing; processed ice cream is best served soon after spinning (that's the nature of the Pacojet method).
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly; a standard ice cream maker can substitute for the Pacojet with a different (less dense/smooth) final texture.
- **Skill focus**: Cream-cheese-stabilized ice cream base (a Jeni's-style technique for a cleaner scoop), infusing with caramelized nuts, Pacojet-specific processing.

### Chocolate Bonbons

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - chocolate tempering and molding technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Chocolate Bonbons.docx` (formatted as a lesson plan). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: About 24 bonbons (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Silicone or polycarbonate bonbon mold, double boiler or microwave, thermometer, piping bag or small spoon, spatula, small saucepan.
- **Ingredients**: Shell: 12 oz high-quality couverture chocolate (dark, milk, or white). Choose one or more fillings: Ganache: 4 oz dark chocolate (finely chopped), 1/4 cup heavy cream, 1 Tbsp unsalted butter. Caramel: 1/2 cup sugar, 3 Tbsp unsalted butter, 1/4 cup heavy cream, pinch of salt. Fruit: 1/2 cup fruit purée (raspberry or strawberry), 2 Tbsp sugar, 1 tsp cornstarch. Peanut Butter: 1/2 cup peanut butter, 2 Tbsp powdered sugar, 1 Tbsp melted butter.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Temper the chocolate: chop finely, melt two-thirds in a double boiler or microwave (15-second bursts) to 115°F (dark), 110°F (milk), or 105°F (white). Remove from heat; stir in the remaining chocolate gradually until cooled to 88-90°F (dark), 86-88°F (milk), or 80-82°F (white).
  2. Pour tempered chocolate into the mold, filling each cavity. Tap to remove air bubbles. Flip over a bowl to drain excess, leaving a thin shell. Scrape the top clean; refrigerate 5-10 minutes until set.
  3. Prepare the chosen filling(s): Ganache - heat cream until steaming, pour over chopped chocolate, stir smooth, add butter. Caramel - melt sugar until amber, add butter, then slowly stir in cream; cool. Fruit - heat purée with sugar; thicken with a cornstarch slurry; cool. Peanut Butter - mix all ingredients smooth.
  4. Fill each shell 3/4 full with the chosen filling using a piping bag or spoon. Let sit a few minutes to set.
  5. Seal with more tempered chocolate; scrape off excess and refrigerate about 30 minutes until fully set.
  6. Pop out of the mold carefully.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling; keep filled bonbons refrigerated until served.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, peanut (if using that filling); tree nut cross-contact possible depending on chocolate brand.
- **Quality indicators**: Glossy, snappy chocolate shell (a sign of proper temper), clean release from the mold, filling fully sealed with no leaks.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate; bring to cool room temperature before serving for the best texture/sheen.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by mold cavity count.
- **Skill focus**: Chocolate tempering by temperature stages, mold-shell technique (thin even coating, no air bubbles), filling and sealing without leaks. *For general chocolate molding technique reference (mold care, temperature ranges for polycarbonate vs. metal molds, hollow figure molding), see the "On Baking" textbook excerpt this file's broader chapter was drawn from - the source itself includes several pages of general molding guidance beyond this specific bonbon formula.*

### Classic Fudge (Collegiate Variations)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - historical candy-making survey.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Fudge Recipes.doc` - a collection of nine named fudge recipes in one file, each attributed to its traditional origin in the source. Standardized 2026-07 as a single entry covering the shared technique with each variant's distinct twist noted, rather than nine near-duplicate entries, since most share the same soft-ball-stage cook-and-beat method.
- **Yield and portion size**: Varies by variant - see individual yields noted below.
- **Equipment**: Heavy saucepan, candy thermometer, buttered pan (size varies by variant).
- **Base technique (shared across most variants)**: Combine the sugar(s) and dairy (cream or evaporated milk) in a heavy saucepan; cook over moderate heat, stirring, until hot, then add chocolate and stir constantly until it reaches soft-ball stage (238°F unless noted otherwise). Remove from heat, add butter/flavoring, cool slightly, then beat until the fudge thickens and loses its gloss. Pour into a buttered pan; cool and cut into squares.
- **Named variants**:
  - **Vassar Fudge** (origin: Emelyn B. Hartridge, Vassar College): 2 cups sugar, 1 cup cream, 2 oz unsweetened chocolate (chopped), 1 Tbsp butter. Cut into diamond shapes before fully hardened.
  - **Wellesley College Fudge**: as Vassar, plus 1/2 lb marshmallows added with the butter.
  - **Smith College Fudge** (closer to penuche): 4 Tbsp butter, 1 cup granulated sugar, 1 cup brown sugar, 1/4 cup molasses, 1/2 cup heavy cream, 2 oz unsweetened chocolate, 1.5 tsp vanilla.
  - **Peanut Butter Fudge** (yield: 3 lb): 4 cups granulated sugar, 1 cup light brown sugar, 1/2 cup butter, 1.5 cups heavy cream, 7 oz marshmallow creme, 16 oz peanut butter. Boil 7 minutes, stirring constantly, then stir in marshmallow creme and peanut butter off heat.
  - **Vegan Peanut Butter Fudge** (no-cook): 3/4 cup vegan margarine (melted), 1 cup peanut butter, 1.75 cups confectioners' sugar. Melt margarine, stir in peanut butter, gradually stir in sugar; no thermometer needed.
  - **Microwave Fudge** (no thermometer): 2 cups chocolate chips, 1 (14 oz) can sweetened condensed milk, 1.25 cups chopped nuts, 1 tsp vanilla. Microwave chips and milk at 50% power 3-5 minutes, stir in nuts and vanilla.
  - **Almost Sugar-Free Fudge**: 1 envelope gelatin (bloomed in 1/4 cup cold water), 1 square unsweetened chocolate, 3/4 tsp liquid sweetener, 1/2 cup evaporated milk, 1/4 cup water, 1/4 cup chopped nuts. Uses gelatin rather than sugar crystallization to set.
  - **Fantasy Fudge** (yield: 3 lb): 1.5 sticks salted butter, 3 cups sugar, 5.5 oz can evaporated milk, 12 oz chocolate chips, 7 oz marshmallow creme, 1 cup chopped nuts, 1 tsp vanilla. Microwave-based: melt butter, add sugar/milk, microwave to a boil, then stir in remaining ingredients off heat.
  - **Vanilla Fudge** (yield: 1 lb) and **Chocolate Fudge** (yield: 1 lb, doubles well): both use 1 cup heavy cream, 2 cups sugar, plus butter/corn syrup (vanilla) or chocolate chips/walnuts (chocolate); both use the classic wash-down-the-pot-with-a-wet-pastry-brush technique to prevent sugar crystals, cook to soft-ball (238°F), shock in a water bath for 1 minute to stop cooking, then beat at 110°F until creamy.
  - **Easy Fudge** (yield: 1 lb, microwave, no thermometer): 1/4 cup heavy cream, 1 box powdered sugar, 1/2 cup cocoa, 1 stick butter (cut into pieces), 1 tsp vanilla. Microwave 2 minutes, mix, pour, cool, cut.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling; cooked variants involve very hot sugar syrup - handle with care.
- **Allergens**: Dairy; tree nut or peanut depending on variant.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, matte set (cooked variants) or firm, non-greasy set (no-cook variants); varies by variant as noted.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight at room temperature (refrigerate the Chocolate Peanut Butter Fudge variant above, which uses fresh dairy without a cooked sugar stage stabilizing it as strongly).
- **Scaling notes**: Each variant scales independently at its own stated yield.
- **Skill focus**: Comparing multiple fudge-making techniques side by side - true cooked (soft-ball stage) candy vs. no-cook melt-and-mix vs. gelatin-set vs. microwave shortcut - and how each affects final texture, plus recognizing shared technique across recipes from different named sources (a survey/comparison exercise as much as a single recipe).

### Fudge (Marshmallow Creme and Pecan)

### Marshmallows

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - gelatin/sugar-syrup confection technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Marshmallows.pdf` (source: NYT Cooking). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield about 36 (1.5") marshmallows (source yield).
- **Equipment**: 8x8" baking dish, stand mixer with whisk, candy thermometer, long sharp knife.
- **Ingredients**: Neutral oil (for greasing), 3 envelopes (2 Tbsp + 1.5 tsp) powdered unflavored gelatin, 1.5 cups (302 g) granulated sugar, 1 cup light corn syrup, 1/4 tsp kosher salt, 1 Tbsp vanilla bean paste or extract, 1/2 cup (51 g) confectioners' sugar.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Lightly oil an 8x8" dish. Combine gelatin with 1/2 cup cold water in a stand mixer bowl; set aside.
  2. Combine granulated sugar, corn syrup, salt, and 1/2 cup cold water in a saucepan. Stir just to combine, then cook over medium-high heat, swirling occasionally, until it reaches 240°F, about 10 minutes.
  3. With the mixer on low, carefully pour the hot syrup into the gelatin mixture; mix until the gelatin dissolves. Add vanilla.
  4. Turn to high; whip until very thick, stiff, and opaque, about 10 minutes.
  5. Pour into the prepared dish; spread with a knife. Let sit uncovered until firm, about 4 hours at room temperature or 2 hours refrigerated.
  6. Sift confectioners' sugar generously onto a work surface; unmold the marshmallow onto it, pulling out with fingertips. Dust the top; cut into 1.5" cubes with a long, sharp knife, cleaning the blade periodically as it gets sticky.
  7. Toss cubes in confectioners' sugar to coat all sides. Store airtight up to 4 days.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard handling; hot sugar syrup requires care.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Thick, stiff, opaque whip (not runny - a sign of adequate whipping time), clean-cut cubes fully coated in sugar to prevent sticking.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight up to 4 days at room temperature.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by pan size.
- **Skill focus**: Cooked sugar syrup into a whipped gelatin base, precise candy-thermometer work, cutting and coating a very sticky finished product cleanly.

### Candied Citrus Peel

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - preserving/garnish technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/CANDIED CITRUS PEEL.docx`, standardized 2026-07.

- **Quality indicators**: Translucent, tender peel; glossy sugar coating if glazed.
- **Holding/storage**: Store in an airtight container at room temperature; can also be dried further in a dehydrator.
- **Scaling notes**: Grapefruit needs 3 oz sugar per peel and 3 water changes during blanching; lime needs only 1.5 oz sugar per peel.

### Swiss Meringue Buttercream

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - cooked-egg-white buttercream technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Swiss Meringue Buttercream.doc`, standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Italian Meringue Buttercream below - a different cooking method (heating egg whites and sugar together over a bain-marie, vs. pouring hot syrup into whipped whites), a genuinely different technique.
- **Yield and portion size**: Makes about 9 cups, enough for about 28 cupcakes (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Heatproof stand mixer bowl, pan for a bain-marie, whisk and paddle attachments.
- **Ingredients**: 2.5 cups sugar, 10 large egg whites, 4 cups (8 sticks/2 lb) unsalted butter (cut into pieces), 2 tsp pure vanilla extract.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Place sugar and egg whites in a heatproof mixer bowl set over a pan of gently simmering water. Whisk until the sugar dissolves and the whites are hot to the touch, about 3 minutes (test by rubbing between fingers - it should feel completely smooth, no sugar grit).
  2. Transfer to the mixer stand; whip on high with the whisk attachment until cooled completely and forming stiff, glossy peaks, about 10 minutes.
  3. Add butter one piece at a time, beating until incorporated after each addition. (Don't worry if it looks curdled partway through - it smooths out with continued beating.)
  4. Add vanilla; beat just to combine.
  5. Switch to the paddle attachment; beat on the lowest speed 5 minutes to eliminate air pockets.
  6. Use within a few hours at cool room temperature, or refrigerate airtight up to 3 days. Bring to room temperature and rebeat on low with the paddle before using.
- **Food safety controls**: Heating the egg whites over simmering water to a hot, sugar-dissolved state pasteurizes them adequately for this technique.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, silky texture (not curdled or soupy - both are normal transitional stages that resolve with continued beating, per the source's own note), stiff glossy meringue before butter is added.
- **Holding/storage**: Room temperature a few hours, or refrigerate up to 3 days; rebeat before using.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly from the 9-cup yield.
- **Skill focus**: Cooking egg whites over a bain-marie to a safe, sugar-dissolved state, recognizing and working through the "curdled" transitional stage of buttercream-making without panicking and starting over.

### Italian Meringue Buttercream

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - professional-scale cooked-sugar buttercream.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Italian_Meringue_Buttercream__1_.pdf` (source: professional textbook). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 18.25 lb (already at large production scale).
- **Equipment**: Saucepan, candy thermometer, stand mixer with whisk attachment.
- **Ingredients**: 6 lb granulated sugar (divided), 1 lb 8 oz water, 3 lb egg whites, 7 lb unsalted butter (diced, room temperature), 0.5 oz vanilla extract, 0.25 oz fine salt.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Combine water and 4 lb of the sugar; cook to 250°F.
  2. While the syrup cooks, whip egg whites with the remaining sugar to stiff peaks.
  3. When the syrup reaches 250°F, remove from heat and pour slowly into the stiff-peak meringue while the mixer runs at medium speed.
  4. Whip on high to stiff peaks, until the bowl is cooler but still warm.
  5. Add room-temperature butter to the warm meringue with the mixer running on medium.
  6. Add vanilla and salt.
  7. Whip until it forms a buttercream.
  8. Ready to use immediately, though it contains less air if used the following day, per the source's own note.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling; the hot sugar syrup poured into the whites cooks them to a safe temperature (the Italian meringue method).
- **Allergens**: Dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Stiff, glossy meringue before butter is added; smooth, stable buttercream after.
- **Holding/storage**: Best used the same day for maximum airiness; can be held and rebeaten the next day.
- **Scaling notes**: Already at large production scale (18.25 lb); scale down proportionally for smaller batches.
- **Skill focus**: Italian meringue technique (hot syrup poured into whipping whites, rather than heated together as in the Swiss method above) - a direct technique comparison against Swiss Meringue Buttercream.

### Parisian Macarons (Italian Meringue Method)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, Baking & Pastry unit - advanced piped cookie technique.
- **Source and revision date**: `Baking & Pastry/Parisian Macarons--Italian Meringue.pdf` (source: CIA). Standardized 2026-07. Kept separate from the Macaron (French Macaroon) entry in the Cookies section above - this uses the Italian meringue method (a cooked sugar syrup whipped into the whites), a more stable and professional technique than that entry's French meringue (raw whipped whites) method.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 1.5 full sheet pans, about 50 sets (100 shells).
- **Equipment**: Food processor (Robot Coupe or similar), sifter, 5-quart stand mixer with paddle, saucepan with candy thermometer, pastry bag with a #803 round tip.
- **Ingredients**: 87.5 g egg whites #1 (fresh, aged a day if possible, room temp), 250 g almond flour, 250 g confectioners' sugar, 90 g egg whites #2 (fresh, room temp), 230 g granulated sugar (divided), 100 g water, powdered water-soluble food coloring as needed.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Process almond flour and confectioners' sugar together; sift.
  2. Place the sifted mixture in a stand mixer bowl with the paddle attachment; add egg whites #1. Mix until combined.
  3. Make an Italian meringue with egg whites #2, sugar, and water - cook the sugar syrup and remove from heat at 245°F, pouring into the whipping whites.
  4. Add food coloring at this point, going one shade darker than desired (it will fade) - don't overdo it.
  5. Whip until cool.
  6. Paddle 1/4 of the cooled meringue into the almond paste to soften it, about a minute.
  7. Fold in the remainder of the meringue by hand, until the mixture is a shiny mass that makes ribbons when it falls from the bowl scraper, dissipating within 15 seconds (the classic macaronage consistency test).
  8. Pipe with a #803 tip; let dry 45 minutes or longer (longer on humid/rainy days) - properly dried shells shouldn't be tacky at all and shouldn't break when touched.
  9. Bake in a convection oven at 280°F for 7 minutes on high fan, rotating pans; then 7 more minutes on low fan. Check and bake in 1-minute increments as needed. Cool and fill (or wrap and freeze unfilled).
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling; the Italian meringue method cooks the egg whites via the hot syrup.
- **Allergens**: Egg, tree nut (almond).
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, shiny macaronage batter that ribbons and levels within 15 seconds (under- or over-mixed batter fails this test), a proper "foot" after baking, dry (non-tacky) shell surface before baking.
- **Holding/storage**: Cool completely before filling; unfilled shells wrap and freeze well.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Italian meringue macaronage (a more stable, professional-standard technique than French meringue macarons), macaronage consistency testing by ribbon/dissipation time, humidity-dependent drying judgment, convection fan-speed adjustment mid-bake.

### Blueberry Eggnog Muffins

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - seasonal muffin technique.
- **Source and revision date**: Adapted from Sally's Baking Addiction, per Chef Margaret Foret's collection. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 1 dozen muffins per batch; scales to 10 dozen per the source's own table.
- **Equipment**: Mixer with paddle, 12-cavity muffin pan, paper liners, portion scoop.
- **Ingredients (1 dozen)**: 4 oz butter, 7 oz sugar, 2 eggs, 2 tsp baking powder, 1/2 tsp kosher salt, 1 tsp vanilla, 8.5 oz AP flour, 2 oz whole milk, 2 oz eggnog, 1/4 tsp eggnog extract, 1 pint blueberries. Streusel: 3.75 oz brown sugar, 3.5 oz sugar, 2.1 oz AP flour, 1.5 tsp cinnamon, 2 oz butter (cubed 1/4").
- **Procedure**:
  1. Gather and scale all ingredients. Preheat oven to 375°F.
  2. Cream butter and sugar until completely smooth, scraping the bowl as needed.
  3. Add eggs one at a time, scraping the bowl between additions.
  4. Add baking powder, salt, vanilla, and eggnog extract; beat just until combined.
  5. Add the flour in alternating increments with the milk/eggnog; scrape the bowl.
  6. Fold in washed, dried blueberries by hand - do not use the mixer for this step (to avoid crushing/bruising the berries and streaking the batter purple).
  7. Portion into a paper-lined muffin pan with a scoop.
  8. Top with streusel (combine dry streusel ingredients, then rub the butter through until crumbly).
  9. Bake 20-25 minutes, until a toothpick comes out clean.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Blueberries whole and evenly distributed (not streaked through the batter), crumbly streusel top, clean toothpick test.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight; best within a couple of days.
- **Scaling notes**: Source gives an exact 1-dozen to 10-dozen scale-up table.
- **Skill focus**: Hand-folding delicate mix-ins to avoid bruising/streaking, a seasonal (eggnog) twist on a standard muffin base, streusel topping technique.

### Lemon Poppy Seed Loaf

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, Baking & Pastry unit - citrus quick bread.
- **Source and revision date**: Adapted from Sally's Baking Addiction, per Chef Margaret Foret's collection. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Yield 1 loaf per batch; scales to 15 loaves per the source's own table.
- **Equipment**: Mixing bowls, whisk, loaf pan(s), foil (for shielding during baking).
- **Ingredients (1 loaf)**: 8.5 oz AP flour, 1 Tbsp + 1 tsp poppy seeds, 1 tsp baking soda, 1/2 tsp baking powder, 1/4 tsp kosher salt, 1 egg, 5.25 oz sugar, 2.67 oz canola oil (measure by volume, not weight, per the source's own note), 2.8 oz sour cream, 5.3 oz whole milk, 1.5 oz lemon juice, 1 Tbsp lemon zest. Glaze: 2 oz powdered sugar (sifted), 1/2 oz lemon juice.
- **Procedure**:
  1. Gather and scale all ingredients. Preheat oven to 325°F.
  2. Combine flour, poppy seeds, baking soda, baking powder, and salt in a large bowl.
  3. Whisk egg and sugar together in a separate bowl.
  4. Whisk oil, milk, sour cream, lemon juice, and lemon zest into the egg/sugar mixture.
  5. Add the liquid ingredients to the dry; stir just until combined - some small lumps are fine, do not overmix.
  6. Portion into prepared loaf pan(s); bake 35-40 minutes, until a toothpick comes out clean. Cover with foil halfway through baking to prevent over-browning.
  7. Cool before glazing.
  8. For the glaze: whisk powdered sugar and lemon juice together until smooth; drizzle over the cooled loaf.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg/dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, dairy, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Moist, tender crumb with small lumps okay in the raw batter (a sign it wasn't overmixed), evenly browned top under the foil shield.
- **Holding/storage**: Store airtight at room temperature.
- **Scaling notes**: Source gives an exact 1-loaf to 15-loaf scale-up table. Chef's reference weight: one loaf of batter weighs 1 lb 3 oz before baking - use this to portion accurately.
- **Skill focus**: Oil-based quick bread technique (measured by volume specifically, per the source's flag), foil-shielding to control browning, working from a precisely pre-scaled recipe table.
