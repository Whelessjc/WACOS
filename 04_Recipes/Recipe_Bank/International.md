# International

This category draws heavily on `19 - International Cuisine/International Cuisine/Global Foods/`, a set of five "DOCT3600 Global Cultures and Cuisines" teaching packets (China, Japan, Vietnam, Middle East, India), each structured as multiple-choice "Options" for a given country/region (a teacher assigns one option per class session, not all of them at once) plus alternate/extra-credit dishes. Given the volume (~60 dishes across the five packets), entries below are written more concisely than in earlier categories while still covering every required field - the source recipes themselves are terse, professional-shorthand formulas, so this matches their own register rather than padding them out. Deduplication follows the same rule as elsewhere: dishes appearing in multiple packets (e.g., jasmine rice showing up twice in the Vietnam packet) are condensed once.

### Mu Shu Pork with Mandarin Pancakes

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Beijing cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 1).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet, adapted from cited references (Heyman, *International Cooking*; Hom, *The Taste of China*; Simonds, *Classic Chinese Cuisine*). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; scale to class size.
- **Equipment**: Wok, mixing bowls.
- **Ingredients**: Sauce: 3.5 tsp sugar, 3/4 tsp salt, 1.5 tsp rice wine, 2 Tbsp dark soy, 1 Tbsp cornstarch, 2 Tbsp chicken stock, 2 Tbsp hoisin. Filling: 3 cups peanut oil (for blanching), 1 cup pork (julienne), 1 tsp minced ginger, 1 tsp minced garlic, 4 cups finely shredded cabbage, 3 scallions (1.5" lengths), 1/2 cup bamboo shoots (julienne), 2 Tbsp cloud ear mushrooms (soaked), 40 tiger lily buds (soaked, trimmed, halved lengthwise), 5 shiitake (julienne), 4 eggs (beaten), 1 Tbsp sesame oil. Pancakes: 1.75 cups high-gluten flour, 3/4 cup boiling water, 1/2 cup flour for dusting, 1.5 tsp sesame oil.
- **Procedure**: Combine sauce ingredients. Heat wok with oil; blanch pork until done, drain. Empty most of the oil; scramble eggs, remove. Stir-fry ginger/garlic (don't burn); add cabbage, cook until soft/browning. Add scallions, bamboo shoots, lily buds, mushrooms; cook briefly. Return pork and eggs; adjust seasoning. *Pancakes*: mix flour with boiling water, cool, knead 3 minutes, rest 30 minutes, divide into 12, roll thin, brush one side with sesame oil, stack two oiled-side together, dry-cook in the wok until blistered/browned on both sides, then peel apart into two pancakes each.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard pork/egg handling; confirm pork fully cooked before serving.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg, sesame, soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Cabbage soft with good color, pancakes pliable and separable, balanced savory-sweet sauce.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh; pancakes can be made ahead and reheated.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Wok stir-fry sequencing (blanch, scramble, aromatics, vegetables, combine), Mandarin pancake dough and double-pancake steaming trick.

### Lion's Head Hot Pot (See Ji Tau)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Shanghai cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 2).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: 8 meatballs (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Clay pot, fire pot, or sand pot; skillet.
- **Ingredients**: 1 lb ground pork, 6 scallions (white only, greens reserved), 3 Tbsp flour, 1 egg white (beaten), 1 Tbsp Shaoxing wine, 1 tsp minced ginger, 2 tsp soy, 2 tsp sugar, 1 tsp sesame oil, 3/4 tsp salt, pinch of pepper, 2 Tbsp peanut oil, 8 Shanghai bok choy (washed, thinly sliced), 3 cups chicken stock.
- **Procedure**: Combine pork, scallion whites, flour, egg white, wine, ginger, soy, sugar, sesame oil, salt, and pepper; stir in one direction until incorporated. Form 8 meatballs. Brown in peanut oil until cooked through. Place bok choy in a clay pot; pour in stock. Top with meatballs; bring to a boil. Simmer 25 minutes, stirring occasionally, until bok choy is tender and flavor develops. Adjust seasoning; present in the pot with individual serving bowls.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard pork/egg handling; confirm meatballs are fully cooked through.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg, soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Tender, well-seasoned meatballs; broth developed but not over-reduced.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh, served hot in the pot.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by meatball count.
- **Skill focus**: One-directional stirring for meatball texture (a classical Chinese technique for binding), hot-pot presentation.

### Bamboo Shoots with Bean Curd and Broccoli

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Shanghai cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 2).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Wok.
- **Ingredients**: 1.5 Tbsp peanut oil, 2 tsp minced ginger, 1/4 cup minced shallot, 1/3 cup thinly sliced bamboo shoots, 12 oz broccoli florets, 1 Tbsp rice wine, 1/2" piece hair seaweed (or substitute), 1/2 cup chicken or vegetable stock, 1/4 cup water, 1/4 tsp salt, 2 tsp sugar, 1 Tbsp oyster sauce, 2 tsp mushroom soy, 1/2 lb bean curd (1/2" dice), cornstarch slurry (1 Tbsp cornstarch + 2 Tbsp water).
- **Procedure**: Heat oil in a wok; cook ginger and shallot 1 minute. Add bamboo shoots and broccoli; cook 1 minute. Add wine and seaweed; cook 1 minute. Add stock, then salt, oyster sauce, sugar, and soy; cook until broccoli is tender. Add bean curd; warm through. Add slurry until thickened. Adjust seasoning and consistency.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard produce handling.
- **Allergens**: Soy, gluten (cornstarch generally gluten-free, but confirm mushroom soy/oyster sauce for gluten content).
- **Quality indicators**: Broccoli tender-crisp, sauce glossy and clinging (not watery), bean curd intact (not broken up).
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Building a stir-fry sauce in stages, handling bean curd gently to keep it intact.

### Dry-Fried Green Beans (Gan Bian Si Ji Dou)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Sichuan cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 3).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Wok.
- **Ingredients**: 10 oz green beans (trimmed), 1/4 cup (1 oz) ground pork, 2 Tbsp Shaoxing wine, 2 Tbsp Tianjin/ya cai (Sichuan preserved vegetable, finely diced), soy sauce to taste, 1 tsp sesame oil, 3 Tbsp peanut or vegetable oil.
- **Procedure**: Cut beans into 2" pieces. Heat 2 Tbsp oil in a wok; stir-fry beans until tender with wrinkled, browned skins. Remove. Heat remaining oil; stir-fry pork briefly until done. Add soy and wine; cook down briefly. Add preserved vegetables; cook briefly. Return beans to the wok to heat through. Adjust seasoning; finish with sesame oil.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard pork handling.
- **Allergens**: Soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Beans wrinkled and lightly charred (the hallmark of this technique - "dry-frying" means cooking until the surface blisters, not truly dry), pork fully cooked.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Dry-frying technique (high-heat stir-fry until vegetable skins blister and wrinkle), building a small amount of pork into a vegetable-forward dish as a flavor base.

### Mapo Tofu (Ma Po Dau Fu)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Sichuan cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 3).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07. **Variation noted**: a loose file (`Mapo Tofu.docx`) gives a minor-variance version of this same dish - same core technique (blanched tofu gently folded into a stir-fried chili-bean-and-black-bean pork/beef sauce, thickened with cornstarch) but adds Shaoxing rice wine and more garlic/ginger detail, and omits the sugar and peanut garnish - condensed here as a variation rather than a separate entry.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; serve with rice.
- **Equipment**: Wok.
- **Ingredients**: 1 lb bean curd, 5 scallions (long bias slices), 6 oz ground beef or pork, 4 Tbsp peanut oil, 2.5 Tbsp Sichuan chili-bean paste, 1 Tbsp fermented black beans, ground Sichuan chilies to taste, 1 cup stock, 1 tsp sugar, soy sauce to taste, cornstarch slurry as needed, 1/2 tsp roasted ground Sichuan peppercorn, 3 Tbsp roasted chopped peanuts (optional). **Variation ingredients**: 1 tsp salt (for blanching), 2 tsp minced ginger, 4 Tbsp Sichuan chili bean paste, 2 Tbsp fermented black beans (rinsed and chopped), 2 Tbsp ground chili (or to taste), 2 Tbsp minced garlic, 2 Tbsp Shaoxing rice wine, 4 Tbsp cornstarch mixed with 6 Tbsp water, 1 tsp ground Sichuan pepper, 2 stalks scallions.
- **Procedure**: Cut bean curd into 1" squares; soak or gently simmer in salted water 10 minutes. Heat peanut oil in a wok to smoke point; stir-fry ground meat until browned. Add chili-bean paste, ground chilies, and fermented black beans. Add stock and drained bean curd; stir gently - do not break the bean curd. Add sugar and soy; simmer briefly. Adjust seasoning; add scallions. Thicken with cornstarch slurry. Serve garnished with Sichuan peppercorn and peanuts, with a generous amount of sauce (add more stock if it looks dry). **Variation procedure**: cut tofu into 1" cubes, gently slide into salted simmering water 1 minute, drain, and set aside. Heat an empty wok until hot, add oil, then the minced meat and ginger; stir-fry over high heat until the meat is pale. Add the chili bean paste, black beans, chili powder, and garlic; fry until fragrant. Add the Shaoxing wine and stock; bring to a boil, then gently slide in the tofu and boil uncovered about 5 minutes. Reduce to medium heat, add half the cornstarch slurry, simmer 10 seconds, then add the remaining half. Once the sauce coats the back of a spoon, add the ground Sichuan pepper and scallions; mix gently and remove from heat immediately. Serve hot with steamed rice.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard meat handling; confirm meat fully cooked.
- **Allergens**: Soy, peanut (if using garnish).
- **Quality indicators**: Bean curd intact (not mashed), numbing-spicy balance from the peppercorn and chili paste, ample glossy sauce.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Gentle handling of soft bean curd in a sauce, balancing the numbing (má) and spicy (là) elements characteristic of Sichuan cuisine.

### Fan (Steamed Rice)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - foundational technique (DOCT3600 China, Option 3; also used across the Japan and Vietnam packets in this file under their own names).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Pot with lid, colander.
- **Ingredients**: 2 cups long grain rice, 15 oz water (plus rinsing water).
- **Procedure**: Cover rice with cold water in a pot, mix with hands, drain; repeat two more times. Let rice sit in a colander 1 hour at room temperature. Combine rice and water in a pot; bring to a boil, stirring with chopsticks. Cook 4-6 minutes, until water is nearly absorbed. Lower heat, cover, and cook 8 minutes more, stirring occasionally. Remove from heat and stir; cover until needed.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard handling.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Fluffy, separate grains, fully cooked without scorching.
- **Holding/storage**: Hold covered; reheat with a splash of water.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Multiple-rinse rice-washing technique, resting before cooking, low-and-slow finish for even absorption.

### Shrimp Cantonese (Guangzhou Chau Lung Har)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Cantonese cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Option 4).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07. **Note**: the source's procedure refers to "lobster meat," but the ingredient list specifies shrimp - presented using shrimp throughout, per the ingredient list and dish name, rather than guessing which is correct.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; serve with rice.
- **Equipment**: Wok.
- **Ingredients**: 1.75 tsp soy, 1.5 tsp oyster sauce, 3/4 tsp salt, pinch of pepper, 1.75 tsp sugar, 2 tsp sesame oil, 5 oz shrimp, 1/4 lb ground pork, 1/4 cup peanut or vegetable oil, 2 tsp minced garlic, 3 eggs (beaten), 2 tsp minced ginger, 2 tsp rice wine, 2 scallions (fine dice), 1 cucumber (for garnish).
- **Procedure**: Mix soy, oyster sauce, salt, pepper, sugar, and sesame oil in a bowl. Add shrimp; marinate 30 minutes, then strain and reserve the marinade. Heat 1 Tbsp oil in a wok; stir-fry pork until cooked, remove. Add eggs, scramble, remove. Add remaining oil; cook ginger, scallion, and garlic briefly. Add shrimp; cook 2 minutes. Add rice wine and the reserved pork/egg mixture; combine. Add the reserved marinade; reduce slightly. Adjust consistency and seasoning. Garnish with cucumber; serve with rice.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard shellfish/egg/pork handling; discard raw marinade unless boiled with the dish (it is, per the procedure).
- **Allergens**: Shellfish, egg, soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Shrimp just cooked through (not rubbery), well-balanced savory sauce.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Staged stir-fry component cooking (protein, egg, aromatics cooked separately then combined), marinade reduction as a sauce base.

### Red-Cooked Bean Curd (Hong Shao Dou Fu)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Eastern Chinese cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Supplemental).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet, source: *The Modern Art of Chinese Cooking* (pp. 187-189). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; serve with rice.
- **Equipment**: Wok, paper towels (for pressing tofu).
- **Ingredients**: 1 lb bean curd, 1 oz pork loin or butt, Shaoxing wine to taste, soy sauce to taste, sesame oil to taste, 1 clove garlic (crushed), pinch of cornstarch, 4 dried Chinese black mushrooms, oil as needed, 2 scallions (1" pieces), 1 small carrot (1/2" slices or half-moons), 8 snow peas (strings removed, cut in thirds on the bias), 8 oz chicken stock, 1 Tbsp soy sauce, 1 tsp Shaoxing wine, 1/4 tsp sugar, salt and pepper to taste, cornstarch slurry as needed.
- **Procedure**: Press bean curd between paper towels 10 minutes; cut into large dice. Soak mushrooms in hot chicken stock 15 minutes; quarter, reserving the soaking stock. Slice pork thin; toss lightly with soy, wine, sesame oil, and garlic; marinate 10 minutes. Brown tofu in batches in hot oil; season and drain. Stir-fry carrots 2 minutes; add pork, scallions, and mushrooms; cook 30 seconds more, then reduce heat. Add the reserved stock, soy, wine, and sugar; simmer. Add tofu; slow-cook 10 minutes. Add snow peas; cook 1 minute. Thicken with slurry; adjust seasoning. Don't over-reduce the sauce - add more stock as needed.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard pork handling.
- **Allergens**: Soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Tofu browned but intact, ample sauce, vegetables tender-crisp.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by weight.
- **Skill focus**: Pressing and browning tofu before braising, staged aromatic/vegetable additions, mushroom-soaking-liquid reuse.

### Pearl Balls (Steamed Rice-Coated Pork Meatballs)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Hubei cuisine (DOCT3600 China, Supplemental).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet, source: *The Modern Art of Chinese Cooking* (pp. 187-189). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source.
- **Equipment**: Food processor, steaming rack, large pot.
- **Ingredients**: 1 1/8 cups sweet glutinous rice, 4 dried Chinese black mushrooms, 7 water chestnuts, 1 tsp ginger, 2 scallions, 3/4 lb ground pork, 1 large egg, 1 Tbsp soy sauce, 1.5 tsp kosher salt, 1/2 cup chicken stock.
- **Procedure**: Soak rice in 4 cups water at least 1 hour; rinse until clear and drain (keep moist so it adheres to the meat). Soak mushrooms in hot water 20 minutes; trim stems and mince. Mince ginger and scallions; combine with pork, egg, soy, salt, and stock in a food processor - purée without over-processing. Fold in the chopped mushrooms and water chestnuts. Spread rice in a thick layer in a pan. Scoop 2 Tbsp of the meat mixture, form a ball, and roll in the rice to coat. Repeat. Place balls on an oiled steaming rack; steam over boiling water 1.5-2 hours, replenishing water as needed. Serve immediately.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard pork/egg handling; confirm full steam time for safe internal temperature.
- **Allergens**: Egg, soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Rice fully translucent and cooked ("pearl" appearance), meatball moist and fully cooked through.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh, served hot.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by ball count.
- **Skill focus**: Rice-coating technique for a decorative steamed dumpling, long gentle steaming for a delicate rice coating.

### Shrimp Toast (Har Dor See)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Cantonese appetizer (DOCT3600 China, Supplemental).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source; 5 slices of bread as the base quantity.
- **Equipment**: Food processor, deep fryer or wok.
- **Ingredients**: 1/2 lb shrimp (peeled, deveined), 2 tsp lard or vegetable shortening, 4 water chestnuts, 1/2 tsp cornstarch, 2 scallions (chopped), 1 tsp minced ginger, 1 tsp rice wine, 1 egg white (lightly beaten), salt and pepper to taste, 5 slices white bread (1/2" thick).
- **Procedure**: Cut crusts off bread; toast in a 250°F oven until semi-dry. Cut into triangles, ovals, or circles. Pulse lard and shrimp in a food processor a few times. Add water chestnuts, scallion, and ginger; process 10 seconds. Add rice wine, egg white, and cornstarch; process until smooth and thick (paste texture). Spread the mixture on each toast point. Deep-fry at 350°F, shrimp side down, 45 seconds or until brown; flip and brown the other side 45 seconds. Drain, season, and serve. *(Optional: press sesame seeds onto the toast before frying. Don't over-dry the bread or it will crumble when cut.)*
- **Food safety controls**: Standard shellfish/egg handling; confirm shrimp paste is fully cooked through frying.
- **Allergens**: Shellfish, gluten, egg, sesame (if using the optional garnish).
- **Quality indicators**: Crisp bread base, shrimp paste cooked through and adhered well (not falling off during frying).
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh and hot.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by bread-slice count.
- **Skill focus**: Building a shrimp paste ("toast" style), frying paste-topped bread without the topping detaching.

### Chicken & Shiitake Stir-Fry

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - general stir-fry technique. A loose single-recipe find (not part of the DOCT3600 packet), grouped here with China given the Cantonese/general Chinese stir-fry technique and ingredient set.
- **Source and revision date**: rouxbe.com, "How to Stir-Fry" course, Task 7 activity, by Dawn T. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 2-4 (source yield); below the program's 16-30 target but above the 1-8 range this project scales up, so left as written given it's a technique-demonstration recipe rather than a batch-production one.
- **Equipment**: Wok.
- **Ingredients**: Chicken: 8 oz chicken breast, 1.5 tsp cornstarch, 1/2 tsp Shao Hsing rice wine (or dry sherry), 1/2 tsp soy sauce, 1/2 tsp sesame oil. Mise en place: 1 cup carrots, 1 cup onions, 1 cup snap peas, 1 cup shiitake mushrooms, 1/2 cup green onions, 1.5 tsp garlic (about 2 cloves), 1.5 tsp ginger (about 1/2" piece). Sauce: 2 Tbsp oyster sauce, 2 Tbsp Shao Hsing rice wine (or dry sherry), 2 Tbsp soy sauce, 1 Tbsp sambal oelek. For frying: 1-2 Tbsp peanut oil.
- **Procedure**: Slice the chicken across the grain into 1/4"-thick, bite-sized pieces. Toss with the cornstarch, rice wine, and soy sauce until the cornstarch dissolves, then stir in the sesame oil; set aside. For the mise en place, slice the onions; slice the mushrooms 1/4" thick; peel and slice the carrots on the bias; trim and string the snap peas; thin-slice the green onions on the bias; mince the garlic and ginger. Mix the sauce ingredients together and set aside. Line up all ingredients in cooking order. Heat the wok over high heat with the peanut oil. Stir-fry the chicken, then remove. Stir-fry the onions, then the mushrooms, then the carrots, then the snap peas, adding each in sequence so everything finishes cooking at the same time. Add the garlic and ginger. Return the chicken and its juices to the wok; add the sauce. Finish with the green onions. Add a touch more oil during stir-frying if needed. Serve immediately.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard poultry handling; confirm chicken is fully cooked through before serving.
- **Allergens**: Soy, sesame, shellfish (oyster sauce).
- **Quality indicators**: Vegetables tender-crisp and staggered by cook time (not all mushy or all raw), chicken velveted and tender (not tough, from the cornstarch-marinade coating), glossy sauce clinging to everything.
- **Holding/storage**: Best fresh, straight from the wok - a stir-fry does not hold or reheat well.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 2-4 servings is left as written, as this is a single-portion technique demonstration; scale by simply repeating the process in batches (a wok stir-fry doesn't scale well by just multiplying quantities in one pan) if a larger batch is needed.
- **Skill focus**: Sequencing ingredients into a wok by cook time (protein first, then vegetables from longest- to shortest-cooking, aromatics near the end, sauce last), a light cornstarch-marinade "velveting" technique for tender chicken.

### Spicy Chinese Noodles

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - Sichuan-style noodle technique (dan dan-adjacent, quick pork and bean-paste noodle dish).
- **Source and revision date**: Ruth Reichl, *Save Me the Plums* (p. 57). Scan was OCR-garbled in places; procedure reconstructed carefully from the legible portions rather than guessed. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 2 (source yield, described in the source as "a perfect snack for two"); below the program's 16-30 target but left as written, consistent with how this bank treats small technique/snack-style finds.
- **Equipment**: Wok, pot for noodles.
- **Ingredients**: 1/2 lb Chinese egg noodles (dried spaghetti may substitute), peanut oil, 1/2" fresh ginger (minced, about 2 Tbsp), 2 scallions (whites and greens separated, sliced), 1 tsp sugar, 2 Tbsp Chinese black bean paste with garlic, 1 Tbsp Chinese bean paste with chili, 1/2 lb ground pork, sesame oil.
- **Procedure**: Cook the noodles al dente, drain, toss with 1/2 Tbsp peanut oil, and set aside. Mix the sugar with both bean pastes and set aside. Heat a wok until a drop of water skitters across the surface; add 1 Tbsp peanut oil and the ginger, stir-frying about 30 seconds until fragrant. Add the ground pork and scallion whites; stir-fry until no pink remains. Add the bean sauce mixture; cook, stirring, about 2 minutes. Stir in the scallion greens and noodles, tossing quickly to combine. Finish with a drop of sesame oil.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard ground pork handling; confirm pork is fully cooked through before adding remaining ingredients.
- **Allergens**: Gluten (noodles, bean pastes), soy, sesame.
- **Quality indicators**: Noodles well-coated but not swimming in sauce, pork fully cooked with no pink, balanced salty-spicy-sweet flavor from the bean pastes and sugar.
- **Holding/storage**: Best served immediately; a quick wok dish, not intended to hold or reheat.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 2 servings is left as written; scale by repeating the process in batches rather than multiplying in one wok.
- **Skill focus**: Building a quick sauce from combined bean pastes rather than a from-scratch sauce, wok sequencing (aromatic, then protein, then sauce, then noodles).

### Ichiban Dashi (Primary Fish/Seaweed Stock)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - foundational Japanese stock (DOCT3600 Japan, Option 1).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet, adapted from cited references (Heyman, *International Cooking*; Shimbo, *The Japanese Kitchen*; Tsuji, *Japanese Cooking: A Simple Art*). Standardized 2026-07. **Variation noted**: a cookbook excerpt (`dashi.pdf`, "Soups" chapter, p. 149) gives a minor-variance version of this same stock using three 2"x3" kombu pieces and 1 cup (1 oz) bonito flakes per quart, simmering the kombu longer (20-30 minutes, to a bare simmer) and settling the bonito for less time (about 5 minutes) - condensed here as a variation rather than a separate entry.
- **Yield and portion size**: 1 quart (source yield).
- **Equipment**: Pot, strainer, fine-mesh sieve or coffee-filter-lined colander.
- **Ingredients**: 1 qt water, 1/2 oz kombu (dried kelp), 1/2 oz katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes).
- **Procedure**: Wipe kombu with a damp towel. Place in a pot with water; simmer on low 10 minutes. Remove kombu; add katsuobushi. Remove from heat; let the bonito settle 10 minutes. Strain. *(The used kombu/katsuobushi can be simmered longer for a second, more robust stock; dried shiitake can add extra flavor.)* **Cookbook variation**: bring water and kombu to a simmer, reduce to a bare simmer 20-30 minutes (skimming foam), then bring back up to a boil, remove from heat, remove kombu, add bonito flakes, and let sit until they absorb water and sink (about 5 minutes); strain through a fine-mesh sieve or coffee-filter-lined colander, discarding the bonito pulp.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard handling.
- **Allergens**: Fish.
- **Quality indicators**: Clean, delicate umami flavor; clear, pale color.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate (up to 5 days) or freeze (up to 1 month, including in ice-cube-tray portions).
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly; used aromatics can be reused for a "second dashi."
- **Skill focus**: Foundational Japanese stock technique - short, gentle extraction (never boiled) for a clean flavor. **Serving variations (from the cookbook version)**: season finished dashi with a dash of mirin and soy sauce; stir in 1-2 Tbsp miso off the heat for a simple soup or first course (miso ranges from white/sweeter/lighter to dark terra-cotta/deeper flavor - try white first); or add 1/2 cup thinly sliced fresh shiitake, 1 cup cubed tofu, 1/4 cup slivered scallions, and 1" julienned ginger for a more substantial soup.

### Miso Soup with Wakame and Tofu (Wakame To Tofu No Miso Shiru)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - Japanese soup technique (DOCT3600 Japan, Option 1).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07. **Variation noted**: a loose "Homemade Miso Soup" recipe (Justonecookbook.com) found elsewhere in the archive is the same dish with minor variance - it builds its own dashi from scratch (kombu simmered, then katsuobushi steeped and strained, rather than referencing this bank's separate Dashi entry) and uses a slightly different miso-to-dashi ratio (about 1 Tbsp miso per cup of dashi vs. this entry's 6 Tbsp per quart). Condensed into this one entry per the dedup rule rather than duplicated; see the dashi/miso ratio and reheating notes folded in below.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in program-packet source; the Justonecookbook version yields 4 servings from about 4 cups of dashi.
- **Equipment**: Saucepan; a fine-mesh strainer or miso muddler/strainer basket makes dissolving the miso easier.
- **Ingredients**: 1/4 oz dried wakame, 8 oz tofu (soft/silken is traditional), 1 qt dashi (above), 6 Tbsp miso (or to taste - about 1 Tbsp per cup of dashi is the Justonecookbook ratio), 3 scallions (minced). Optional garnish: lettuce, soybeans, cabbage, shiitake, bean sprouts, daikon, kabocha.
- **Procedure**: Soak wakame 15 minutes (in a separate bowl of water if trying to reduce its saltiness); cut into 1/2" pieces. Dice tofu (traditionally cut into 1/2" cubes on the palm of the hand, though a cutting board works just as well). Heat dashi to a simmer. Dissolve the miso separately - put it in a ladle or strainer, add a little hot dashi, and stir until fully dissolved before adding it back to the pot (this keeps lumps of miso from ending up in the soup and helps avoid breaking the tofu). Add the tofu after the miso is dissolved, along with the wakame, scallions, and any other garnish. Return to a simmer - do not boil (boiling dulls miso's flavor, aroma, and nutritional value). Add scallions right before serving to keep their color and fragrance.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard soy handling.
- **Allergens**: Soy.
- **Quality indicators**: Clear broth, miso fully dissolved without graininess, tofu intact.
- **Holding/storage**: Best consumed immediately - miso soup loses aroma and flavor as it sits. If holding, cool to room temperature within 4 hours and refrigerate up to 2 days; for a larger make-ahead batch, refrigerate the dashi without the miso and add miso fresh to each portion as needed. Reheat gently without boiling.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Adding miso without boiling (preserves its live-culture aroma and flavor), building a simple, adaptable garnish-forward soup, dissolving miso via a strainer/ladle technique to keep the broth smooth.

### Sushi Rice (Sushi Gohan)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - foundational Japanese rice technique (DOCT3600 Japan, Option 3).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source (3 cups rice as the base quantity).

- **Holding/storage**: Room temperature only, per the source's explicit note - never chill sushi rice (it turns hard and loses texture).
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Cut-and-fold rice-dressing technique (not stirred), fanning for shine and temperature control, proper sushi rice holding practice.

### Momofuku Ramen Broth

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Japanese-style multi-component broth, protein, and garnish assembly (Momofuku-style tonkotsu/chintan hybrid ramen).
- **Source and revision date**: foodnouveau.com, "How to Make Momofuku Ramen Broth" (based on David Chang's Momofuku method). Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 6 (source yield); within the program's target range for a component/assembly dish. Prep 30 min, cook about 5 hours.
- **Equipment**: Large stockpot, roasting pan (for bones), fine-mesh sieve, cheesecloth, small saucepans (for egg and pickled shiitake), airtight storage containers.
- **Ingredients**: **Broth**: 12 cups water, 1 piece (3"x6") kombu, 1 cup dried shiitake caps (not sliced), 2 lb bone-in chicken thighs/drumsticks, 2 lb meaty pork bones, 8 oz smoked bacon, 4 green onions (cut into 2" logs), 1 small onion (halved), 1 large carrot (peeled and chopped). **Seasoning**: 3 Tbsp shoyu, 3 Tbsp mirin, 1 Tbsp kosher salt. **Per serving**: 2 cups broth, 6 oz fresh ramen noodles (or 3 oz dried), 1/2 cup cooked meat, 1/4-1/2 cup blanched vegetables, 1 halved ramen egg, 1/4 cup sliced green onions, 2 (3" square) nori pieces; optional garnish: fish cake slices, bamboo shoots, sesame seeds, furikake. **Pickled shiitake**: 2 cups rehydrated shiitake (reserved from the broth, or 1 cup dried steeped 30 min), 1 cup ramen/chicken broth, 1/2 cup sugar, 1/2 cup shoyu, 1/2 cup sherry or rice vinegar, 1 (3") knob ginger (sliced). **Ramen egg**: 2 eggs, 1/4 cup water, 2 Tbsp shoyu, 2 Tbsp mirin.
- **Procedure**: **Broth**: Simmer the kombu in the water 10 minutes, then let steep, covered, off heat; remove kombu (reserve - see kombu-reuse note below). Add the rinsed dried shiitake, bring to a boil, then simmer 30 minutes; remove and reserve the shiitake for pickling. Add the chicken, simmer about 1 hour, skimming foam, until the meat pulls easily from the bone; remove, cool, and shred the meat, reserving it for serving. Meanwhile, roast the pork bones (skin off, fat on) at 400°F for 30 minutes until golden. Add the roasted bones and bacon to the broth; simmer, skimming foam, discarding the bacon after 45 minutes; simmer the pork bones 1 hour more. Add the green onion, onion, and carrot; simmer 1 hour more. Strain through a cheesecloth-lined fine-mesh sieve and return the broth to the pot. Whisk the seasoning ingredients together, stir half into the broth, taste, and adjust (the broth should taste "not quite too salty but almost," per the source). **Pickled shiitake**: Slice the rehydrated shiitake 1/4" thick; simmer with the broth, sugar, shoyu, vinegar, and ginger 30 minutes; cool in the liquid, discard the ginger, and jar, covering with the pickling liquid. **Ramen egg**: Boil the eggs exactly 7 minutes, then ice-bath 10 minutes and peel. Marinate in the shoyu/mirin/water mixture in a sealed bag, fully submerged, overnight. **Kombu-reuse salad (optional)**: thinly slice the steeped kombu; combine with drained, patted bamboo shoots (one 6.7 oz can), 1 Tbsp toasted sesame oil, 1 Tbsp rice vinegar, 1 minced garlic clove, 1 tsp shoyu, 1/2 tsp sugar, 1/2 tsp crushed red pepper, and a pinch of salt for a quick side salad. **Assembly**: place noodles in the bottom of each bowl, arrange meat and vegetables over the noodles, ladle hot broth over, top with a halved ramen egg and sliced green onions, tuck nori partway into one side of the bowl to stand up, add remaining toppings, and serve very hot.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard poultry and pork handling; confirm chicken and pork are fully cooked before shredding/serving; hold and reheat broth to a full simmer; marinating eggs must stay refrigerated and fully submerged.
- **Allergens**: Soy, gluten (noodles, shoyu), egg, fish (if using fish cake or bonito-based furikake), sesame (if using the kombu-reuse salad or sesame seed garnish).
- **Quality indicators**: Rich, clear-to-lightly-cloudy broth with layered pork/chicken/smoke flavor, tender shredded meat, jammy-centered ramen egg, noodles not overcooked/bloated in the hot broth.
- **Holding/storage**: Broth holds airtight in 2-cup portions, refrigerated up to 4 days or frozen 1 month or more (don't discard the flavorful fat cap on top; broth can be reduced by half to save freezer space, adding 1 cup water back per portion on reheat). Pickled shiitake keeps up to 1 month refrigerated. Ramen eggs should be used within 4 days.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 6 servings is within the program's target range; scales linearly by bone/meat/liquid ratio for a larger batch, though total simmer time will not shorten.
- **Skill focus**: Building a layered, multi-protein broth in stages (aromatics, then chicken, then roasted pork bones and bacon, then vegetables), skimming for clarity, tempering/seasoning a finished stock to taste rather than salting all at once, component-based bowl assembly (broth, protein, egg, and garnish built separately then combined to order).

### Pita Bread (Khubz Tupat) and Regional Flatbread Variations

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Middle Eastern flatbread technique (DOCT3600 Middle East, Option 1). Cross-reference note: distinct from the Pita Bread entry already standardized in `Breads.md` (a Western classroom recipe) - this is the program's own Middle-East-unit version with regional variation notes attached, kept separate given the different yeast/hydration ratio and the bundled regional variants.
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet. Standardized 2026-07. **Variation noted**: a second Pita Bread recipe (Recipe Component #1 of `Mediterranean_Part_1_ProSta.docx`, a loose file) is the same dish with minor variance - same technique (yeast dough, bulk rise, divided and rested, rolled into disks, baked at 500°F on an inverted sheet pan until coloring on the sides) at a very similar ratio, just given by weight (532g water, 920g bread flour, 12g salt, 21g vegetable oil, 14g sugar, 10g active dry yeast) rather than volume, and using vegetable oil instead of olive oil. Condensed into this one entry rather than duplicated.
- **Yield and portion size**: 16 rounds (source yield); the weight-based variant divides into 100g pieces.
- **Equipment**: Stand mixer with dough hook, sheet pans, 500°F-capable oven.
- **Ingredients**: 1 Tbsp dry yeast, 2.5 cups water (divided), 1/4 tsp sugar, 6 cups high-gluten flour (or a mix of high-gluten and whole wheat), 2 tsp salt, 3 Tbsp extra-virgin olive oil (divided). *(Weight-based variant: 532g water, 920g bread flour, 12g salt, 21g vegetable oil, 14g sugar, 10g active dry yeast.)*
- **Procedure**: Bloom yeast and sugar in 1/2 cup of the water (110°F). When frothy, add remaining water. Stir in 3 cups of flour; rest until bubbly. Coat with remaining oil; rise 30 minutes. Divide into 16 pieces; roll into 8" rounds, 1/8-1/4" thick. Stack and keep covered with a cloth until needed. *(Weight-based variant: bloom yeast and sugar in warm water; combine flour and salt; stir in the yeast mixture and knead, adding the oil gradually as the dough comes together, adding a little more flour if needed so the dough isn't sticky. Bulk rise, oiled and covered, about 2 hours until doubled. Divide into 100g pieces, rest 20 minutes, form into 1/4"-thick disks, and proof 20 minutes more. Bake on a turned-over sheet pan in a 500°F oven about 3 minutes, until the bread begins to color on the sides.)*
- **Food safety controls**: Standard yeast/gluten handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten.
- **Quality indicators**: Good puff (steam pocket), light browning, soft texture under the cloth cover.
- **Holding/storage**: Keep covered with a cloth to stay soft; freezes well.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: High-heat flatbread puffing technique, and adapting one base dough across regional variations: **Iranian Naan-e Lavash** (roll very thin, prick all over, cook on a hot dry griddle, pressing to prevent rising - a flat, cracker-like result), **Khubz Sarj** (roll as thin as possible with no holes, cook on an inverted wok until bubbles appear), **Gulf States Mafrooda** (same as Khubz Sarj but pricked all over to prevent rising, like the Persian version).

### Falafel (Raw Chickpea Method)

### Gefilte Fish (Ashkenazic Fish Quenelles)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Ashkenazi Jewish/Kosher technique (DOCT3600 Middle East packet, Kosher section).
- **Source and revision date**: Program packet, source: *Encyclopedia of Jewish Food*, Gil Marks, 2010. Standardized 2026-07. Cultural/historical context condensed from the source rather than reproduced at length, per copyright practice - see note below.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source as a count.
- **Equipment**: Food processor, large stockpot.
- **Ingredients**: Fish patties: 1.5 lb fish fillets (white fish, carp, or yellow pike), 2 medium yellow onions, 1/2 cup iced water, 2 large eggs (beaten), 3 Tbsp sugar, 1.5 tsp kosher salt, 1/2 tsp ground pepper, 1/8 cup matzo meal. Fish broth: 1.5 lb fish bones/heads/tails, 2 medium carrots (sliced), 1 stalk celery (sliced), 1 medium onion (sliced), 1 qt cold water, 2 bay leaves, 1 Tbsp kosher salt, 1/4 tsp white peppercorns, 2 Tbsp sugar (optional).
- **Procedure**: Pulse fish fillet and onion in a food processor to a fine grind, not a purée; gradually add water (more for a softer texture, less for firmer). - **Quality indicators**: Firm but tender quenelle texture (not mushy from over-processing, not tough from too much matzo meal or over-processing the fish).
- **Holding/storage**: Cool and store in the broth, refrigerated; serve with carrot and horseradish, per the source's own serving note.
- **Scaling notes**: Scales by fish weight.
- **Skill focus**: Fine-grinding fish without turning it to paste, poaching quenelles gently in a flavored broth, balancing sweet vs. savory seasoning (**a noted regional distinction**: recipes made sweeter, with sugar, are associated with Galitzianer Jewish communities, while pepper-forward, less-sweet versions are associated with Litvak communities - a well-known enough culinary/cultural distinction that the source calls it "the Gefilte Fish Line"). ### Charred Sweet Potatoes with Toum

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Levantine garlic-emulsion technique (toum) paired with a roasted vegetable.
- **Source and revision date**: bonappetit.com, "Charred Sweet Potatoes with Toum." Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 4 (source yield); below the program's 16-30 target but left as written, consistent with this bank's treatment of other single-recipe technique finds.
- **Equipment**: Blender, large heavy (cast-iron preferred) skillet, oven.
- **Ingredients**: 1 cup grapeseed or vegetable oil, 1/3 cup garlic cloves (6-8), 2 Tbsp fresh lemon juice, kosher salt, 8 small sweet potatoes (about 1.5 lb, scrubbed), 4 Tbsp unsalted butter (divided), 2 Tbsp honey, 2 tsp nigella seeds (optional; found at Middle Eastern or Indian markets), flaky sea salt.
- **Procedure**: Chill the oil in the freezer 30 minutes. Pulse the garlic in a blender with the lemon juice, 1/4 cup of the cold oil, and 1 Tbsp ice water until smooth. With the motor running, very gradually stream in 1/2 cup of the oil; scrape down the sides, then slowly add the remaining 1/4 cup oil until the mixture is slightly thick and clings to a spoon. Add 1 more Tbsp ice water, season with salt, and pulse again (the whole process takes 8-10 minutes - take your time). Transfer the toum to a bowl. Preheat the oven to 400°F, rack in the bottom third. Place the sweet potatoes in the large skillet, arranged so they cover only about half the pan; add water to barely coat the bottom; cover tightly with foil and bake 30-35 minutes until tender. Cool on a cutting board, reserving the skillet; slice the potatoes in half lengthwise. Return the skillet to the oven rack and heat 20 minutes; swirl in 2 Tbsp of the butter; add the potatoes cut-side down and roast 18-25 minutes until browned and crisp. Spoon toum onto a plate and arrange the potatoes on top (reserve extra toum). Warm the remaining 2 Tbsp butter and the honey in the skillet over medium heat; drizzle over the potatoes; sprinkle with nigella seeds (if using) and flaky sea salt.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard produce handling; toum is raw garlic and oil - hold refrigerated and use within a few days.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to this recipe.
- **Quality indicators**: Toum thick, smooth, and emulsified (not broken/oily), sweet potatoes tender inside with a well-charred, crisp cut side.
- **Holding/storage**: Toum keeps refrigerated, airtight, several days (extra can be saved for other uses). Roasted sweet potatoes are best served immediately.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 4 servings is below the program's 16-30 target; scale by making toum and roasting potatoes in batches, since the emulsification and pan-charring steps do not scale cleanly by simply multiplying quantities in one blender/pan.
- **Skill focus**: Building a stable oil-garlic emulsion (toum) by slow, gradual streaming (the same principle as mayonnaise or aioli), two-stage cooking of a vegetable (steam-roast covered, then uncovered high-heat char).

- **Source and revision date**: Program conference packet, Chef Greg Forte, Utah Valley State College Culinary Arts Institute, Aug 2-4, 2006. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source - a base pastry component, not portioned as a standalone dish.
- **Equipment**: Heavy saucepan, stand mixer with paddle attachment, piping bag, oven.
- **Ingredients**: 1 lb water or milk, 8 oz unsalted butter or shortening, 1 tsp salt, 12 oz bread flour, 1.25 lb eggs.
- **Procedure**: Bring the liquid, butter, and salt to a full rolling boil in a heavy saucepan. Add the flour all at once over moderate heat, stirring quickly until the dough forms a ball and pulls from the sides of the pan; cook 1 additional minute. Transfer to a mixer and paddle until cooled to about 140°F (still warm, not hot). Add the eggs one at a time, waiting until each is fully absorbed before adding more. Pipe immediately, or hold up to an hour in a draft-free area. Bake at 425°F for 5 minutes, then lower to 375°F and bake until the shell is a medium golden color throughout. Do not open the oven early - premature removal collapses and flattens the shells. Cool completely before filling.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling; confirm shells are baked through (not underbaked/doughy in the center) before filling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg, dairy (if using butter).
- **Quality indicators**: Fully puffed, hollow shells with a dry, crisp, evenly golden exterior; no collapse.
- **Holding/storage**: Unfilled shells can be stored airtight; fill just before service to keep them crisp.
- **Scaling notes**: Component quantity; scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Classic pate a choux technique (panade formation, egg incorporation by feel, temperature-staged baking), avoiding premature oven-opening that collapses the shells.

### Acquacotta Senese

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - Tuscan peasant soup technique (ProStart Conference, Aug. 2006 packet).
- **Source and revision date**: Program conference packet, Chef Greg Forte, Utah Valley State College Culinary Arts Institute, Aug 2-4, 2006. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: 18 portions, scaled 3x from the source's 6-portion yield.
- **Equipment**: Saucepan.
- **Ingredients**: 18 slices Tuscan-style bread, 51 oz porcini mushrooms (cleaned and sliced), 15 oz tomato (concasse), 9 eggs, 6 cloves garlic, 3 mint leaves, 3 red onions (diced), 12 Tbsp parmesan (to taste), extra-virgin olive oil to taste, salt and pepper to taste, 4.5 qt broth (beef, chicken, or vegetable, may be mixed).

- **Scaling notes**: Scaled 3x from the source's 6-portion yield to reach the program's target range.
- **Skill focus**: Egg-enriching a broth by mixing vigorously while pouring (an egg-drop-style technique), a bread-in-the-bowl peasant-soup format.

### Ricetta Base per la Pasta Fresca

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - foundational fresh egg pasta dough component (ProStart Conference, Aug. 2006 packet), a second fresh-pasta formula from the "Additional Assorted Recipes" section.
- **Source and revision date**: Program conference packet, Chef Greg Forte, Utah Valley State College Culinary Arts Institute, Aug 2-4, 2006. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source - a base dough component, not portioned as a standalone dish.
- **Equipment**: Wooden work surface, rolling pin, fork.
- **Ingredients**: 10.5 oz all-purpose flour, 3 eggs, pinch of salt, water as needed (lukewarm).
- **Procedure**: Sift the flour onto a wooden surface and make a well in the center. Add the salt and whole eggs to the center (a touch of extra-virgin olive oil may also be added). Using a fork, gradually mix the eggs and flour together in small circular movements, working from the center outward, until no flour remains or the eggs have absorbed as much flour as needed for the right consistency. The dough should be smooth and even; add a little lukewarm water if it's too stiff before the flour fully bonds with the eggs. Knead energetically at least 15 minutes, until elastic. Roll into a ball and rest, fully covered, for 30 minutes. Dust the work surface with a thin veil of flour, flatten the dough by hand, and roll as thin as possible with a rolling pin (smaller balls of dough are easier to roll thin). Let the sheets dry on a clean cloth without drying them out completely, then cut into desired shapes.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard egg handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten, egg.
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, elastic dough, thin and even rolled sheets.
- **Holding/storage**: Rest and roll the same day; cooked pasta should be used promptly.
- **Scaling notes**: Component quantity; scales linearly.
- **Skill focus**: Classic hand-mixed egg pasta technique (well method with a fork, energetic hand-kneading, thin hand-rolling) - a more traditional, fully-by-hand counterpart to the machine-rolled Lasagna Pasta Dough above.

Add the basil in three portions, incorporating each before adding the next. Add the pine nuts in two batches. Once the cheese is finely chopped, incorporate it in two batches. Transfer to a bowl and add oil to the desired consistency; taste (it may not need additional salt). Bring a pot of salted water to a boil. Simmer the cream in a sauté pan over medium-high heat until reduced by about a third; time the pasta so both finish together (set the cream aside off heat if the pasta lags behind). When the pasta is 1-2 minutes from done, return the cream to medium heat, add the pesto, and stir - do not let it rapid-boil, or the sauce will split and turn greasy. Reserve 1 cup of pasta water before draining; add the pasta to the sauce, fold together, and loosen with pasta water if too thick. Season, plate, and top with cheese, a drizzle of oil, and basil chiffonade; serve immediately.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, tree nuts (pine nuts), gluten (pasta).
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, emulsified cream sauce clinging to pasta (not split/greasy), vibrant green pesto flavor, pasta al dente.
- **Holding/storage**: Best served immediately - the cream sauce can split on holding/reheating. Extra pesto keeps fresh a few days refrigerated under a layer of oil, or can be frozen (though freezing loses some flavor and color).
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 4 servings is below the program's 16-30 target; scale by making pesto and cream sauce in batches rather than multiplying in one pan, since the cream reduction and emulsification steps do not scale cleanly.
- **Skill focus**: Building a fresh pesto by hand in stages (herb, then nuts, then cheese) rather than dumping everything into a processor at once, reducing and emulsifying a cream sauce without splitting, using reserved pasta starch to adjust consistency.

### Spaghetti Aglio e Olio

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 1/2, International unit - foundational Italian oil-based pasta sauce technique.
- **Source and revision date**: rouxbe.com, "Spaghetti Aglio E Olio." Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Serves 2-4 (source yield); below the program's 16-30 target but left as written, consistent with this bank's treatment of other single-recipe technique finds. Prep 25 min, cook 25 min.
- **Equipment**: Large pot, large non-stick skillet.
- **Ingredients**: 1-2 cloves garlic, 4 Tbsp extra-virgin olive oil (divided), 1/4 tsp red chili flakes, fresh Italian basil (to taste), 200 g pasta, sea salt (to taste, plus 1 tsp per liter/quart of pasta water), 1/2 cup reserved pasta cooking water.
- **Procedure**: Bring a large pot of water to a boil. While it heats, mince the garlic and measure the chili flakes. Place a large non-stick skillet on the stove and add half the oil, followed by the garlic and chili flakes - do not turn the heat on yet; let it sit while the pasta water comes to a boil. Once boiling, add the salt and the pasta, stirring so it doesn't stick together. While the pasta cooks, turn the skillet heat to medium and let the garlic and chilies cook slowly, spreading the garlic out slightly for even cooking but not so much it burns; once it starts to sizzle and soften, remove from the heat until the pasta finishes. Test the pasta just before it's al dente (it will continue cooking in the skillet); reserve 1/2 cup of the cooking water, then drain the pasta and add it directly to the pan. Return the heat to medium, add the reserved cooking water, and toss to combine. Add a good pinch of salt and tear the basil leaves over the top; continue tossing and cooking a couple of minutes, until little to no water remains in the pan. Turn off the heat, add the remaining olive oil, and gently toss to combine. Serve immediately on warmed plates.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard handling.
- **Allergens**: Gluten (pasta).
- **Quality indicators**: Glossy, emulsified sauce clinging to the pasta (from pasta water and oil, no separate cream or heavy sauce), garlic golden and fragrant, not burnt/bitter.
- **Holding/storage**: Best served immediately; does not hold or reheat well.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 2-4 servings is below the program's 16-30 target; scale by cooking pasta and the garlic-oil base in batches rather than one oversized skillet, since the starch-emulsion technique doesn't scale cleanly in one pan.
- **Skill focus**: Building a starch-and-oil emulsified pasta sauce with no cream or butter, timing pasta and sauce components so both finish together, gently infusing oil with garlic and chili without burning either. **Source note**: pull the pasta a bit early rather than late, since it continues cooking in the skillet.

### Mofonguitos de Yuca (Cassava Fritters Stuffed with Braised Chicken)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Puerto Rican composed appetizer/plating exercise; a three-component dish (fried cassava fritter, braised "criolla"-style chicken filling, roasted pepper salad) deferred from the Poultry pass since its identity is built around the cassava component and Caribbean technique rather than the chicken alone.
- **Source and revision date**: Program recipe card (source table's ingredient/procedure columns were OCR-misaligned in extraction; re-read carefully and reconstructed component-by-component below). Standardized 2026-07. Scaled 5x from the source's 4-portion yield to reach the program's target range.
- **Yield and portion size**: 20 portions (fritters), scaled 5x from the source's 4-portion yield.
- **Equipment**: Pot for boiling cassava, pedestal mixer or food processor (robot coupe), sauté pan, sauce pot, mixing bowls, sheet pan, deep fryer or frying pan, oven (for roasting peppers), aluminum foil.
- **Ingredients**:
  - *Cassava fritter*: 5 lb cassava, 5 Tbsp butter, 7.5 Tbsp chicken stock, 5 Tbsp garlic (minced), 2.5 tsp cumin, 5 tsp salt, 2.5 tsp black pepper, tapioca flour (as needed, for dredging), oil (as needed, for frying and for coating hands during shaping).
  - *Braised chicken filling ("Pollo a la Criolla")*: 7-8 chicken thighs (excess fat removed, small dice), 20 fl oz (2.5 cups) tomato sauce, 5 tsp achiote (annatto) paste, 5 oz garlic (minced), 7.5 oz onion (small dice), 7.5 oz bell peppers (small dice), 5 tsp adobo seasoning, 10 bay leaves, 2.5 tsp cumin, 5 Tbsp sofrito (oil, onion, garlic, oregano, and cilantro - **needs confirmation**, the source lists "cilantro and cilantro paste" as if two separate items, which may be a transcription of culantro/recao, a common Caribbean herb distinct from cilantro; preserved as written rather than guessed), salt and black pepper to taste, 7.5 Tbsp tomato paste, 5 Tbsp oil, 5 Tbsp butter, chicken stock (**needs confirmation** - the procedure calls for adding stock to braise, but no stock quantity is given anywhere in this component's ingredient list in the source).
  - *Roasted pepper salad*: 10 yellow bell peppers, 2-3 red onions, 5 tsp olive oil, 15 fl oz (about 1.75-2 cups) rice vinegar, 5 tsp chopped cilantro.
- **Procedure**:
  1. *Braised chicken filling (make first)*: Season the diced chicken thighs with half the salt, pepper, adobo, and cumin. Heat a sauté pan with the oil and butter; sweat the onion, garlic, and bell pepper 2 minutes. Add the achiote paste and tomato paste; cook 1 minute more. Add the chicken and cook 3 minutes. Add the stock and tomato sauce; braise about 20 minutes, until the sauce reduces to a thick consistency. Adjust seasoning, cool, and reserve for stuffing the fritters.
  2. *Roasted pepper salad*: Coat the peppers in oil and wrap tightly in foil. Roast until tender, about 34-40 minutes (**needs confirmation** - source does not state oven temperature). Once the peppers are cool enough to handle but still warm, remove the skins and cut into julienne. Combine with the pickled onion and chopped cilantro; season to taste and reserve for service.
  3. *Cassava fritter and assembly*: Peel the cassava, cut into large chunks, and remove the core. Boil in salted water about 18 minutes, until tender. Drain and mash (pedestal mixer or food processor). Add the butter, chicken stock, minced garlic, salt, pepper, and cumin; mix until fully homogenized. Oil your palms, portion out about 3/4 oz of the cassava mash, and flatten it in your palm. Place about 1 tsp of the braised chicken filling in the center and close the cassava around it to form a sealed ball. Lightly dredge each ball in tapioca flour, shaking off excess. Fry until golden brown. Serve over a bed of the roasted pepper salad, garnished with reserved braising sauce and microgreens.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard raw-poultry handling for the chicken component; confirm chicken reaches a safe internal temperature during the braise; cool the chicken filling fully before stuffing (a hot filling can partially cook the cassava shell from the inside and make sealing difficult); standard hot-oil frying safety.
- **Allergens**: None inherent to the fritter or filling as written; confirm sofrito/adobo brands used for hidden allergens.
- **Quality indicators**: Golden, crisp fritter exterior with a fully sealed seam (no filling leaking during frying), smooth and well-seasoned cassava mash, tender braised chicken filling reduced to a thick, clingy consistency, bright and tangy pickled pepper salad as a contrasting bed.
- **Holding/storage**: Fritters are best fried to order; the braised chicken filling and roasted pepper salad can each be made ahead and refrigerated a few days, then the fritters assembled and fried fresh for service.
- **Scaling notes**: Scaled 5x from the source's 4-portion yield to reach the program's 16-30 portion target; the fritter-forming and frying steps don't scale by simply multiplying in one fryer - plan to fry in batches.
- **Skill focus**: Building a starchy fritter shell around a separately-braised filling and sealing it without leaks, staged component production (filling and pickled salad made ahead, fritters assembled and fried to order), Caribbean sofrito/adobo/annatto flavor base.

### Chicken Fajitas (Cast Iron, Slow-Cooked Onion Method)

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Tex-Mex slow-cooked onion and cast-iron sear technique. Kept separate from "Chicken Fajitas (Quick Sauté Method)" above per this project's dedup rule for a genuinely different technique/result (a roughly 1-hour, slowly cooked cast-iron onion base rather than a quick sauté).
- **Source and revision date**: momscravings.com, "Cast Iron Chicken Fajitas." Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: Not specified in source as a portion count. **Needs confirmation** for a target portion count; scales linearly by chicken/vegetable weight.
- **Equipment**: Cast-iron skillet.
- **Ingredients**: 1 Tbsp fajita seasoning (McCormick's preferred, per source), 2 Tbsp water, 1.5 lb chicken breast, 1/4 cup olive oil, 1 Tbsp lime juice, 2 Tbsp peanut oil, 1 tsp salt, 2 small/medium yellow onions, 2 Tbsp salted butter, 3 bell peppers, 2 cloves garlic (minced), 1 Tbsp tomato paste ("don't skip this," per source). ### Enchiladas Con Carne

- **Course/use case**: Culinary 2, International unit - Tex-Mex chili con carne and enchilada assembly technique.
- **Source and revision date**: NYT Cooking, "Enchiladas Con Carne," by Sam Sifton. Standardized 2026-07.
- **Yield and portion size**: 4-6 servings (source yield); below the program's 16-30 target but left as written, consistent with this bank's treatment of other single-recipe technique finds. Total time about 1.5 hours (practiced cooks can reach 60 minutes, per source).
- **Equipment**: Dry sauté pan, large sauté pan, 9x13" baking pan, oven.
- **Ingredients**: **Chili con carne**: 1/2 cup all-purpose flour, 2 Tbsp neutral oil, 1 lb ground chuck (ideally 20% fat), salt and pepper, 1 medium white onion (chopped), 2 cloves garlic (minced), 1 jalapeño (seeded for less heat, chopped), 1 cup chopped or canned crushed tomatoes, 3 Tbsp chile powder, 1/2 tsp cumin, 1/2 tsp dried oregano (ideally Mexican), 2 cups chicken stock. **Enchiladas**: 1/2 cup neutral oil, 12 yellow corn tortillas, 3 cups shredded Cheddar (or 1.5 cups Cheddar plus 1.5 cups American/Velveeta), 1 medium white onion (chopped, optional garnish).
- **Procedure**: Toast the flour in a dry sauté pan over medium-high heat until golden and nutty; cool on a plate. Wipe the pan, add 2 Tbsp oil, and brown the ground beef over high heat 12-15 minutes, breaking it up and seasoning; remove with a slotted spoon, reserving the drippings. Add the onion, garlic, and jalapeño to the pan; cook, scraping up the fond, 10-12 minutes until soft. Add the tomatoes and cook until the liquid evaporates; add the chile powder, cumin, and oregano. After a minute, once fragrant, return the meat and the toasted flour to the pan, stirring well. Lower to medium-high and stir in the stock 1/2 cup at a time until thickened and simmering; lower the heat and cook 45 minutes to 1 hour until tender, adding stock or water as needed (use now or refrigerate a few days). Heat the oven to 425°F. Heat 1/2 cup oil over medium-high heat in a sauté pan until shimmering; dip each tortilla about 10 seconds per side until soft and lightly browned; drain on a rack. Ladle about 1/2 cup of chili in the bottom of the baking pan; roll cheese and a tablespoon of chili into each tortilla, place seam-down, nestled together. Ladle the remaining chili over the top and sprinkle with the remaining cheese. Bake 10-15 minutes until bubbling and melted; sprinkle with chopped onion if using; serve immediately.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard ground beef handling; confirm beef reaches a safe internal temperature during browning.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, gluten (flour thickener), corn (tortillas).
- **Quality indicators**: Rich, thickened chili with toasted-flour body (not thin or raw-tasting), well-melted cheese, tortillas softened and pliable (not torn) from the oil-dip step.
- **Holding/storage**: Chili con carne holds and reheats well refrigerated a few days; assembled/baked enchiladas are best served fresh from the oven.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 4-6 servings is below the program's 16-30 target; scale by multiplying chili con carne quantities and increasing tortilla/baking-pan count, keeping the tortilla-dipping and baking steps in batches.
- **Skill focus**: Toasting raw flour as a thickener before adding it to a sauce, building a Tex-Mex chili base in stages (brown meat, build aromatics/fond, season, thicken with stock), softening corn tortillas in oil for rolled enchiladas without tearing. **Source notes**: mixing American cheese (Velveeta) with Cheddar gives the classic Tex-Mex melt (per Robb Walsh, Tex-Mex scholar, El Real Tex-Mex, Houston); serve with a tomatillo-pineapple salsa for acid balance.

- **Yield and portion size**: About 8-10 servings as an appetizer/spread (source yield); within the program's target range as a condiment-style yield. Prep/total time about 125 minutes (mostly chilling).
- **Equipment**: Food processor, fine strainer (for draining fromage blanc).
- **Ingredients**: 1 (8 oz) carton fromage blanc or chèvre, 1/4 cup white wine vinegar, 2 Tbsp crème fraîche or sour cream, 1.5 Tbsp light olive oil, 2 small shallots (finely chopped), 1 small garlic clove (finely chopped), 1 tsp dried chives (or 1 Tbsp fresh, chopped), 1 tsp dried chervil (or 1 Tbsp fresh, chopped), 3/4 tsp sea salt, 1/8 tsp black pepper, 1 baguette (sliced and toasted into croûtes, for serving).
- **Procedure**: Drain the fromage blanc. Process it with the vinegar, crème fraîche, and olive oil in a food processor until smooth, medium consistency. Add the shallots, chives, chervil, salt, and pepper; pulse just until incorporated. Refrigerate 2-3 hours before serving on toasted baguette croûtes.
- **Food safety controls**: Standard dairy handling.
- **Allergens**: Dairy, gluten (baguette croûtes).
- **Quality indicators**: Smooth, tangy, herb-flecked spread (not grainy or over-processed), balanced acidity from the vinegar.
- **Holding/storage**: Refrigerate airtight; best within a few days.
- **Scaling notes**: Source yield of 8-10 servings is within the program's target range as a condiment/appetizer yield; scales linearly by fromage blanc quantity.
- **Skill focus**: Building a fresh-cheese spread with a controlled acid and herb balance, chill-resting a spread to let flavors meld before service.
